वेदान्त
Chapter I The Origins वेदान्त · 1500–500 BCE

The Origins of Vedanta

From the Vedas to the Upanishads — the philosophical climax of ancient India

Vedanta is the philosophical culmination of the Vedas, the oldest scriptures of India, dating back to c. 1500 BCE. The word "Vedanta" literally means "the end of the Vedas" — referring both to the Upanishads (the final sections of the Vedic corpus) and to the ultimate purpose of Vedic knowledge.

The Vedic tradition developed over several centuries. The earliest layer, the Saṃhitās, consists of hymns, prayers, and rituals. The Brāhmaṇas (c. 1000–800 BCE) contain ritual instructions and priestly commentary. The Āraṇyakas ("forest texts") mark a transition toward interiority and meditation. Finally, the Upanishads (c. 800–200 BCE) shift the focus from ritual to knowledge — from karma (action) to jñāna (wisdom).

"From the unreal lead me to the real. From darkness lead me to light. From death lead me to immortality."

— Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 1.3.28

The Upanishads ask the deepest questions: What is the nature of reality? Who am I? What is the relationship between the individual self and the ultimate ground of existence? Their answers form the foundation of all later Vedantic philosophy.

उपनिषद्
Chapter II The Source उपनिषद् · c. 800–200 BCE

The Upanishads

The foundational texts of non-duality — the seeds of Vedanta

The Upanishads are the philosophical core of the Vedas. There are over 200 Upanishads, but the ten principal ones (the Mukhya Upanishads) form the foundation of Vedanta. They contain the seed of all later Advaita philosophy.

The central teaching of the Upanishads is the identity of Ātman (the individual self) with Brahman (the absolute reality). This identity is expressed in the four Mahāvākyas — the great sayings that later teachers would use as the core of their meditation.

The Four Great Sayings · चत्वारि महावाक्यानि

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — the shortest of the principal Upanishads at only twelve verses — became the foundation for Gaudapada's Kārikā and through it, the entire Advaita tradition. Its teaching on the four states of consciousness (jāgrat, svapna, suṣupti, turīya) provides a complete map of human experience and points toward the fourth state, the silent witness, as the ultimate reality.

भगवद्गीता
Chapter III The Synthesis भगवद्गीता · c. 5th–2nd BCE

The Bhagavad Gita

The practical integration of Upanishadic wisdom

The Bhagavad Gita is often called the "essence of the Upanishads." It presents the core teachings of Vedanta not as abstract philosophy but as a practical dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra. It is a Smṛti text (tradition, not direct revelation) but holds the status of an Upanishad in practice.

The Gita resolves the apparent conflict between ascetic renunciation and worldly duty by integrating the three main paths of Yoga into a harmonious whole:

Karma Yoga
कर्म योग
The path of selfless action — acting without attachment to the fruits of action. Doing what is right because it is right, not for personal gain.
"You have the right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not entitled to the fruits of your actions."Gita 2.47
Bhakti Yoga
भक्ति योग
The path of devotion — surrendering all actions and their results to the Divine. Love as the highest form of knowledge.
"Offer your mind to Me, offer your heart to Me, offer your actions to Me. By doing so, you will surely come to Me."Gita 18.65
Jnana Yoga
ज्ञान योग
The path of knowledge — the direct investigation into the nature of the Self, culminating in the recognition of one's identity with Brahman.
"The wise man sees the same Self in the learned sage, the cow, the elephant, and the dog."Gita 5.18

⚡ The Gita's Role in Vedanta

Why does the Gita matter so much? Because it takes the profound, abstract truths of the Upanishads — "That Thou Art" — and shows how they can be lived in everyday life. It bridges the gap between the monastery and the battlefield, teaching that liberation is not achieved by escaping the world but by engaging with it without attachment. The Gita is the most accessible and practical expression of Vedantic philosophy, making it one of the most widely read spiritual texts in the world.

ब्रह्मसूत्र
Chapter IV The Logic ब्रह्मसूत्र · c. 200 BCE – 200 CE

The Brahma Sutras

The systematic philosophy of Vedanta

The Brahma Sutras (also known as the Vedanta Sutras) are a collection of 555 aphorisms composed by Bādarāyaṇa. They systematically organize the teachings of the Upanishads into a coherent philosophical system.

Each sutra is a brief, precise statement that encapsulates a key Vedantic principle. They are arranged in four chapters (Adhyāyas), each divided into four sections (Pādas):

  • Chapter I: Samanvaya — The harmony of all Upanishadic teachings. Establishes that Brahman is the ultimate reality.
  • Chapter II: Avirodha — Non-contradiction. Refutes opposing philosophical views (Sāṃkhya, Yoga, etc.) and reconciles apparent contradictions in the Upanishads.
  • Chapter III: Sādhana — The means of realization. Discusses the path to liberation, including the role of meditation and the qualifications required for the seeker.
  • Chapter IV: Phala — The fruit. Describes the nature of liberation (mokṣa) and the state of the liberated soul.

"That which is the subtle essence — in that all that exists has its Self. That is the Truth. That is the Self. That Thou Art."

— Chāndogya Upaniṣad 6.8.7, quoted in Brahma Sūtra 1.1.4

The Brahma Sutras are the logical thread that strings the pearls of Upanishadic wisdom into a coherent philosophical system. They do not introduce new teachings but organize and defend the existing ones, providing a framework for all later Vedantic commentary.

Chapter V The Triple Canon प्रस्थानत्रयी

The Prasthanatrayi

The three foundational texts of Vedanta

The Prasthanatrayi is the triple canon of Vedanta. It consists of the three texts that form the basis of all classical Vedantic philosophy. They complement each other perfectly:

Together, these three texts form the complete foundation of Vedanta. The Upanishads provide the revelation (Śruti) — the direct perception of truth. The Bhagavad Gita provides the synthesis (Smṛti) — the practical application. The Brahma Sutras provide the logic (Nyāya) — the systematic defense and organization of the teachings.

📜 The Unity of the Three

No single text can stand alone. The Upanishads are too vast and seemingly contradictory; the Gita is too practical to serve as a complete philosophical system; the Brahma Sutras are too abstract without the context of the Upanishads and the Gita. Together, they form a complete, integrated system of spiritual knowledge — the Prasthanatrayi.

शब्दकोश
Chapter VI Core Vocabulary शब्दकोश

Key Concepts

The essential terms of Vedanta — a concise glossary

The following terms form the core vocabulary of Vedanta. They appear repeatedly in the Upanishads, the Gita, and the Brahma Sutras, and are essential to understanding the non-dual philosophy.

Term Translation Meaning in Vedanta
Ātman Self The eternal, unchanging consciousness that is your true nature. Beyond mind, body, and all attributes. Identical with Brahman.
Brahman Absolute The ultimate reality, the ground of existence. Non-dual, infinite, eternal. In Advaita, Ātman and Brahman are one.
Māyā Illusion The power of appearances. The mechanism by which the one reality appears as the many. Not a trick — the inherent creative power of Brahman.
Avidyā Ignorance The fundamental misperception that separates the self from reality. The root cause of suffering and bondage.
Mokṣa Liberation Freedom from the cycle of birth and death. The direct recognition of one's identity with Brahman. The ultimate goal of Vedanta.
Jñāna Knowledge Not intellectual knowledge, but direct, intuitive realization. The recognition of the Self as Brahman. The path of Jñāna Yoga.
Bhakti Devotion Love and surrender to the Divine. In the Gita, the highest form of devotion is the recognition that all is Brahman.
Karma Action Action and its consequences. The law of cause and effect. In Vedanta, action performed without attachment leads to liberation.
Turīya The Fourth Not a state but the unchanging awareness that witnesses waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. The silent background of all experience.
Viveka Discrimination The capacity to distinguish the real from the apparent, the permanent from the transient. The essential faculty on the path.

The Upanishads are the source, the Gita is the synthesis, and the Brahma Sutras are the logical thread. — The Prasthanatrayi

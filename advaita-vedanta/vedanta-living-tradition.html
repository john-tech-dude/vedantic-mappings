उपनिषद्
Chapter I The Source उपनिषद्

The Upanishadic Source

The foundational texts of non-duality · c. 800 BCE

The Vedas are the oldest scriptures of India, dating back to c. 1500 BCE. The Upanishads (c. 800–200 BCE) are their philosophical culmination — the Vedānta (end of the Vedas). The twelve principal Upanishads contain the seed of all later Advaita Vedanta.

The central teaching is the identity of Ātman (the individual self) with Brahman (the absolute reality). This identity is expressed in the four Mahāvākyas — the great sayings that later teachers would use as the core of their meditation.

"That which is the subtle essence — in that all that exists has its Self. That is the Truth. That is the Self. That Thou Art."

— Chāndogya Upaniṣad 6.8.7 (Tat Tvam Asi)
The Four Great Sayings · चत्वारि महावाक्यानि
Prajñānaṁ Brahma
Consciousness is Brahman
Aitareya · Rig Veda
Ahaṁ Brahmāsmi
I am Brahman
Bṛhadāraṇyaka · Yajur Veda
Tat Tvam Asi
That thou art
Chāndogya · Sāma Veda
Ayam Ātmā Brahma
This self is Brahman
Māṇḍūkya · Atharva Veda

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — the shortest (only twelve verses) — became the foundation for Gaudapada's Kārikā and through it, the entire Advaita tradition. Its teaching on the four states of consciousness (jāgrat, svapna, suṣupti, turīya) is the blueprint for all later Vedantic cartographies.

गौडपाद
Chapter II The Architect गौडपाद

Gaudapada — The Architect of Ajāta

c. 6th–7th century CE · Author of the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā

Gaudapada is the first historical teacher to write a systematic exposition of Advaita in Sanskrit. His Māṇḍūkya Kārikā is a commentary on the twelve verses of the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad, but it is also an independent philosophical treatise establishing the doctrine of ajāta vāda — non-origination.

His radical teaching: "There is no birth, no destruction, no one in bondage, no seeker of liberation, and no one liberated. This is the highest truth." (Karika 2.32). The entire phenomenal universe is a superimposition, like a rope mistaken for a snake, or a dream seen by a sleeping mind. He uses the analogy of a fire-brand swung in a circle: it appears to produce a circle of fire, but no circle is ever actually created.

"That which is non-existent at the beginning and in the end is also non-existent in the middle. It is like a mirage in the desert."

— Mandukya Karika 4.31

Gaudapada's disciple Govinda Bhagavatpada transmitted the teaching to Adi Shankara, making Gaudapada the parama-guru of the entire classical Advaita tradition.

Key Text
Māṇḍūkya Kārikā
माण्डूक्य कारिका
The first systematic exposition of Advaita in Sanskrit. Establishes ajāta vāda — the doctrine that nothing is ever born.
"No jiva is ever born. No cause exists for anything that appears. The highest truth is that nothing is ever born."Karika 3.15
Core Contribution
Ajāta Vāda
अजात वाद
The uncompromising teaching that the world never truly arises. It is an illusion like a dream. Only Brahman is real.
"There is no dissolution, no birth, none in bondage, none aspiring for freedom, none liberated. This is the ultimate truth."Karika 2.32
शङ्कर
Chapter III The Systematizer शङ्कर

Adi Shankara — The Great Expositor

c. 788–820 CE · Author of the Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya

Adi Shankara systematised Advaita Vedanta into a coherent philosophical framework. He wrote commentaries on the ten principal Upanishads, the Bhagavad Gītā, and the Brahma Sūtras, establishing Advaita as India's dominant philosophical school.

His key contributions: the six pramāṇas (means of knowledge), the distinction between Nirguṇa and Saguṇa Brahman, the doctrine of vivarta (apparent modification), and the Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya (fourfold qualifications for liberation).

"Brahman alone is real; the world is an illusion; the individual soul is none other than Brahman."

— Brahma Jñānāvalīmālā 20

He established four monasteries (Āmnāya Mathas) in the four corners of India, preserving the teaching for over twelve centuries. His lineage continues unbroken to the present day.

Key Text
Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya
ब्रह्म सूत्र भाष्य
His magnum opus — a commentary on the Brahma Sūtras of Bādarāyaṇa. Establishes Advaita through rigorous dialectic.
"The scripture is the only means for knowing Brahman, because Brahman is beyond all sense perception and reasoning."Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya 1.1.2
Core Contribution
The Path of Jñāna Yoga
ज्ञान योग
The three stages: śravaṇa (hearing), manana (reflection), and nididhyāsana (deep meditation). The direct path to liberation.
"The mind becomes purified by the practice of these virtues, and then the truth of the Upaniṣads shines forth like a lamp in a windless place."Vivekachūḍāmaṇi 18
रमण
Chapter IV The Living Master रमण

Ramana Maharshi — The Living Recognition

1879–1950 · The Sage of Arunachala

Ramana Maharshi realised the Self spontaneously at the age of sixteen, without any formal teaching or spiritual practice. His method, Atma Vicāra (Self-enquiry), is the direct investigation into the nature of the "I"-thought: "Who am I?"

He taught for 54 years from Tiruvannamalai, offering the simplest and most direct practice: trace every thought back to its source, the "I"-thought, and that source will dissolve when looked at directly. His primary teaching: "The Guru is none other than the Self."

"Of all the thoughts that rise in the mind, the thought 'I' is the first thought. Enquire into the nature of the 'I'. This is Self-enquiry."

— Who Am I? (Nan Yar, 1902)

Ramana's teaching is the experiential enactment of the Mahāvākyas. Where the Upanishads declare identity in language, Ramana points to immediate recognition. His influence spread globally through his disciples, including Papaji (H.W.L. Poonja), who went on to teach Mooji and many others.

Key Text
Who Am I? (Nan Yar)
नान् यार्
The foundational text of Ramana's teaching. A direct inquiry into the nature of the "I"-thought. The shortest and most powerful of his works.
"By the inquiry 'Who am I?' the mind will merge into the Self. If the mind is not destroyed, Self-realisation is not possible."Who Am I?
Core Contribution
Self-Enquiry (Vicāra)
आत्मविचार
The direct method of tracing the "I"-thought back to its source. Not a mantra to repeat — the actual, living recognition of consciousness itself.
"The seeker is the sought. The search is for what was never lost. When this is seen, the search ends."The Essential Teaching
निसर्गदत्त
Chapter V The Radical Pointer निसर्गदत्त

Nisargadatta — The Radical Pointer

1897–1981 · The Guru of Mumbai

Nisargadatta Maharaj was an ordinary householder and beedi-shop owner until the age of 36. Following his guru's single instruction — "abide in the sense of 'I Am'" — he realised the Self within three years. He taught from his humble Khetwadi flat in Mumbai for forty years, attracting seekers from around the world.

His method, Nisarga Yoga, is the practice of abiding in the bare "I Am" — the sense of existing prior to thought. He identified this "I Am" with Ishvara, the creative principle. His radical teaching: even the "I Am" is an appearance. What is prior to it — Parabrahman — is the ultimate reality.

"The knowledge 'I am' is the link between the Absolute and the relative. The Absolute is prior to consciousness."

— I Am That

Nisargadatta's teaching is often compared with Ramana's. Both point to the same reality, but their methods differ: Ramana traces the "I"-thought to its source; Nisargadatta abides in the "I Am" until it reveals what is prior to itself.

Key Text
I Am That
अहम् तत्
The collected conversations of Nisargadatta, translated by Maurice Frydman. Never out of print since 1973. The most widely-read Advaita text of the modern era.
"The sense 'I am' is the basis of all experience. Without it, there is no experience. Find that sense, hold on to it, and you will know yourself."I Am That, Chapter 7
Core Contribution
The Distinction
चिदाकाश · परमाकाश
The critical distinction between Consciousness (Chidākāśa) and the Absolute (Paramākāśa). Consciousness is still an appearance; the Absolute is prior to consciousness.
"The entire universe exists only in consciousness (Chidākāśa), while the Jñānī has his abode in the Absolute (Paramākāśa)."Nisargadatta Maharaj

Chapter VII Comparative Summary तुलना · संवाद

Many Voices, One Truth

A comparative overview of the four primary teachers and the broader tradition

Four teachers, four methods, one truth. Each has contributed a unique facet to the diamond of Advaita. The table below summarises their key teachings, methods, and contributions.

Teacher Era Key Text Core Method Unique Contribution
Gaudapada 6th–7th c. Māṇḍūkya Kārikā Philosophical analysis + direct pointing Ajāta Vāda — the doctrine of non-origination
Adi Shankara 8th c. Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya Systematic philosophy + Jñāna Yoga Systematization — established Advaita as a complete philosophical system
Ramana Maharshi 1879–1950 Who Am I? (Nan Yar) Self-Enquiry (Vicāra) Direct pointing — the living practice of the Mahāvākyas
Nisargadatta 1897–1981 I Am That Nisarga Yoga — abiding in "I Am" Consciousness vs. Absolute — even the "I Am" is an appearance

⚡ The Living Convergence

Despite their different methods and emphases, all four teachers — and indeed all the teachers listed in the lineage above — point to the same reality: the Self that is ever-present, beyond mind, beyond words, beyond all appearance. Gaudapada established the philosophical foundation; Shankara systematised it; Ramana made it a living practice; Nisargadatta pushed it to its ultimate conclusion. The lineage continues through the Śaṅkarācāryas, the modern teachers, and countless unnamed sages. Together, they form a golden thread that stretches from the Upanishads to the present — an unbroken transmission of the non-dual teaching.

The Golden Thread · सुवर्ण सूत्र

The golden thread of Advaita: from the Upanishads through Gaudapada, Shankara, Ramana, and Nisargadatta — an unbroken transmission of non-dual recognition.

Unified Glossary

All key Sanskrit terms from the Advaita Vedanta tradition — from the Upanishads through Gaudapada, Shankara, Ramana, Nisargadatta, and the broader lineage.

🔍

No matching terms found.

Upaniṣad उपनिषद्
Scripture

The philosophical and mystical texts at the end of the Vedas. The foundation of Vedanta. Twelve principal Upanishads form the core of Advaita.

Ātman आत्मन्
The Self

The eternal, unchanging consciousness that is your true nature. Beyond mind, body, and all attributes. Identical with Brahman.

Brahman ब्रह्मन्
The Absolute

The ultimate reality, the ground of existence. Non-dual, infinite, eternal. In Advaita, Ātman and Brahman are one — the Self is Brahman.

Mahāvākya महावाक्य
The Great Sayings

The four great sayings of Vedanta: Prajñānaṃ Brahma, Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi, Tat Tvam Asi, Ayam Ātmā Brahma. Each from a different Veda.

Ajāta अजात
Gaudapada

"Non-origination" — the central teaching of Gaudapada that nothing is ever born, created, or produced. The world is a mirage; only Brahman is real.

Alataśānti अलतशान्ति
Gaudapada

"Extinction of the Fire-brand" — the fourth chapter of the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā, using the analogy of a swinging fire-brand to show that the world of cause and effect is an illusion.

Māṇḍūkya Kārikā माण्डूक्य कारिका
Gaudapada

Gaudapada's commentary on the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad. The first systematic exposition of Advaita in Sanskrit and the foundation of ajāta vāda.

Pramāṇa प्रमाण
Shankara

A means of valid knowledge. Shankara systematised six: Pratyakṣa (perception), Anumāṇa (inference), Upamāṇa (comparison), Arthāpatti (postulation), Anupalabdhi (non-apprehension), Śabda (verbal testimony).

Vivarta विवर्त
Shankara

"Apparent modification" — the doctrine that the world is a superimposition on Brahman, like a rope mistaken for a snake. The world appears but is not ultimately real.

Nirguṇa / Saguṇa निर्गुण · सगुण
Shankara

Nirguṇa Brahman — Brahman without qualities, the formless absolute. Saguṇa Brahman — Brahman with qualities, the personal God (Īśvara) worshipped through devotion.

Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya साधन चतुष्टय
Shankara

The fourfold qualifications for Jñāna Yoga: Viveka (discrimination), Vairāgya (dispassion), Ṣaṭsampatti (six virtues), Mumukṣutva (longing for liberation).

Śravaṇa, Manana, Nididhyāsana श्रवण · मनन · निदिध्यासन
Shankara

The three stages of Jñāna Yoga: hearing the teaching (śravaṇa), reflecting on it (manana), and deep meditation (nididhyāsana).

Vicāra विचार
Ramana Maharshi

"Self-enquiry" — the method of turning attention inward to investigate the "I"-thought. The primary sadhana of Ramana's teaching.

Ahaṁkāra अहंकार
Ramana Maharshi

The "I"-thought — the first thought that arises and is the root of all others. Dissolved by Self-enquiry.

Mouna मौन
Ramana Maharshi

"Silence" — the highest teaching and the highest compassion. The direct transmission from Self to Self, beyond words.

Nisarga Yoga निसर्ग योग
Nisargadatta

Nisargadatta's method: abiding in the bare "I Am" without qualifiers. Effortless recognition of what is always already present.

Chidākāśa चिदाकाश
Nisargadatta

"Consciousness-Space" — the field of knowingness in which the entire universe exists. The "I Am-ness." Still an appearance, not the ultimate.

Paramākāśa परमाकाश
Nisargadatta

"Absolute Space" — the abode of the Jñānī, prior to consciousness. In the Absolute there is no consciousness of "I am."

Parabrahman परब्रह्म
Nisargadatta

That which is prior to consciousness, prior to "I Am." The ultimate reality without qualities, without being or non-being. What you are when all appearances subside.

Ishvara ईश्वर
Nisargadatta

God — identified by Nisargadatta with the "I Am" itself. The creative principle, the source of the universe. Still an appearance on the Absolute.

Jñānī ज्ञानी
The Knower

One who has realised the Self. The separate self-sense has dissolved completely. Appears to live normally but with no person behind it.

Māyā माया
Appearance

The power of illusion. Not a trick — simply the apparent appearance of the world on the Self. The mechanism by which the Absolute apparently forgets itself.

Turīya तुरीय
The Fourth

Not a state but the unchanging awareness that witnesses and contains waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. The silent background of all experience.

Jāgrat, Svapna, Suṣupti जाग्रत् · स्वप्न · सुषुप्ति
The Three States

The three ordinary states of consciousness: waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. All three come and go; Turīya, the fourth, is the witness.

Viveka विवेक
Discrimination

The capacity to distinguish the real from the apparent, the permanent from the transient, consciousness from its contents. The essential faculty on the path.

Bādarāyaṇa बादरायण
Pre-Shankara

The author of the Brahma Sūtras (c. 200 BCE – 200 CE). His 555 aphorisms systematise the Upanishadic teachings and form the foundation of Vedanta philosophy.

Vidyāraṇya विद्यारण्य
Post-Shankara

Author of the Pañcadaśī (c. 14th century CE), a comprehensive summary of Advaita in fifteen chapters. Also wrote on Jīvanmukti (liberation while living).

Madhusūdana Sarasvatī मधुसूदन सरस्वती
Post-Shankara

Author of the Advaita Siddhi (c. 16th century CE), a definitive defense of Advaita against rival schools. Also wrote on the unity of bhakti and jñāna.

Śaṅkarācārya शङ्कराचार्य
Institutional Lineage

The title of the heads of the four monasteries (mathas) established by Shankara. An unbroken lineage of teachers preserving the Advaita tradition.

Swami Vivekananda स्वामी विवेकानन्द
Modern Teacher

Brought Advaita Vedanta to the West at the World's Parliament of Religions in 1893. Founded the Ramakrishna Mission. Key work: Jnana Yoga.

Swami Chinmayananda स्वामी चिन्मयानन्द
Modern Teacher

Popularised Advaita for modern audiences. Founded the Chinmaya Mission. Author of commentaries on all major Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gītā.

Ajāta Vāda

The Doctrine of Non‑Origination

Nothing ever arises; the world is an illusion like a dream

Gaudapada’s most radical and enduring contribution to Advaita Vedanta is the teaching of ajāta vāda – the doctrine that nothing whatsoever is ever born, created, or originated. He declares: “There is no birth, no destruction, no one in bondage, no seeker of liberation, and no one liberated. This is the highest truth.” (Kārikā 2.32)[3].

The entire phenomenal universe – all names, forms, objects, and even the sense of a separate self – is a superimposition, like a rope mistaken for a snake, or a dream seen by a sleeping mind. Just as a dream has no independent reality apart from the dreamer, so the waking world has no reality apart from pure consciousness, Brahman. Gaudapada uses the analogy of a mirage: water that appears in the desert never actually exists. Similarly, the world appears but never truly arises.

This teaching is not nihilism. It is the recognition that the only reality is the formless, unchanging awareness in which all appearances come and go. The world is not false in the sense of being utterly non‑existent, but it is not real in the sense of being self‑subsistent. It is sad-asad-vilakṣaṇa – different from both real and unreal, like a dream.

“There is no dissolution, no origination, no one in bondage, no one striving for liberation, and no one liberated. This is the highest truth.”

— Māṇḍūkya Kārikā 2.32

Consciousness

The Four States of Consciousness

Waking, Dreaming, Deep Sleep, and the Fourth (Turiya)

Gaudapada’s commentary on the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (the shortest of the principal Upaniṣads, only twelve verses) is the first systematic exposition of the four states of consciousness. He shows that all three ordinary states – waking (jāgrat), dreaming (svapna), and deep sleep (suṣupti) – are appearances within the single, unchanging awareness that is Turiya (the “fourth”).

Turiya is not a fourth state in sequence; it is the substratum that underlies and witnesses the other three. It is pure consciousness without any object, the Self that never comes and goes. Gaudapada identifies this Turiya with Brahman, the absolute reality. The realisation of Turiya is the direct recognition that one is never born, never dies, and never touched by the events of the world.

Non‑Duality

No Duality, No Creation

The refutation of cause and effect

Gaudapada dedicates the fourth chapter of his Kārikā, the Alātaśānti (the “Extinction of the Fire‑brand”), to a thorough refutation of causality. He uses the famous analogy of a fire‑brand (alāta) swung in a circle: it appears to produce a circle of fire, but no circle is ever actually created. Similarly, consciousness appears to produce the world of cause and effect, but in truth nothing is ever produced.

He shows that the very concept of causation is a mental projection. If the world is an illusion, then there is no real creator, no real creation, and no real dissolution. The only reality is the non‑dual Self, which is free from all attributes and all relationships. This is the highest teaching of Advaita: advaita means not‑two; there is no second thing. Therefore, there can be no birth of a second thing.

“The fire‑brand when it is moving produces the illusion of a straight line, a circle, a curve, etc. Similarly, consciousness appears to generate the manifold universe through its own movement. But when the movement ceases, the illusion disappears. The Self alone remains.”

— Kārikā 4.47–48[5]

Lineage

The Ancient Preceptors

From Nārada to Gaudapada to Shankara – and the voice of Ashtavakra

Gaudapada stands in a lineage that traces back to the mythical sages Nārada and Sanatsujāta, but his direct teacher is said to be Śuka, the son of Vyāsa. He is the first historical teacher to have written a systematic exposition of Advaita in the Sanskrit language. His disciple Govinda Bhagavatpāda was the guru of Adi Shankara, making Gaudapada the parama-guru (grand‑teacher) of the entire classical Advaita tradition.

Even before Gaudapada, the radical non‑dual teaching appears in the Ashtavakra; a radical non-dual text">Aṣṭāvakra Gītā, a short but fierce dialogue between the sage Ashtavakra and King Janaka. Gaudapada’s ajāta vāda echoes the same uncompromising voice:

“You are one, pure, and spotless. You are the witness of all, free from the taint of desire. You are untouched by the world, like space. You are the Self that has never been born and never dies.”

— Aṣṭāvakra Gītā 2.6[6]

Ashtavakra’s teaching – “The world is unreal, you are pure awareness” – is the very same that Gaudapada systematised and that Shankara later defended. Though the Aṣṭāvakra Gītā is difficult to date, its content is a pure expression of ajāta vāda, and it stands as a parallel stream of the same living wisdom.

Ashtavakra to Janaka

“You are not the body. You have no caste, no creed, no family. You are pure consciousness. You are the witness of all things. You are without fear and without desire. You are the Self, eternal and free.”

Aṣṭāvakra Gītā 1.3–4[7]

Practice

Asparśa Yoga · The Yoga of Non‑Contact

Resting in consciousness without touching any object

Gaudapada’s method of practice is called asparśa yoga – the yoga of non‑contact. It is the direct abidance in Turiya, the witness consciousness, without engaging with the objects of the mind. This is not a technique of concentration, but a recognition that the mind itself is an appearance within awareness. By not contacting any thought, desire, or perception, the mind dissolves into its source.

He contrasts this with other yogas that involve effort, breath control, or meditation on forms. Asparśa yoga is the natural, effortless resting in one’s own true nature. It is identical to Ramana Maharshi’s Self‑enquiry when pursued to its source: the enquiry “Who am I?” eventually leads to the realisation that there is no “I” apart from pure awareness.

As Gaudapada says in Kārikā 3.46[8]: “The mind is not to be brought to a state of stillness by force. It is to be understood. When the mind is seen as nothing but consciousness, it becomes still on its own.”

“The wise man, having realised that the Self is the only reality, abides in the Self and does not return to the false perception of the world. He is like the fire‑brand that has ceased moving – only light remains.”

— Kārikā 4.58[9]

01 / The Central Principle

The Status of the "I Am"

In classical Advaita, the "I Am" is the gateway to the Absolute — but the Absolute itself is beyond the "I Am." Adyashanti's treatment of this core concept reveals a fundamental divergence.

Nisargadatta Maharaj
"The 'I am' is observed by the Absolute, it has no senses or eyes, witnessing just happens. Your true identity — The AbsoluteParabrahmanThe Absolute Reality in Advaita Vedanta, beyond all attributes, forms, and even the "I Am" consciousness itself. It is the ultimate substratum of all existence. — is prior to the 'I am.' When you go deep inside, nothing is all there is. There is no 'I am'. The 'I am' merges in the absolute."
Source: I Am That — Nisargadatta Maharaj 1
Ramana Maharshi
"The ego (the 'I-thought') is the first and primary illusion — the false identification with body and mind. The Self is pure, non-dual consciousness — not the ego, not the body, not the mind."
Source: Teachings of Ramana Maharshi 2
Adyashanti
"I AM is pure Being. It is the ultimate confession of Reality. Beyond ego is universal Being, beyond Being, is the Infinite."
Source: Adyashanti Teachings 3

⚠ Critical Divergence

Adyashanti creates a tripartite hierarchy (Ego → Being → Infinite) that does not exist in classical Advaita. For Nisargadatta, the Absolute is not "beyond Being" — Being (Sat) is the Absolute. The distinction between Ātman and Brahman is only apparent (due to māyāMāyāIn Advaita Vedanta, the cosmic illusion that makes the one Reality appear as many. It is the power of Brahman that projects the phenomenal world.), not ontologically real. Adyashanti's hierarchy suggests three distinct levels of reality, which contradicts the fundamental non-dual axiom: ekam eva advitīyam — "One without a second."

02 / The Nature of Ego

Ego as Illusion vs. Ego as "Natural Stage"

Classical Advaita teaches that the ego is pure illusion (avidyāAvidyāIgnorance or spiritual ignorance in Advaita Vedanta — the mistaken identification with the body-mind complex as the self. It is the root cause of bondage and suffering.) — it never actually happened. Adyashanti reframes it as a developmental phase.

Nisargadatta Maharaj
"There is no such thing as a person. There are only restrictions and limitations. The sum total of these defines the person. The 'I am' itself is going to dissolve."
Source: I Am That 1
Adyashanti
"The experience of separation, although it's illusory, seems to be a natural stage in the development of consciousness — just like adolescence is a natural stage of development... I see that sense of separate self as ultimately illusory but actually quite natural stage of development."
Source: Adyashanti — Various Talks 4

⚠ Critical Divergence

In classical Advaita, the ego is not a "natural stage" of consciousness — it is avidyā (ignorance), a superimposition (adhyāsaAdhyāsaThe superimposition of the unreal (the ego/world) upon the real (Ātman/Brahman). It is the fundamental error that Advaita seeks to correct through knowledge (jnāna).) that never actually happened. Adyashanti's view aligns more with developmental psychology or evolutionary spirituality than with the radical non-duality of Nisargadatta or Ramana, for whom the ego is pure falsehood to be seen through, not a stage to be outgrown like adolescence.

03 / The Paradox of Unity

Unity and Individuality?

In Advaita Vedanta, BrahmanBrahmanThe Ultimate Reality in Advaita Vedanta — the infinite, eternal, unchanging reality that is the substratum of all existence. It is identical to Ātman (the Self). alone is real. The jīvaJīvaThe individual soul or embodied being in Advaita Vedanta. It is identical to Brahman but appears separate due to ignorance (avidyā). (individual soul) is identical to Brahman; the difference is only apparent. There is no real individuality in the ultimate state.

Nisargadatta Maharaj
"To know the self as the only reality and all else as temporal and transient is freedom, peace and joy. There is no 'me' or 'mine' in the realized state."
Source: I Am That 1
Ramana Maharshi
"The Self is always there — it is you. There is nothing but the Self. The world is an illusion; only Brahman is real."
Source: Teachings of Ramana Maharshi 2
Adyashanti
"Enlightenment is being able to simultaneously experience unity and individuality without experiencing any conflict. We're very unique, and we're very different, and there's a beautiful, individualized manifestation of the one — but the whole substance of that is all one."
Source: Adyashanti — Various Talks 4
Adyashanti
"Non-separation and profound autonomous individuality experienced at the same moment."
Source: Adyashanti — Various Talks 4

⚠ Critical Divergence

For Nisargadatta and Ramana, there is no "individuality" in the realized state — the very concept is a residue of the ego. Adyashanti's "paradox" of unity-and-individuality is not paradoxical in Advaita; it is simply dualistic. The "individual" does not survive realization — only the Self (Brahman) remains. Adyashanti's formulation preserves the individual as a "beautiful manifestation," which classical Advaita would reject as maintaining the illusion of separation.

04 / The Role of Practice

Sadhana vs. "Letting Go"

Both Nisargadatta and Ramana emphasized sadhana (spiritual practice) as essential. Adyashanti's approach rejects structured inquiry in favor of passive surrender.

Ramana Maharshi
"Self-inquiry (ātma-vichāra) is the direct path. 'Who am I?' — trace the 'I'-thought back to its source and discover that the Self alone is real, while the ego/world are illusory appearances."
Source: Teachings of Ramana Maharshi 2
Nisargadatta Maharaj
"You have to meditate. It won't be available free. Stabilize in the 'I Am' and then transcend it."
Source: I Am That 1
Adyashanti
"Real meditation is not about mastering a technique; it's about letting go of control. You come into the natural state by letting go of control by letting go of effort and resting in a state of vividness. Whenever you aren't manipulating your experience, you're meditating."
Source: Adyashanti — The Way of Liberation 5

⚠ Critical Divergence

While Adyashanti's "letting go" sounds non-dual, it rejects the structured inquiry that Nisargadatta and Ramana insisted upon. Nisargadatta's teaching was not "just let go" — it was intense, focused investigation of the "I Am" until it dissolved. Ramana's "Who am I?" is an active inquiry, not passive letting-go. Neo-Advaita (which Adyashanti is often associated with) has been criticized for this exact departure from traditional practice.

05 / The Nature of Reality

Sat-Chit-Ananda vs. "Infinite Emptiness"

Classical Advaita describes Reality as plenary fullness — Being-Consciousness-Bliss. Adyashanti's description aligns more with Buddhist śūnyatā.

Nisargadatta Maharaj
"Awareness is primordial; it is the original state, beginning-less, endless, uncaused, unsupported, without parts, without change. The Absolute is the fullness prior to the 'I Am.'"
Source: I Am That 1
Adyashanti
"What is actually real is an infinite expanse of emptiness."
Source: Adyashanti Teachings 3

⚠ Critical Divergence

This is a philosophical chasm. For Nisargadatta, the Absolute is not empty — it is the fullness (Sat) prior to the "I Am." Adyashanti's "infinite expanse of emptiness" is a Buddhist/Mahayana formulation (śūnyatā), not the Sat (Being) of Advaita. In Advaita, emptiness would imply the absence of Brahman — an impossibility. Reality is Sat-Chit-AnandaSat-Chit-AnandaThe threefold nature of Brahman in Advaita Vedanta: Sat (Being/Existence), Chit (Consciousness), Ananda (Bliss). This is the plenary, not empty, nature of the Absolute. — full, conscious, blissful — not an "expanse of emptiness."

06 / The Nature of Awakening

Dissolution of the Perceiver vs. "Shift in Perception"

Classical Advaita teaches that realization is the transcendence of the perceiver itself. Adyashanti frames it as a change in how one perceives.

Nisargadatta Maharaj
"Realization is nothing to be gained anew. It is only becoming aware of your natural state. But this natural state is not a perception — it is the Absolute beyond consciousness."
Source: I Am That 1
Ramana Maharshi
"Realization is nothing to be gained anew. It is only becoming aware of your natural state. The Self is always there — it is you. There is nothing but the Self."
Source: Teachings of Ramana Maharshi 2
Adyashanti
"The simplest thing one can say about the experiential knowledge of awakening is that it is a shift in one's perception. Awakening is a fundamental shift in the way we perceive and feel about ourself — what and who we take ourselves to be."
Source: Adyashanti — The Way of Liberation 5

⚠ Critical Divergence

For Nisargadatta and Ramana, realization is not a shift in perception — perception (consciousness) itself is transcended. The Absolute is not perceived; it is the source of perception. Adyashanti's language keeps the perceiver intact (just shifting), whereas classical Advaita demands the total dissolution of the perceiver. The "shift in identity" language suggests the individual remains but changes perspective — precisely what Advaita denies.

07 / Ancient Authority

The Śruti & Smṛti on Non-Duality

The divergence between Adyashanti and classical Advaita is not merely a matter of personal style — it contradicts the foundational texts of the tradition itself. Here is what the ancient masters actually taught.

The Three Non-Negotiable Axioms
  • Brahman satyam — Brahman alone is real
  • Jagan mithyā — the world is illusory
  • Jīvo brahmaiva nāparāḥ — the individual is not different from Brahman
Gaudapada — Māṇḍūkya Kārikā II.32
"There is neither dissolution nor creation, none in bondage and none practicing disciplines. There is none seeking Liberation and none liberated. This is the absolute truth."
Source: Māṇḍūkya Kārikā — Gaudapada 7
Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — Verse 7
"Turiya is not that which is conscious of the inner world, nor that which is conscious of the outer world, nor that which is conscious of both, nor that which is a mass of consciousness. It is not simple consciousness nor is it unconsciousness. It is unperceived, unrelated, incomprehensible, uninferable, unthinkable and indescribable. The essence of the realisation of oneness of the Self, it is the cessation of all phenomena; it is non-dual peace-holiness-bliss. This is what is known as the Fourth. This is Ātman and this has to be realised."
Source: Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — Verse 7 8
Śaṅkarācārya — Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya 2.1.14
"In reality, the cause and effect are one and the same as the Primordial Cause is the Supreme Being and the result if any is also the Supreme Being. The Supreme Being is untainted and unattached to both the cause and effect as IT is the Unmanifest and the Unknown."
Source: Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya 2.1.14 9
Śaṅkarācārya — Vivekacūḍāmaṇi
"There is no Ignorance (Avidyā) outside the mind. The mind alone is Avidyā, the cause of the bondage of transmigration. When that is destroyed, all else is destroyed, and when it is manifested, everything else is manifested. In dreams, when there is no actual contact with the external world, the mind alone creates the whole universe consisting of the experiencer etc. Similarly in the waking state also; there is no difference. Therefore all this (phenomenal universe) is the projection of the mind."
Source: Vivekacūḍāmaṇi — Verses 169-170 10
Śaṅkarācārya — Ātma Bodha
"As one who regards a rope as a snake, so too one regarding oneself as an ego, is overcome by fear; 'I am not the ego, but the Supreme Self' — if he realizes this, he becomes fearless."
Source: Ātma Bodha — Verse on Rope-Snake 11

📜 The Ancient Consensus

These texts establish four non-negotiable principles that Adyashanti's teachings contradict: (1) There is no creation, no bondage, and no liberation — these are all conceptual fabrications (Gaudapada). (2) The Absolute (TuriyaTuriyaThe Fourth state of consciousness in the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. It is not a state of consciousness but the cessation of all phenomena, the absolute reality itself.) is not a state of consciousness or perception — it is the cessation of all phenomena, including consciousness itself (Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad). (3) Cause and effect are not two — there is only the Supreme Being, and the world is its superimposition, not a developmental stage (Śaṅkara). (4) The mind alone projects the universe; the ego is a rope mistaken for a snake — it never actually existed as a separate entity (Śaṅkara).

08 / The Rope-Snake Analogy

Adhyāsa — Superimposition, Not Development

The central pedagogical device of Advaita Vedanta is the rope-snake analogy: the ego is not a "natural stage" but a perceptual error that never actually occurred. This is not metaphorical — it is the precise technical description of avidyāAvidyāIgnorance or spiritual ignorance in Advaita Vedanta — the mistaken identification with the body-mind complex as the self. It is the root cause of bondage and suffering..

Figure 1 — The Rope-Snake Analogy (Adhyāsa)
Left: In Advaita, the ego is like a snake superimposed on a rope — it was never there, only the rope (Self) ever existed. Right: Adyashanti's model treats the ego as a real (though temporary) developmental stage, like adolescence, through which consciousness evolves toward the Infinite. This is not adhyāsa (superimposition) but pariṇāma (evolutionary transformation) — a Sāṃkhya/Yoga concept, not Advaita.

🔍 Why This Matters

Śaṅkara's adhyāsa-bhāṣya (introduction to the Brahma Sūtras) defines the entire problem of spirituality as the superimposition of the unreal (the ego/world) upon the real (Ātman). The ego is not a "stage" that consciousness passes through — it is a fundamental misperception that never actually occurred, like seeing a snake where there is only a rope. When the error is corrected, the snake does not "graduate" or "evolve" — it is simply seen to never have been. Adyashanti's developmental model contradicts this foundational teaching.

09 / Ajatavada

No Creation, No Bondage, No Liberation

Gaudapada's doctrine of ajāta (non-creation) is the most radical expression of Advaita: the world never came into being, the ego never arose, and therefore no "development" toward enlightenment is possible. This directly contradicts evolutionary spirituality.

Figure 2 — Ajāta (Non-Creation) vs. Evolutionary Spirituality
Left (Ajāta): Gaudapada's absolute truth — there is no creation, no stages, no evolution. Brahman alone is, always was, always will be. The world is māyā (illusion), not a developmental process. Right (Evolutionary): Adyashanti's model implies the ego is real enough to evolve, time is real enough for stages, and the individual is real enough to persist. This is precisely what Gaudapada denies as "the absolute truth."
Gaudapada — Māṇḍūkya Kārikā I.17
"If the phenomenal universe were real, then certainly it would disappear. The universe of duality which is cognised is mere illusion (māyā); Non-duality alone is the Supreme Reality."
Source: Māṇḍūkya Kārikā I.17 7

⚡ The Radical Implication

Gaudapada's verse I.17 is devastating to evolutionary spirituality: if the world were real, it would disappear upon realization. But since it is māyā, it never appeared in the first place. There was only ever Brahman. This means there is no "adolescent stage" of consciousness to outgrow, no "individuality" that becomes refined, and no "shift in perception" — because there is no perceiver, no perceived, and no perception. All these are conceptual fabrications superimposed on the non-dual Self. Adyashanti's entire framework assumes the reality of the individual who evolves, which Gaudapada explicitly rejects.

10 / The Two Truths

Vyāvahārika vs. Pāramārthika

Śaṅkara's doctrine of two truths (two levels of reality) is often misunderstood as a hierarchy of stages. It is not — it is a pedagogical distinction between the empirical and the absolute, with only ONE reality existing at the absolute level.

Figure 3 — Two Truths (Advaita) vs. Three Tiers (Adyashanti)
Left (Śaṅkara): Only two levels are pedagogically distinguished: the absolute (Brahman alone) and the empirical (world-appearance). At realization, the empirical collapses — it was never real. There is only ONE reality. Right (Adyashanti): Three distinct levels (Ego → Being → Infinite) are treated as ontologically real stages. This is not Advaita but a form of graduated theism or panentheism, where the individual is real but evolving.
Śaṅkarācārya — Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya (Two-Tiered Reality)
"From the absolute standpoint there is only Brahman. The empirical order is a provisional ontology, whereas the ultimate reality is a final ontology. The universe's relationship with this ground is dependent and asymmetrical. This relationship collapses at the ultimate order of nondual reality."
Source: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Śaṅkara 12

⚠ The Ontological Error

Śaṅkara's "two truths" are not two realities — they are one reality viewed from two perspectives. The vyāvahārikaVyāvahārikaThe empirical or conventional reality in Advaita's two-truth doctrine — the world of everyday experience. It is provisionally real for practical purposes but ultimately illusory from the absolute standpoint. (empirical) is a pedagogical concession for those still in ignorance; the pāramārthikaPāramārthikaThe absolute or ultimate reality in Advaita's two-truth doctrine — Brahman alone. This is the only reality that truly exists; the empirical world is only provisionally real. (absolute) is the only reality that actually exists. Adyashanti's three-tier model (Ego → Being → Infinite) treats all three as real levels through which consciousness actually progresses. This is not a "different interpretation" of Advaita — it is a different metaphysics entirely, closer to Hegelian dialectics or Sri Aurobindo's integral yoga than to Śaṅkara's radical non-duality.

Conclusion

Adyashanti's teachings, while using non-dual vocabulary, contain significant and systematic departures from classical Advaita Vedānta as defined by Śaṅkara, Gaudapada, Nisargadatta Maharaj, and Ramana Maharshi:

  1. He preserves individuality as a "beautiful manifestation" coexisting with unity — a position classical Advaita rejects as dualistic (jīvo brahmaiva nāparāḥ).
  2. He rehabilitates the ego as a "natural stage" rather than pure illusion (avidyāAvidyāIgnorance or spiritual ignorance in Advaita Vedanta — the mistaken identification with the body-mind complex as the self. It is the root cause of bondage and suffering./adhyāsaAdhyāsaThe superimposition of the unreal (the ego/world) upon the real (Ātman/Brahman). It is the fundamental error that Advaita seeks to correct through knowledge (jnāna).), contradicting the rope-snake analogy.
  3. He creates hierarchical ontologies (Ego → Being → Infinite) that don't exist in Advaita, which teaches only ONE reality (ekam eva advitīyamEkam eva advitīyamA foundational Upanishadic statement: "One without a second." This is the core axiom of Advaita Vedanta — there is only one reality (Brahman) and nothing else truly exists.).
  4. He rejects structured sādhanaSādhanaSpiritual practice or discipline in Advaita Vedanta. Classical Advaita prescribes specific practices (śravaṇa, manana, nididhyāsana) for liberation. in favor of passive "letting go," contrary to the prasthāna-trayīPrasthāna-trayīThe "triple canon" of Vedanta: the Upanishads (Śruti), the Bhagavad Gītā (Smṛti), and the Brahma Sūtras (Nyāya). In Advaita, Reality is full Being, not emptiness.) of Advaita, aligning with Mahāyāna śūnyatāŚūnyatāEmptiness or voidness in Mahāyāna Buddhism — the realization that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence.   6. He frames awakening as a "shift in perception" rather than the transcendence of the perceiver itself (prapañcoparamaPrapañcoparamaThe cessation of all phenomena (prapañca) — the definition of Turiya (the Fourth) in the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad.   7. He implies an evolutionary model of consciousness that contradicts Gaudapada's ajāta-vādaAjāta-vādaThe doctrine of non-creation in Advaita Vedanta, taught by Gaudapada. It states that the universe never actually appeared or was created — it is pure illusion from the absolute standpoint. (non-creation), the most radical teaching of Advaita.

These are not minor stylistic differences — they represent substantive philosophical divergences that place Adyashanti closer to Neo-Advaita, American pragmatic spirituality, or evolutionary panentheism than to the rigorous, radical non-duality of the Indian masters he is often compared to. The thesis is correct: Adyashanti is not truly non-dual in the classical Advaitic sense. He is a synthesizer of Buddhist emptiness, developmental psychology, and Advaita vocabulary — a valuable teacher in his own right, but not a representative of the tradition he invokes.

Śaṅkarācārya, Ātma Bodha — A prakaraṇa grantha (introductory text) attributed to Śaṅkara, using the rope-snake analogy to explain adhyāsa (superimposition). The ego is compared to a snake mistakenly seen in a rope — it never existed, and its disappearance is not a "stage" but the correction of an error. Available at chinfo.org and shlokam.org

Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Śaṅkara — Academic analysis of Śaṅkara's metaphysics, including the doctrine of two-tiered reality (vyāvahārika and pāramārthika), the indeterminability of the world (anirvacanīya), and the postulate of māyā. Available at plato.stanford.edu/entries/shankara

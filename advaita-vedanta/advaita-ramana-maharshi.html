Origin & Awakening · आदि जागरण

The Death Experience That Became Liberation

Madurai, 1896 — A boy of sixteen confronted death and found the Self.

Ramana himself described the event with extraordinary precision: "The fear of death seized me. I said to myself: 'Now death has come. What does death mean? It is the death of the body. But I am not the body. The body dies, but I, the Self, am not touched by death.' I then lay down, stiffened my limbs, and held my breath. The mind turned inward. A current of awareness arose and I realised: 'I am the immortal Self.'"

He later explained: "That state has never left me. It is not something that comes and goes. It is my natural state. The body may speak, act, or be silent, but the awareness of the Self remains unbroken."

"The Guru is not a person. Arunachala is the Guru. The Self is the Guru. The mountain called me. I never decided to come here – I was drawn."

— Ramana Maharshi, on his journey to Arunachala
The Transmission Lineage · गुरु परम्परा

Ramana Maharshi रमण महर्षि

1879 – 1950 · Tiruvannamalai

Realized at 16 through spontaneous recognition of the Self. No formal teacher, no spiritual practices before the event. Spent 54 years transmitting Self-knowledge through silence, gaze, and direct pointing to the ever-present awareness.

Arunachala Hill अरुणाचल

Sacred Mountain · Tiruvannamalai

Ramana's guru — the sacred mountain as Jyotirlinga, pillar of light. He circumambulated it barefoot and composed five hymns in its honour. "The mountain itself taught me."

Papaji (H.W.L. Poonja) पापाजी

1910 – 1997 · Lucknow

Spent 13 years with Ramana. Became the "Lightning Guru" for the way his presence triggered sudden awakenings. Key teaching: "The guru's grace is like the sun — it is always shining. The question is only whether you open your door."

David Godman

Contemporary · Custodian of the Teachings

Editor and translator of Ramana's works. Key texts: Be As You Are, The Essential Teachings. Preserves the transmission with scholarly precision and living presence.

Vedantic Roots · वैदान्तिक जड़ें

The Ancient Sources

Ramana's teaching belongs squarely within Advaita Vedanta. His contribution was directness: experiential enactment of what the ancient texts encoded in philosophy.

The method of Self-enquiry is the living practice of the Mahavakyas — the great sayings. Where the Upanishadic sages declared identity in language, Ramana pointed to immediate recognition.

The Primary Upanishads · प्राचीन उपनिषद्
Aitareya Upanishad ऐतरेय ऐतरेय ▾
Aitareya Upanishad — Rig Veda

The first primary Upanishad. Its opening mahavakya "Prajñānaṃ Brahma" (Consciousness is Brahman) is fundamental to Advaita. Ramana often quoted this to point directly to awareness itself.

Aitareya Upanishad 1.1
Chandogya Upanishad छान्दोग्य छान्दोग्य ▾
Chandogya Upanishad — Sama Veda

Contains "Tat Tvam Asi" (That Thou Art). The teaching of Uddalaka to his son Svetaketu unfolds gradually, culminating in the recognition of non-duality.

Chandogya Upanishad 6.8–15
Brihadaranyaka Upanishad बृहदारण्यक बृहदारण्यक ▾
Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — Yajur Veda

Largest Upanishad, containing "Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi" (I Am Brahman). Also contains "neti, neti" (not this, not this) — negating all objects to arrive at the irreducible witness.

Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upanishad 1.4, 4.2
Mandukya Upanishad माण्डूक्य माण्डूक्य ▾
Mandukya Upanishad — Atharva Veda

Smallest but most concentrated — 12 verses. Teaches the four states: waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and turīya. Forms the basis of Ramana's teaching on states of consciousness.

Māṇḍūkya Upanishad 1–12

"The conclusion of the Vedas is that Brahman alone exists as the one undivided reality. There is no world apart from it; there is no second thing. Self-enquiry is the direct means to verify this."

— Ramana Maharshi, in dialogue
मिथ्या
Stage I Mistaken Identity मिथ्या

The Starting Condition

Vyakti · The Apparent Person

The ordinary human takes itself to be a body-mind organism — a vyakti, a person with a name, biography, desires and fears. This identification is the root of all suffering. It is not a moral failing but an innocent error of attention: awareness has forgotten its own nature by becoming absorbed in its content.

The Taittirīya Upaniṣad maps five sheaths — kośas — in which the Self appears to be embedded. For Ramana, crucially, these layers are not veils obscuring awareness but objects appearing within consciousness.

The Five Sheaths · पञ्च कोश
Physical Body Annamaya-kośa अन्नमय कोश▾

The physical body, outermost sheath. Made of food, sustained by food. Not the perceiver — an object perceived within consciousness.

Vital Energy Prāṇamaya-kośa प्राणमय कोश▾

The vital breath animating the body. Contains physiological functions. Subtler than physical body, but still an object in awareness.

Mental Manomaya-kośa मनोमय कोश▾

The mind, emotions, thinking faculty. Where the ego-sense operates. Processes sensory input and generates desires and aversions.

Intellect Vijñānamaya-kośa विज्ञानमय कोश▾

The intellect and higher mind. Contains capacity for discrimination. Not yet the Self — even the discriminating intellect is an object.

Bliss Ānandamaya-kośa आनन्दमय कोश▾

Subtle layer of peace experienced in meditation or deep sleep. Closer to Self but still an appearance — the subtlest veil before the witness.

"As long as you identify with the body-mind you are conditioned. Once you stabilize in the knowledge 'I am' unconditionally you are free."

— Ramana Maharshi
आत्मविचार
Stage II The Core Practice आत्मविचार

Self-Enquiry

Atma Vicara · The Investigation of the Self

The pivot of Ramana's teaching. Locate the source of the "I"-thought — the sense that says "I exist." This is not conceptual thinking but direct inquiry: "Who am I?" "To whom does this arise?" "What is the nature of the 'I'?"

Ramana said: "Of all the thoughts that rise in the mind, the thought 'I' is the first thought. Enquire into the nature of the 'I'. This is Self-enquiry." The method is simultaneously the simplest and the most direct: trace every appearance back to its source, and that source will dissolve when looked at directly.

The "I"-thought and the Self · अहंकार और आत्मन्
Māyā · माया Phenomenal Universe

The Witness साक्षी Turiya

The "I"-thought (ego) is a ripple on the surface of the Self. Self-enquiry traces this thought back to its source, dissolving the illusion of separation.

This is not a mantra to repeat. It is the actual, living recognition of consciousness itself, investigating its own nature. The question "Who am I?" is a pointer — the real work is turning attention backward toward the source.

"By the inquiry 'Who am I?' the mind will merge into the Self. If the mind is not destroyed, Self-realisation is not possible. The mind is destroyed through 'Who am I?' enquiry."

— Ramana Maharshi
अविद्या
Stage III The Obstacles अविद्या

The Three Veils

Avidya · Fundamental Ignorance

Three fundamental errors sustain the illusion of separation. Each must be seen through directly — not merely understood intellectually, for intellectual understanding itself is a product of mind.

Body Identification · देहाभिमान

Mistaking the body for the self. The body is an object perceived within consciousness — it is not the perceiver. Ramana: "The body is composed of five elements and is insentient. Can it say 'I'? No."

Mind Attachment · संकल्प

The mind generates concepts, including spiritual concepts. Every concept must ultimately be relinquished. The Upanishads' "neti, neti" (not this, not this) negates all objects to arrive at the irreducible witness.

Sense of Doership · कर्तृत्व

The belief "I act, I choose, I achieve" sustains separation. Actions happen through the body-mind; there is no independent doer. Ramana: "Doership is only an illusion. The Self has nothing to do with activities."

"The very idea that you are the body is ridiculous. Consciousness is experiencing its manifestation. You are not bound."

— Ramana Maharshi
तुरीय
Stage IV States of Consciousness तुरीय

The Fourth

Turiya · Beyond the Three States

Rooted in the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad — twelve verses containing the most concentrated teaching of consciousness — Ramana maps experience across four dimensions. All three ordinary states come and go; awareness, the witness, persists through them all.

विवेक
Stage V The Crucial Move विवेक

The Critical Distinction

Viveka · Discrimination Between Mind and Self

The ultimate distinction: the "I"-thought is not the Self. The ego-sense, however refined, is still an object in awareness. Ramana taught that the search itself is for what was never lost, and the seeker is the sought.

Tracing the "I" inward, you discover that there is no "I" — only awareness that is aware of the apparent "I." The "I" thought has a source, and when that source is directly contacted, the separate self-sense dissolves.

मौन
Stage VI The Unspoken मौन

Silence & Grace

Mouna · The Highest Teaching

Ramana taught that the highest transmission is not through words but through silence. "Silence is the highest teaching and the highest compassion." His presence itself was the teaching — a direct transmission from Self to Self.

Grace is not something to be acquired. It is your own nature. The Self is grace. What appears as grace is simply the Self recognizing itself. The guru's role is to dissolve the false identification, and this happens through the power of being, not doing.

"The Guru is not a person. Arunachala is the Guru. The Self is the Guru. The mountain called me, and I came and never left."

— Ramana Maharshi, on finding his guru
साहज
Stage VII The Fruit साहज

The Jñāni

Sahaja Samadhi · The Natural State

The jñāni is one in whom the separate self-sense has completely dissolved. The body continues, words arise, activities happen. But there is no person claiming to be the doer. The jñāni does not claim liberation — liberation is simply the recognition that separation was never real.

Ramana lived this state for 54 years — from his spontaneous realization at 16 until his death in 1950. The very fact of his existence was the teaching: that it is possible to abide as the Self while the body-mind continues its apparent functioning.

"You are already That. The seeker is the sought. There is no difference between the Guru and the disciple. They are one consciousness. When the seeker knows this, he is free."

— Ramana Maharshi
Sadhana · साधना

How to Practice

A practical guide to Atma Vicara — Self-enquiry as lived practice, in Ramana's own words.

I
Find the Sense of "I" अस्मि
"Of all the thoughts that rise in the mind, the thought 'I' is the first thought. Before any other thought, locate the bare sense of existing — not 'I am this body' or 'I am thinking' — just the raw fact of being. This is the gateway." — Ramana Maharshi, Who Am I?

It is always present; the task is simply to notice it before thought adds anything to it.

II
Turn Attention Inward मुड़ना
"Instead of following thoughts outward, gently reverse attention. Not toward any object, but toward the sense of 'I' itself. This reversal is the entire method." — Ramana Maharshi, Talks

Do not concentrate or strain. Simply rest in the recognition of "I Am." It is effortless because it is already here.

III
Ask "Who?" कः
"When a thought arises, gently ask: 'To whom? To me. Then who am I?' This is not intellectual analysis. It is a gentle inquiry that returns awareness to its source." — Ramana Maharshi, Who Am I?

The question "Who am I?" is a pointer — the real work is turning attention backward toward the source of the "I"-thought.

IV
Allow the Mind to Subside शान्तिः
"When attention remains steady on the 'I'-sense, the mind naturally subsides. Thoughts lose urgency. Stillness opens. This is approaching Turiya — the recognition of the witness always present." — Ramana Maharshi, Final Talks

The mind is not to be brought to stillness by force. It is to be understood. When the mind is seen as nothing but consciousness, it becomes still on its own.

V
Surrender as Complement समर्पण
"For those inclined to devotion, complete surrender of the ego-sense is the path. Self-enquiry and surrender are two faces of the same movement — both dissolve identification." — Ramana Maharshi, Day by Day

Surrender is not giving up the world — it is giving up the false sense of being a separate doer. The Self already is.

✦
The Final Understanding अनासक्ति
"There is nothing to gain. The Self is already what you are. The effort of self-enquiry is not an effort to become — it is the dissolution of the habit of appearing to be something else." — Ramana Maharshi, The Essential Teaching

This is the recognition that the seeker is the sought. The search is for what was never lost. When this is seen, the search ends.

"Just 'TO BE' and to remain steady with the awareness 'I AM' is the beginning and end of spiritual practice."

— Ramana Maharshi
Glossary · शब्दकोश

Key Terms

Sanskrit and technical terms used throughout this teaching.

🔍

No matching terms found.

Ātman आत्मन्
The Self

The eternal, unchanging consciousness that is your true nature. Beyond mind, body, and all attributes. Not to be confused with ego.

Brahman ब्रह्मन्
The Absolute

The ultimate reality, the ground of existence. Non-dual, infinite, eternal. In Advaita, Ātman and Brahman are one — the Self is Brahman.

Turīya तुरीय
The Fourth

Not a state but the unchanging witness of waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. The background awareness in which all three appear and disappear.

Jñāni ज्ञानी
The Knower

One who has realized the Self. The separate self-sense has dissolved completely. Ramana is a jñāni.

Ahaṃkāra अहंकार
The Ego

The sense of being a separate individual. The "I"-thought that appears first and is root of all other thoughts. Dissolved by Self-enquiry.

Vicāra विचार
Enquiry

The method of turning attention inward to investigate the "I"-thought. The primary sadhana of Ramana's teaching.

Māyā माया
Appearance

The power of illusion. Not a trick — simply the apparent appearance of the world on the Self. Like images in a mirror.

Mouna मौन
Silence

The highest teaching and the highest compassion. The direct transmission from Self to Self, beyond words.

Sahaja साहज
Natural State

The natural, effortless abidance as the Self. Not something to be attained — the recognition of what always is.

Kośas कोश
The Five Sheaths

Annamaya, Prāṇamaya, Manomaya, Vijñānamaya, Ānandamaya. All appearances in consciousness, not the Self.

Origin & Awakening

The Death Experience That Became Liberation

Madurai, 1896 — A boy of sixteen confronted death and found the Self

Ramana himself described the event: "The fear of death seized me. I said to myself: 'Now death has come. What does death mean? It is the death of the body. But I am not the body. The body dies, but I, the Self, am not touched by death.' I then lay down, stiffened my limbs, and held my breath. The mind turned inward. A current of awareness arose and I realised: 'I am the immortal Self.'"

He later explained: "That state has never left me. It is not something that comes and goes. It is my natural state. The body may speak, act, or be silent, but the awareness of the Self remains unbroken."

He concluded: "The Guru is not a person. Arunachala is the Guru. The Self is the Guru. The mountain called me. I never decided to come here – I was drawn."

Context

Arunachala as Guru

Ramana said: "Arunachala is the embodiment of Shiva, the form of the Guru. I came here and never left. The mountain itself taught me. There is no need for a human Guru when the Self is the only true teacher."

He added: "The outer Guru confirms what the inner Guru already knows. The true teaching is not in words. It is the silence that points to the Self."

The Transmission Line

Teachers & Living Lineage

How Ramana's teaching continues through his direct disciples

Ramana never established a formal order or declared a successor. Yet his teaching has transmitted naturally through those transformed by his presence. These figures function as powerful pointers — not gurus in the hierarchical sense, but as vessels of the same Recognition.

Papaji (Sri H.W.L. Poonja)
1910–1997 · Lucknow, India

After Ramana's glance dissolved his seeking, he spent 13 years with Ramana. He became the "Lightning Guru" for the way his presence would trigger sudden awakenings. Key teaching: "The guru's grace is like the sun — it is always shining. The question is only whether you open your door."

⚠

Note: Papaji's lineage includes teachers like Mooji and Gangaji. Seekers should always verify authenticity through their own recognition, not through lineage.

Annamalai Swami
1906–1995 · Arunachala

Served Ramana for 23 years and was fully liberated in his presence. Lived 45 years in a cave on Arunachala in complete solitude. His presence was an embodiment of sahaja samadhi — natural, unbroken abidance as the Self.

Sri Lakshman Swamy
1914–2005

Though he met Ramana only briefly, his realization was a direct fruit of Ramana's teaching. He taught a refined synthesis of Self-enquiry with detailed maps of the inner stages. Key text: The Truth Revealed.

Living Authority: David Godman
Contemporary

Editor of Ramana's words and custodian of the authentic teaching. Key texts: Be As You Are, The Essential Teachings. His work preserves the transmission with scholarly precision.

Ancient Origins

Vedantic & Upanishadic Roots

Ramana did not invent his teaching — he recovered it from its source

Ramana's teaching belongs squarely within Advaita Vedanta — the non-dual philosophy derived from the Upanishads. He never claimed originality. His contribution was the directness with which he pointed to what the ancient texts encoded in philosophical language: that consciousness itself, the witness behind all experience, is the one undivided reality.

The method of Self-enquiry is an experiential enactment of the Mahavakyas (the "great sayings"). Where the Upanishadic sages declared the identity of Self and Absolute in language, Ramana pointed to the immediate, felt recognition.

Philosophical Lineage

Adi Shankaracharya & the Advaita Tradition

Ramana's teaching is unmistakably within the Advaita Vedanta tradition systematized by Adi Shankaracharya (788–820 CE). Shankara's pivotal contribution was his interpretation of the Prasthanatrayi, demonstrating their convergent teaching: the non-dual identity of Ātman and Brahman. However, Ramana simplified Shankara's elaborate philosophical apparatus into a single direct inquiry: find the source of the "I."

"The conclusion of the Vedas is that Brahman alone exists as the one undivided reality. There is no world apart from it; there is no second thing. Self-enquiry is the direct means to verify this." — Ramana Maharshi, in dialogue

The Obstacles

The Three Veils

Three fundamental errors sustain separation: body-identification, mind-attachment, and doership. Self-enquiry undoes them simultaneously.

◈
Body-Identification
Dehātma-buddhi
"The body is composed of five elements and is insentient. Can it say 'I'? No." (Who Am I?)
Ask "To whom does this body appear?" – trace to source.
◇
Mind-Attachment
Manas-abhimāna
"The mind is a bundle of thoughts. The 'I' thought is the root of all other thoughts." (Talks, §33)
Watch thoughts; ask "To whom do they arise?" – they dissolve.
◆
Sense of Doership
Kartṛtva-buddhi
"Doership is only an illusion. The Self has nothing to do with activities." (Talks, §375)
Before action, ask "Who is about to act?" – the doer dissolves.

The Method

How to Practice Self-Enquiry

A practical guide to Ātma Vicāra

"

The state of self-realisation is not something to be attained afresh. It is already there. All that is necessary is to get rid of the thought 'I have not realised.' Stillness or peace is realisation.

— Ramana Maharshi · Talks (1935–1950)

1
Find the Sense of "I"

Sit quietly. Before any thought arises — before "I am tired," "I am happy," "I am the body" — there is a bare, raw sense of existing. That bare "I am" prior to all qualifiers is where the enquiry begins.

2
Turn Attention Toward the "I"

Instead of following thoughts outward into objects, gently turn attention inward — not toward any thought, but toward the very sense of "I" itself. This reversal of attention is the entire method.

3
Notice the Thoughts That Arise

When a thought arises, gently ask: "To whom does this thought arise? To me. Then who am I?" Return to the sense of "I." It is a gentle, persistent return to the witness.

4
Allow the Mind to Subside

When attention rests steadily on the "I"-sense, the mind naturally subsides. Thoughts lose urgency; stillness opens. This is the approach to Turiya — the recognition of the witnessing awareness always present.

5
Surrender as Complement

For those inclined to devotion, Ramana offered complete surrender of the ego-sense to God. Self-enquiry and surrender are two faces of the same movement — both dissolve the ego-sense and arrive at the Self.

✦
The Final Understanding

There is nothing to gain, nothing to achieve, no distance to travel. The Self is already what you are. The effort of self-enquiry is not an effort to become something — it is the gradual dissolution of the habit of appearing to be something else.

Primary Sources

The Works of Ramana Maharshi

The Collected Works and their context in the Vedantic tradition

Nan Yar? — Who Am I?
1902 · Tamil Prose

The foundational text — answers given in writing to Sivaprakasam Pillai. The clearest single statement of Ramana's core method.

Vicharasangraham — Self-Enquiry
1901–02 · Tamil Prose

Companion to Who Am I? — answers to Gambhiram Seshayya. The only prose works Ramana composed in his own hand.

Ulladu Narpadu — Reality in Forty Verses
1928 · Tamil Verse

The deepest philosophical work. Forty verses on the nature of reality — his closest engagement with the Upanishadic tradition in his own words.

Upadesa Saram — Essence of Instruction
1927 · Sanskrit & Tamil Verse

Thirty Sanskrit verses condensing the essence of all sadhana across the three paths — jñāna, bhakti, and karma. One of Ramana's most-chanted texts.

Five Hymns to Arunachala
1914–1918 · Tamil Verse

Devotional poetry to the sacred mountain-guru. The most direct window into Ramana's personal relationship with Arunachala.

Sri Ramana Gita
1917 · Ganapati Muni

Dialogues between Ramana and Ganapati Muni, cast in Sanskrit in the form of the Bhagavad Gita — a precise record of the teaching.

Talks with Sri Ramana Maharshi
1935–39 · Munagala Venkataramiah

Three volumes of daily conversations. The richest single record of Ramana's spontaneous teaching.

Be As You Are
1985 · Ed. David Godman

The finest thematic anthology. The ideal first book and a reliable scholarly resource.

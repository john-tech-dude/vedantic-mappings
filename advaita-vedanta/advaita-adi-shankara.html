Chapter I Pramāṇas प्रमाण

The Six Means of Valid Knowledge

How we know what we know — the Vedantic theory of knowledge

Adi Shankara systematised the six pramāṇas, the means by which knowledge is reliably acquired. They are foundational to Advaita epistemology and distinguish it from other Indian schools.

The Six Pramāṇas · Six Means of Knowledge
CHIT
Consciousness
II Anumāṇa अनुमान Inference
III Upamāṇa उपमान Comparison
IV Arthāpatti अर्थापत्ति Postulation
V Anupalabdhi अनुपलब्धि Non-Apprehension
I Pratyakṣa प्रत्यक्ष Perception
VI Śabda शब्द Verbal Testimony
The six pramāṇas radiate from consciousness. Śabda (scriptural testimony) is the sole means for knowing Brahman, which transcends perception and inference.
I
Pratyakṣa — Perception
Direct sensory knowledge. The most immediate pramāṇa, operating through the five senses and the mind.
II
Anumāṇa — Inference
Knowledge through reasoning and logical deduction. Where there is smoke, there must be fire.
III
Upamāṇa — Comparison
Knowledge by analogy and similarity — recognizing the unknown through its resemblance to the known.
IV
Arthāpatti — Postulation
Knowledge through circumstantial implication. A man who never eats by day yet remains plump must eat by night.
V
Anupalabdhi — Non-Apprehension
Knowledge of absence through non-perception. "There is no pot on the ground" is itself a valid cognition.
VI
Śabda — Verbal Testimony
Knowledge from reliable scripture, especially the Upaniṣads. For Shankara, śabda is the sole means for knowing Brahman, which transcends both sense and reason.

Shankara argues that while the first five pramāṇas operate within the empirical world, only śabda (the Upaniṣadic revelation) can reveal the transcendent, non‑dual reality. This is because Brahman is not an object of perception or inference; it is the very subject – the self‑luminous consciousness.

Śabda Pramāṇa

"The scripture is the only means for knowing Brahman, because Brahman is beyond all sense perception and reasoning."

Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya 1.1.2

The Scope of Reason

"Reason cannot grasp what is beyond the senses. It must be guided by scripture in matters of ultimate reality."

Taittirīya Bhāṣya

Chapter II Two Levels of Reality व्यावहारिक · पारमार्थिक

Two Levels of Reality

Vyāvahārika (empirical) and Pāramārthika (absolute)

Shankara distinguishes between two orders of reality. The vyāvahārika sattā (empirical reality) is the world of everyday experience – it is real for all practical purposes, but ultimately it is a superimposition on the absolute. The pāramārthika sattā (absolute reality) is Brahman alone, non‑dual, unchanging, and free from all attributes.

This distinction resolves the apparent contradiction between the Upaniṣads' declaration of non‑duality and our everyday perception of a manifold world. The world is not absolutely false (like a rabbit's horn), nor is it absolutely real (like Brahman). It is mithyā – neither real nor unreal, but a dependent appearance.

Two Orders of Reality · द्विविध सत्ता
PĀRAMĀRTHIKA SATTĀ
BRAHMAN — THE ABSOLUTE
Non-dual, unchanging, attributeless, pure consciousness
Superimposition · अध्यास
VYĀVAHĀRIKA SATTĀ
THE EMPIRICAL WORLD — MITHYĀ
Names and forms, cause and effect, the waking-dreaming-deep sleep states
— THE ROPE MISTAKEN FOR A SNAKE —
The empirical world (vyāvahārika) is a superimposition on the absolute (pāramārthika). Just as a rope is mistaken for a snake, Brahman is mistaken for the manifold world.

"The world is real as long as the cause of its appearance (avidyā) persists. When avidyā is destroyed by knowledge, the world ceases to appear as separate from Brahman."

— Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya 2.1.14
ब्रह्म
Chapter III Nirguṇa & Saguṇa Brahman निर्गुण · सगुण

Nirguṇa & Saguṇa Brahman

The attributeless absolute and the personal God

One of Shankara's most important distinctions is between Nirguṇa Brahman (Brahman without qualities) and Saguṇa Brahman (Brahman with qualities, i.e., Īśvara or the personal God). Nirguṇa Brahman is the ultimate, formless, attributeless reality that cannot be described in words. Saguṇa Brahman is the same reality seen through the lens of avidyā (ignorance) – the Lord who creates, preserves, and destroys the universe, who is worshipped through devotion.

For Shankara, devotion to Saguṇa Brahman is a valid and necessary step for most seekers. It purifies the mind and leads eventually to the direct realisation of Nirguṇa Brahman. This is the basis of the classical Advaita path: first, worship and devotion (bhakti); later, discriminative knowledge (jñāna) that transcends all forms.

Nirguṇa & Saguṇa Brahman · निर्गुण · सगुण
NIRGUṆA BRAHMAN
Attributeless · Formless · Unchanging
Pure consciousness, beyond all description, the ultimate reality
THE SAME REALITY seen through the lens of avidyā (ignorance)
SAGUṆA BRAHMAN
Īśvara · The Personal God
Creator, preserver, destroyer — worshipped through devotion (bhakti)
Nirguṇa and Saguṇa Brahman are not two different realities. Saguṇa is Nirguṇa reflected through the mind of the devotee — the same sun seen through different clouds.
Nirguṇa Brahman

"Brahman is without parts, without actions, without attributes, without comparison, without defects. It is the unchanging, pure consciousness."

Vivekachūḍāmaṇi 256

Saguṇa Brahman

"For those who cannot fix their minds on the attributeless, the Lord with attributes is given as an object of meditation."

Commentary on Bhagavad Gītā 12.1

Chapter IV Mahāvākyas महावाक्य

The Four Great Sayings

The essence of Upaniṣadic teaching

Shankara identified four "great sayings" (mahāvākyas) from the four Vedas, each declaring the identity of the individual self with Brahman. These are the central mantras of Advaita meditation:

The Four Mahāvākyas · चत्वारि महावाक्यानि
Prajñānaṁ Brahma
Consciousness is Brahman
Aitareya Upaniṣad · Rig Veda
Aham Brahmāsmi
I am Brahman
Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad · Yajur Veda
Tat Tvam Asi
That thou art
Chāndogya Upaniṣad · Sāma Veda
Ayam Ātmā Brahma
This self is Brahman
Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad · Atharva Veda
AUM
The four Mahāvākyas — each from a different Veda — all point to the identity of Ātman and Brahman. They are not mere statements but direct pointers for meditation.

These sayings are not merely philosophical statements; they are direct pointers for meditation (nididhyāsana). By constantly reflecting on them, the mind becomes saturated with the identity of Atman and Brahman, and finally the direct realisation dawns.

"The entire Vedanta is summed up in the four great sayings. He who understands them attains liberation, even if he is ignorant of the rest of scripture."

— Upadeśa Sāhasrī 18.19
Lineage · गुरु परम्परा

The Unbroken Voice of Non‑Duality

From Ashtavakra to Gaudapada to Shankara — the living current of ajāta vāda.

Shankara did not invent Advaita; he gave it a systematic, logical framework and defended it against rival schools. But the living teaching predates him by centuries. The Aṣṭāvakra Gītā, a fierce dialogue between the sage Ashtavakra and King Janaka, already contains the purest expression of ajāta vāda – the doctrine that nothing is ever born. Shankara quotes from this tradition implicitly, and his own works echo its uncompromising tone.

Shankara's genius was to marry the direct pointing of the Upaniṣads and the ajāta vāda of Gaudapada with rigorous logic, making Advaita intellectually unassailable. He travelled across India, defeated scholars in debate, and established four monasteries (Śṛṅgerī, Dvārakā, Purī, Badrīnāth) to preserve and transmit the teaching. In this way, the ancient voice of Ashtavakra – "You are pure awareness, the witness of everything" – became the central teaching of classical Vedanta.

"You are the one witness of all. You are unconditioned and actionless. You are the Self, the supreme Brahman. Give up the delusion that you are the body."

— Aṣṭāvakra Gītā 1.5
The Golden Thread · सुवर्ण सूत्र

Ashtavakra अष्टावक्र

Mythic Sage · Author of the Aṣṭāvakra Gītā

The sage with eight bends. His dialogue with King Janaka is the earliest-known expression of radical non-duality: "You are pure awareness — the witness of all."

Gaudapada गौडपाद

c. 6th–7th century CE · Ajata Vada

The first systematic exponent of Advaita in Sanskrit. Author of the Mandukya Karika, establishing the doctrine of non-origination.

Govinda Bhagavatpada गोविन्द भगवत्पाद

Disciple of Gaudapada · Guru of Shankara

Gaudapada's direct disciple. The bridge between the Karika and Shankara's systematic exposition.

Adi Shankara आदि शङ्कर

c. 8th century CE · Systematizer

The great disciple of Govinda Bhagavatpada. Wrote commentaries on the Karika, the Upanishads, and the Brahma Sutras. Established Advaita Vedanta as India's dominant philosophical school.

Aṣṭāvakra Gītā 8.1

Chapter VI Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya साधन चतुष्टय

The Fourfold Qualifications

The preparatory disciplines for Jñāna Yoga

Shankara taught that not everyone is ready for direct Self‑enquiry. One must first cultivate the fourfold qualifications (sādhana chatuṣṭaya) to purify the mind:

I
Viveka — Discrimination
The capacity to distinguish the eternal (Brahman) from the temporary (world, body, mind). The first and most essential qualification.
II
Vairāgya — Dispassion
Non-attachment to the enjoyment of fruits of actions, both in this life and the next. Freedom from craving for external rewards.
III
Ṣaṭsampatti — The Six Virtues
Tranquillity (śama), restraint (dama), renunciation (uparati), forbearance (titikṣā), faith (śraddhā), and concentration (samādhāna). The purification of the instrument of knowledge.
IV
Mumukṣutva — Longing for Liberation
An intense, burning desire for liberation from the bondage of ignorance. The fire that drives the seeker to the truth.

Only when these are firmly established is the seeker qualified to hear (śravaṇa), reflect (manana), and meditate (nididhyāsana) on the great sayings. For Shankara, liberation is not a reward – it is the natural result of removing the ignorance that conceals one's true nature.

"The mind becomes purified by the practice of these virtues, and then the truth of the Upaniṣads shines forth like a lamp in a windless place."

— Vivekachūḍāmaṇi 18
ज्ञान
Chapter VII Jñāna Yoga ज्ञान योग

The Path of Jñāna Yoga

Śravaṇa, Manana, Nididhyāsana — Hearing, Reflection, Deep Meditation

Jñāna Yoga, the path of knowledge, is the direct means to liberation in Advaita. It does not involve creating a new state of mind, but rather removing the ignorance that obscures the ever-present Self. This is achieved through three stages, which correspond to the cultivation of the Sādhana Chatuṣṭaya (the fourfold qualifications).

Śravaṇa

Hearing the Upaniṣadic teachings from a qualified teacher. This is the foundational stage, where the seeker receives the mahāvākyas with faith and listens attentively to their meaning.

Requires Viveka & Śraddhā

Manana

Reflecting on the teachings through logic and reasoning. The seeker contemplates the teaching repeatedly, removing all doubts and objections through intellectual analysis.

Requires Vairāgya & Śama-Dama

Nididhyāsana

Deep, unbroken meditation on the identity of Atman and Brahman. This is not a forced concentration, but a spontaneous, effortless absorption in the Self, where the mind becomes one with its source.

Requires Mumukṣutva & Samādhāna

These three stages are not strictly sequential; they interweave and support each other. As reflection deepens, meditation arises; as meditation matures, the teaching is heard with greater clarity. Ultimately, they culminate in aparokṣānubhūti — direct, immediate realisation of Brahman.

"Hearing alone does not remove the deep-rooted notion 'I am the doer, I am the enjoyer' — it must be followed by reasoning and steady, one-pointed reflection until the truth of one's identity with Brahman is directly realized."

— Adapted from the teaching of the Upadeśa Sāhasrī

Chapter VIII Āmnāya Mathas आम्नाय मठ

The Four Monasteries

The cardinal seats of Advaita — established by Shankara

To preserve and propagate the Advaita teaching, Adi Shankara established four monasteries (mathas) in the four corners of India. Each matha was entrusted with one of the four Vedas and continues the unbroken lineage of Śaṅkarācāryas to this day.

These mathas became the institutional backbone of Advaita Vedanta. Each is headed by a Śaṅkarācārya, who serves as the spiritual head and guardian of the tradition. They continue to teach, ordain monks, and preserve the vast literary and philosophical heritage of Shankara.

"These monasteries are not merely institutions but living embodiments of the teaching. They are the pillars that uphold the dharma of non-duality."

— Traditional verse
भाष्य
Chapter IX Granthas ग्रन्थ

The Literary Legacy of Shankara

Commentaries, independent treatises, and hymns

All of Shankara's works are characterised by razor‑sharp logic, poetic beauty, and an unwavering pointing to the non‑dual Self. They remain the foundation of Advaita Vedanta to this day.

I
Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya
His magnum opus — a commentary on the Brahma Sūtras of Bādarāyaṇa, establishing Advaita through rigorous dialectic.
II
Commentaries on Ten Principal Upaniṣads
Īśa, Kena, Kaṭha, Praśna, Muṇḍaka, Māṇḍūkya, Taittirīya, Aitareya, Chāndogya, and Bṛhadāraṇyaka — each illuminating the mahāvākyas.
III
Bhagavad Gītā Bhāṣya
An exposition of the Gītā from the Advaita perspective, reconciling karma, bhakti, and jñāna into a single non-dual vision.
IV
Upadeśa Sāhasrī
A thousand teachings in prose and verse — his own independent manual for direct disciples, unmatched in clarity.
V
Vivekachūḍāmaṇi
"The Crest‑Jewel of Discrimination" — a favourite introductory text for seekers, guiding the mind from bondage to liberation.
VI
Ātmabodha
"Self‑Knowledge" — a short poetic summary of Advaita, lucid and direct, designed for immediate contemplation.
VII
Hymns to the Deities
Devotional hymns to Śiva, Devī, and Viṣṇu — revealing his reverence for Saguṇa Brahman and the unity of bhakti and jñāna.

These works form the bedrock of classical Advaita Vedanta, studied and revered to this day.

Chapter X Comparative Thresholds तुलना · संवाद

Advaita in Context

Śaṅkara's Advaita compared with Viśiṣṭādvaita, Dvaita, and Buddhist Idealism

Shankara's Advaita Vedanta was not the only game in town. It emerged in a vibrant philosophical landscape, debating with rival schools that offered alternative visions of reality. Understanding these distinctions sharpens our appreciation of Advaita's unique position.

Tradition Core Teaching Key Distinction from Advaita
Śaṅkara's Advaita Non-dualism — Brahman alone is real; the world is mithyā (dependent appearance). The sole reality is attributeless Brahman. The world and souls are ultimately identical with Brahman.
Rāmānuja's Viśiṣṭādvaita Qualified Non-dualism — Brahman is a whole with attributes and souls as its parts. Brahman has qualities (Saguṇa). The world and souls are real and distinct, though inseparable from God.
Madhva's Dvaita Dualism — God (Viṣṇu) and individual souls are eternally distinct. Complete and eternal distinction between God, soul, and world. Liberation is not union but eternal service.
Buddhist Vijñānavāda Idealism — Consciousness alone exists; external objects are mental projections. Denies a permanent self (Ātman) or absolute Brahman. Consciousness is momentary and self-luminous.

The Living Debate

These philosophical differences shaped the intellectual landscape of India for centuries. Shankara engaged deeply with all these schools, refuting their positions with logic and scriptural interpretation. Yet he also borrowed from them, adopting the dialectical tools of the Buddhists and the devotional language of the Bhakti traditions. Advaita is not a monolithic system but a dynamic, living dialogue.

Despite their differences, all these traditions share a common goal: the cessation of suffering and the realisation of the highest good. For Shankara, that good is the direct, immediate knowledge of one's identity with Brahman — a knowledge that is not merely intellectual but transformative, liberating the mind from the illusion of separation.

"Brahma satyaṁ jagan mithyā jīvo brahmaiva nāparaḥ"
Brahman is real; the world is unreal; the jiva is none other than Brahman. — Adi Shankara, Brahma Jñānāvalīmālā 20

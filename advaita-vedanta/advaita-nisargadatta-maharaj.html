परम्परा

Origin & Lineage

The Inchegiri Sampradāya — Navnath Lineage of the Nine Masters

Sri Siddharameshwar Maharaj सिद्धरामेश्वर

1888 – 1936

"You are not what you take yourself to be." His single instruction to Nisargadatta: abide in the sense of "I Am." Within three years, by his disciple's own testimony, the work was complete. Nisargadatta would later say: "As a result of faith and earnest application, I realised my Self within three years." The Inchegiri Sampradāya traces to the Navnath — nine masters revered across India. Unlike Shankara's Advaita, which emphasizes scriptural analysis, this tradition focuses on the direct experience of consciousness itself.

Nisargadatta Maharaj निसर्गदत्त

1897 – 1981

Ordinary householder and beedi-shop owner until age 36. His realisation came through three years of practice following his guru's single instruction. He taught from his humble Khetwadi flat in Mumbai for forty years, attracting Western seekers in the 1970s who helped publish his teachings globally. He died of throat cancer in 1981, continuing to teach from his sickbed — the body's dissolution was simply the return of an appearance to its source.

I Am That अहम् तत्

1973

Translated by Maurice Frydman, a Polish engineer who became Nisargadatta's devoted disciple. The conversations were recorded in 1972–73 in Khetwadi and published by Chetana Pvt. Ltd., Bombay — never since out of print. Key transmitters include Ramesh Balsekar, who bridged the teaching with Western psychology; Jean Dunn, who preserved the final talks in Prior to Consciousness; and Robert Powell, who created accessible Western introductions.

"My Guru told me: you are the Parabrahman. The words he gave me touched me very deeply. I abided in one thing only: the words of my Guru are the truth."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj, on Siddharameshwar
मिथ्या
Stage I Mistaken Identity मिथ्या

The Starting Condition

Vyakti व्यक्ति — The Apparent Person

The ordinary human takes itself to be a body-mind organism — a vyakti व्यक्ति, a person with a name, biography, desires and fears. This identification is the root of all suffering. It is not a moral failing but an innocent error of attention: awareness has forgotten its own nature by becoming absorbed in its content.

The Taittirīya Upaniṣad तैत्तिरीय maps five sheaths — kośas कोश — in which the Self appears to be embedded. For Nisargadatta, crucially, these layers are not veils obscuring awareness but objects appearing within consciousness. This distinction matters: not layers hiding awareness, but contents within it.

The Five Sheaths · पञ्च कोश — Click to Expand
Physical Body Annamaya-kośa अन्नमय कोश▾
Annamaya Kośa — The Food Sheath

The physical body, outermost of the five sheaths. Made of food and sustained by food, it is the gross form upon which the mistake "I am this body" is superimposed. It is an object perceived within consciousness — not the perceiver.

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.1–2
Vital Energy Prāṇamaya-kośa प्राणमय कोश▾
Prāṇamaya Kośa — The Energy Sheath

The vital breath animating the body. Contains the five physiological functions (prāṇas). Subtler than the physical body, but still an object in awareness — still not the subject.

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.2–3
Mental Manomaya-kośa मनोमय कोश▾
Manomaya Kośa — The Mental Sheath

The mind, emotions, and thinking faculty. The arena where the ego-sense (ahaṃkāra अहंकार) operates. Processes sensory input and generates desires and aversions.

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.4–5
Intellect Vijñānamaya-kośa विज्ञानमय कोश▾
Vijñānamaya Kośa — The Intellect Sheath

The intellect and higher mind. Contains the capacity for discrimination (viveka विवेक). Not yet the Self — even the discriminating intellect is an object in awareness.

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.6–7
Bliss Ānandamaya-kośa आनन्दमय कोश▾
Ānandamaya Kośa — The Bliss Sheath

The subtle layer of peace experienced in deep meditation or deep sleep. Closer to the Self but still an appearance — the subtlest veil before the witness is reached. Even this must be transcended.

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.8

"As long as you identify with the body-mind you are conditioned. Once you stabilize in the knowledge 'I am' unconditionally you are free."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj (1897–1981)
अस्मि
Stage II The Core Practice निसर्ग योग

Abide in "I Am"

Nisarga Yoga निसर्ग योग — The Natural State

The pivot of the entire teaching. Locate the bare sense of existing — "I Am" अस्मि — before any qualifier such as "I am this body" or "I am unhappy." This is Nisarga Yoga, the yoga of the natural state. It is not concentration or effort — it is the effortless recognition of what is always already present.

Nisargadatta identifies this "I Am" with Ishvara ईश्वर — God, the creative principle, the source of the entire universe. To abide in pure "I Am-ness" is to worship God directly, without intermediary. The universe itself is the "I Am" in manifestation.

⚠

This "I Am" is not a mantra to repeat mechanically. It is the actual, living recognition of existence prior to thought. Maharaj called it beingness — the first arising, the primordial sense of presence that spontaneously knows itself.

Stripping Away the Qualifiers

The method is simple in description and demanding in practice: notice how every thought adds a qualifier to bare existence. "I am tired" — tired is the qualifier. "I am happy" — happy is the qualifier. "I am a person" — person is the qualifier. Strip them all away. What remains is the naked "I Am" अस्मि — pure beingness, pure knowingness, Ishvara.

Compared with Ramana Maharshi's vichāra विचार — which traces the "I"-thought back to its source — Nisargadatta's method holds the "I Am" as the object of constant attention until it reveals what is prior to it. Different approaches to the same summit.

"The 'I am' is the beginning and the end of knowledge. Be attentive to the 'I am', once you understand it, you are apart from it."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
अविद्या
Stage III What Blocks the Path अविद्या

The Three Obstacles

Avidyā अविद्या — Fundamental Ignorance

Three fundamental errors sustain the illusion of separation. Each must be seen through directly — not merely understood intellectually, for intellectual understanding itself is a product of the mind and leaves the root untouched.

Obstacle A · Body Identification · देहाभिमान

Mistaking the body for the self is the foundational error. The body is an object perceived within consciousness — it is not the perceiver. It arises in awareness; awareness does not arise in it. The Chandogya Upaniṣad's छान्दोग्य "Tat Tvam Asi" तत्त्वमसि — You Are That — points to identity not with the body but with the universal ground.

Obstacle B · Mind & Concepts · संकल्प

The mind generates concepts — including spiritual concepts. Even "non-duality" and "the Absolute" are mental constructs. Every concept must ultimately be relinquished. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad's बृहदारण्यक "neti, neti" नेति नेति — not this, not this — negates all objects to arrive at the irreducible witness. Nisargadatta's method is the experiential equivalent.

Obstacle C · Sense of Doership · कर्तृत्व

The belief "I act, I choose, I achieve" sustains the illusion of a separate self. Actions happen through the body-mind; there is no independent doer standing behind them. This is the Bhagavad Gītā's भगवद्गीता teaching of actionless action — when doership dissolves, actions happen but there is no actor claiming them.

"The very idea that you are the body is ridiculous. Consciousness is experiencing its manifestation."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj, Consciousness and the Absolute
तुरीय
Stage IV States of Consciousness तुरीय

The Fourth

Turīya तुरीय — Beyond the Three States

Rooted in the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad माण्डूक्य — twelve verses, considered the most concentrated exposition of consciousness in the Vedantic canon — Nisargadatta maps experience across four dimensions. All three ordinary states come and go; awareness, the witness, persists through them all.

I
Waking
Jāgrat · जाग्रत्

The world of objects, names, and forms. The realm of the vyakti. Fully manifest.

II
Dreaming
Svapna · स्वप्न

Subtle world of mind-created imagery. Objects appear without outer stimulus.

III
Deep Sleep
Suṣupti · सुषुप्ति

Consciousness suspended — yet: "I am aware that I am unconscious."

IV
Turīya
The Fourth · तुरीय

Not a state. The unchanging awareness that witnesses and contains all three.

Nisargadatta's unique contribution: consciousness itself (which contains the three states) is still an appearance. Awareness — that which knows consciousness — is prior to all states and is the truer Self. Where Ramana Maharshi often equates turīya with the Self, Nisargadatta pushes deeper: even the witness is not the ultimate.

"The three states of waking, dreaming and sleeping are not me, and I am not in them. Consciousness is an itching rash that makes you scratch. Of course, you cannot step out of consciousness — yet, suddenly, you are free."

— I Am That
विवेक
Stage V The Radical Move विवेक

The Critical Distinction

Viveka विवेक — Consciousness vs. Pure Awareness

This is Nisargadatta's most radical and most commonly misunderstood teaching. He makes a hard distinction between two levels that are conflated in most traditions — including most of traditional Advaita Vedanta.

Consciousness चित्
Chit · "I Am-ness" · Ishvara
  • The sense of being, knowingness
  • Ishvara, the creator-principle
  • The universe itself
  • Luminous, vast, impersonal
  • Still an appearance
  • Arose; will cease at death
  • Depends on the five elements
  • Not the ultimate
Pure Awareness परब्रह्म
The Absolute · Parabrahman
  • That in which consciousness appears
  • No qualities, no "I Am"
  • Prior to being and non-being
  • Never born, never dies
  • Your true nature
  • Independent of all conditions
  • No consciousness of itself
  • The ultimate

"The entire universe exists only in consciousness (Chidākāśa), while the Jñānī has his abode in the Absolute (Paramākāśa). In the Absolute there is no consciousness of 'I am.'"

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

While traditional Advaita identifies the Self with pure consciousness (Cit चित्), Nisargadatta insists that consciousness itself — the "I Am" — is still an appearance. He arrived at this not through philosophy but through direct experience following his guru's instruction. This is "neti, neti" नेति नेति carried to its ultimate conclusion.

परब्रह्म
Stage VI The Ultimate परब्रह्म

Prior to "I Am"

Parabrahman परब्रह्म — Beyond All Appearance

Beyond consciousness, beyond the witness, beyond even the primordial sense of being — this is the Absolute: Parabrahman परब्रह्म. No quality can be attributed to it. It is neither conscious nor unconscious, neither being nor non-being. It is not a state you enter; it is what you are when all appearances subside.

The Nature of the Absolute · परब्रह्म का स्वभाव
  • Not conscious — consciousness is an appearance within it
  • Not unconscious — unconsciousness implies a lack of consciousness
  • Not being — being is an appearance, however subtle
  • Not non-being — non-being is a concept, another appearance
  • Not aware — awareness implies something to be aware of
  • Not unaware — unawareness is a state, and states come and go
  • Prior to all qualities, all states, all concepts
  • The unchanging reality beyond all duality

"The Absolute is prior to consciousness. Consciousness is the link between the Absolute and the relative."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
The Mechanism of Appearance · माया का स्वरूप

What Nisargadatta calls māyā माया is not a trick or punishment — it is simply the Absolute apparently forgetting itself through the mechanism of consciousness. The "I Am" arises spontaneously within the Absolute, and from this arising the entire phenomenal universe unfolds.

The Paradox of Teaching · उपदेश का विरोधाभास

Nisargadatta insisted that teaching is impossible at the ultimate level. Words can only point to appearances; the Absolute is beyond all pointing. Yet he taught for forty years — not to convey knowledge but to dismantle the seeker's mistaken identity. The very act of teaching, he said, is a play of consciousness — a spontaneous arising with no source and no purpose.

"I am dead already. I am not born. I have no body. I am not the mind. I have no knowledge. What is there to teach? The teacher is no more. The teaching is happening spontaneously."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

"Remain focused on the 'I am' till it goes into oblivion — then the eternal is, the Absolute is, Parabrahman is."

— Nisargadatta, compiled by Nic Higham

मुक्ति
Stage VII The Fruit मुक्ति

The Jñānī

Mukti मुक्ति — Liberation Not Attained but Recognised

The jñānī ज्ञानी appears to live normally. The body continues, words arise, activities happen. But there is no person behind it. The jñānī does not claim liberation — liberation is simply the absence of the claim to bondage. The concept of a separate self that needed liberating has been seen through.

Traditional Vedanta speaks of the jīvanmukta जीवन्मुक्त — liberated while living. Nisargadatta's jñānī goes further: not only has the person-sense dissolved, but even the sense "I Am" has been transcended. Abiding as Parabrahman — prior to consciousness itself — while appearing to live an ordinary human life.

"You are already That. The seeker is the sought. The perceiver is the perceived. There is no gap."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
ॐ
प्रेम — Often Overlooked · Central to What He Lived

Love is not selective. Desire is selective. When the centre of selfishness collapses, love for all beings arises naturally — not as a practice but as one's actual nature.

"Life is love and love is life. What keeps the body together but love? What is desire, but love of the self? What is fear but the urge to protect? And what is knowledge but the love of truth?"

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

Between wisdom — I am nothing — and love — I am everything — life flows. This love (prema प्रेम) is not an emotion arising in a person; it is the very substance of consciousness when separation is seen through.

Desire is selective, but love embraces all without distinction — in love there are no strangers. It requires no effort because it is not an achievement; it is simply what remains when the illusion of the separate self dissolves. The consciousness in you and the consciousness in me, apparently two, are really one. Their seeking of unity is love.

मोक्ष — Recognition · Not Achievement

Liberation in This Life

This is not a permanent altered state to be defended. It is recognition of what always already is — the Absolute was never bound, never born, never in question. What changed is only the mistake: the error of identification has been seen through. The search was for what was never lost. The seeker was the sought.

Nisargadatta died of throat cancer in 1981, continuing to teach from his sickbed.[1] He did not present death as a problem — the body's dissolution was simply the return of an appearance to its source. His last teachings, preserved in Prior to Consciousness, are among the most direct pointers in the literature of Advaita.

"When you know you are nothing, you are everything. That is the only thing to know."
ॐ

Parabrahman परब्रह्म
The Absolute

That which is prior to consciousness, prior to "I Am." The ultimate reality without qualities, without being or non-being. What you are when all appearances subside.

Ishvara ईश्वर
God · The Creator

Identified by Nisargadatta with the "I Am" itself. The creative principle, the source of the universe. Still an appearance on the Absolute — luminous, vast, impersonal.

Vyakti व्यक्ति
The Apparent Person

The ordinary human identified with body-mind. The starting condition of mistaken identity. The "personal" that is the prison — innocent but erroneous.

Jñānī ज्ञानी
The Knower

One who has realised the Absolute. Appears to live normally but with no person behind the living. Does not claim liberation.

Turīya तुरीय
The Fourth

Not a state but the unchanging awareness that witnesses and contains waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. The silent background of all experience.

Māyā माया
Appearance

Not a trick or punishment. The spontaneous appearance of the phenomenal universe on the Absolute. The mechanism by which the Absolute apparently forgets itself.

Nisarga Yoga निसर्ग योग
The Natural Yoga

Nisargadatta's method: abiding in the bare "I Am" without qualifiers. Effortless recognition of what is always already present.

Kośas कोश
The Five Sheaths

Annamaya, Prāṇamaya, Manomaya, Vijñānamaya, Ānandamaya. All appearances in consciousness, not the Self. Each subtler than the last.

Chidākāśa चिदाकाश
Consciousness-Space

The field of knowingness in which the entire universe exists. The "I Am-ness." Still an appearance, not the ultimate — but the subtlest appearance.

Paramākāśa परमाकाश
The Absolute Space

The abode of the Jñānī — prior to consciousness. In the Absolute there is no consciousness of "I am."

Viveka विवेक
Discrimination

The capacity to distinguish the real from the apparent, the permanent from the transient, consciousness from its contents. The essential faculty on the path.

Guṇas गुण
The Three Qualities

Sattva (clarity), Rajas (activity), Tamas (inertia) — the qualities of nature upon which consciousness depends. When they subside, the "I Am" dissolves into the Absolute.

साधना

How to Practise

A practical guide to Nisarga Yoga — the "do nothing" is the fruit; the seed is devoted attention. Here, Nisargadatta speaks directly.

I
Locate the "I Am"
"The sense 'I am' is the basis of all experience. Without it, there is no experience. Find that sense, hold on to it, and you will know yourself."
— I Am That, Chapter 7

Find the bare sense of existing — not "I am this" or "I am that" — just the raw fact of being. This is the gateway. It is always present; the task is simply to notice it before thought adds anything to it.

II
Abide Without Effort
"Just keep quiet and be aware of yourself, of your being. The knowledge 'I am' is the first step. Don't lose hold of it."
— I Am That, Chapter 12

Do not concentrate or strain. Simply rest in the recognition of "I Am." It is effortless because it is already here — you are not creating it. Any tension is a sign of adding something to the naked "I Am."

III
Watch Qualifiers Arise
"You are not what you take yourself to be. The mind is full of words and images. Stay as the witness. Watch the mind, but don't identify with it."
— Nisargadatta Maharaj, Conversations

Notice how the mind adds qualifiers: "I am tired," "I am the body." See these as overlays on bare beingness. Return, gently, to the pure "I Am" beneath them. The return is the practice — not the absence of wandering.

IV
See Through the Obstacles
"The mind creates the world. When the mind is still, the world disappears. But the 'I am' remains. Abide in that."
— I Am That, Chapter 45

When body-identification, conceptual thinking, or the sense of doership arise, do not fight them. Meet them with inquiry: "To whom does this appear?" See them as appearances in the "I Am," not as the "I Am" itself.

V
Let the "I Am" Dissolve
"What you are, prior to the 'I am,' is the Absolute. The 'I am' is the door. Go through it, and you are home."
— Nisargadatta Maharaj, final talks

Eventually, even the "I Am" is seen as an appearance. It drops away by itself — this cannot be forced. What remains cannot be described. It is not a state of bliss. It is simply what is, prior to any arising. You are That.

✦
The Timeless Understanding
"I have told you: you are the Absolute. The words my Guru gave me touched me deeply. I abided in one thing only: the words of my Guru are the truth. That is what you must do."
— Nisargadatta Maharaj, on Siddharameshwar

Nisargadatta reached that perspective through three years of devoted practice. The "do nothing" is the fruit, not the seed. The seed is: attend to the "I Am" with your whole being, until it reveals what is prior to itself. Nothing was ever done. The seeker was the sought.

"Just 'TO BE' and to remain steady with the awareness 'I AM' is the beginning and end of spiritual practice."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

"Nisargadatta Maharaj's direct pointing to the 'I Am' as the final abode offers a radical approach to self-realization that transcends traditional practices."

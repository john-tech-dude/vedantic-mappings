The Nine Dimensions

The Vedantic Christ

A Living Map of Non-Dual Realization

Bracketed numbers [1] show scriptural and Vedantic sources on hover.

This exegesis proposes that at the heart of Jesus's proclamation lies a mystical, non-dual realization indistinguishable from the core truth of Advaita Vedanta: the identity of the individual self (Atman) with the ultimate, impersonal ground of all being (Brahman). What emerges is a path to jivanmukti—not through belief in a future salvation, but through the direct knowledge that the Kingdom of God is the eternal, dimensionless reality of pure consciousness: Sat-Cit-Ananda (Being-Consciousness-Bliss), ever-present within.[1]

Sanskrit terms follow simplified Anglicization for consistency.

What follows is a systematic unfolding of this vision across nine dimensions of the Gospel, employing key Sanskrit terms to illuminate the profound convergence between the teachings of Jesus of Nazareth and the timeless wisdom of the Upanishads. This reading is not a reduction of Christianity to Hinduism, nor a dismissal of Christian theology, but a hermeneutical lens that reveals the non-dual metaphysics implicit in the Gospel narrative—a metaphysics that has been preserved in the Christian mystical tradition from Pseudo-Dionysius to Meister Eckhart, from John of the Cross to the anonymous author of The Cloud of Unknowing.

On Method and Intention

This exegesis reads the Gospels as theological texts—particularly the Gospel of John, which presents the most developed Christology—rather than as historical records. The "Jesus" spoken of here is the Christ of faith, the symbolic and archetypal figure through whom the evangelists expressed their mystical understanding. This follows the hermeneutical distinction made by Swami Vivekananda between the "Christ of the Gospels" and the "Jesus of history." This reading treats the Gospel narrative as presented—neither affirming nor denying its historicity—and interprets its events as symbolic of the universal path of ego-dissolution.

It is vital to state plainly: this is one lens among many. Christian theology is a vast forest, and Advaita Vedanta is one particular light by which to walk through it. The nine dimensions presented here are aspects of a single realization, not sequential stages. In Advaita, the Self is never attained; only the ignorance of it is removed. Each section illuminates a facet of the same jewel.

The Native Non-Dualism of the Christian Tradition

Christianity already contains a robust apophatic ("negative") mysticism. These voices speak a language of radical non-duality that resonates with Advaita Vedanta. The Advaitic lens offered here does not impose foreign concepts but makes explicit what is implicit in the Christian mystical heritage.

Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite, in The Mystical Theology, writes of a "darkness beyond light" where all affirmations and negations fall away. He speaks of God as beyond being, beyond unity, beyond all names—a silence that mirrors the neti neti (not this, not that) of the Upanishads. The soul enters this darkness by stripping away every conceptual garment, arriving at a "unknowing" that exceeds knowledge.

Meister Eckhart declares: "God's ground and my ground is one ground." He distinguishes between the "God" of attributes (personal, approachable) and the "Godhead" beyond all attributes—a distinction that parallels Saguna Brahman and Nirguna Brahman. Eckhart's sermons on the "birth of God in the soul" describe a non-dual event in which creature and Creator collapse in the ground of being.

John of the Cross, in the Dark Night of the Soul, maps the passage through utter emptiness—nada, nada, nada (nothing, nothing, nothing)—into luminous union with the Beloved. His "dark night" is not depression; it is the necessary dissolution of the ego's grasping, a purgation that strips the soul of its attachments. This is not far from the practice of Neti-Neti.

The anonymous author of The Cloud of Unknowing instructs the seeker to pierce the cloud with a "naked, blind, urgent longing"—a single syllable of love that bypasses the discursive mind. This is a practical manual for apophatic prayer, a Christian mantra recognizable to any practitioner of Bhakti Yoga.

These are not anomalies at the margins of Christianity. They are central threads in a continuous tradition. The Advaitic reading that follows listens carefully to what these mystics heard in the Gospel.

The Gospel Narrative

The Life of Christ

Chronological Sequence of Sacred Events

The following traces the Gospel narrative as sacred story, without entering the historical-critical debate about the precise dating or authenticity of individual events. These moments are presented as symbolic archetypal events.

c. 4 BCE

Birth in Bethlehem. Lineage traced to King David. The appearance of the divine in particular flesh. [a]

c. 6-4 BCE

Flight to Egypt. Return to Nazareth after Herod's death. The protection of the nascent Self. [b]

c. 12 CE

Age twelve. Debating in the Temple: "Did you not know I must be about my Father's business?" The first stirring of recognition. [c]

c. 26-29 CE

Age thirty. Baptism in the Jordan. The Holy Spirit descends "like a dove." Voice from heaven: "This is my beloved Son." The awakening of the I AM. [d]

c. 26-30 CE

Forty days in the wilderness. Temptation by Satan. Confrontation with Ahamkara. Overcoming the threefold temptation: survival, power, and validation. [e]

c. 26-30 CE

Ministry in Galilee. Calls disciples. Proclaims: "The Kingdom of God is at hand." Healings and exorcisms manifest the awakened state. [f]

c. 30 CE

The Transfiguration. Radiant with light. Moses and Elijah appear. A foretaste of the Sukshma Sharira. [g]

c. 30 CE

Triumphal entry into Jerusalem. Cleanses the Temple. Final confrontation with ego-structure of institutional religion. [h]

c. 30 CE

The Last Supper. Washing of feet. Agony in Gethsemane. The final surrender of separate will: "Not my will, but thine." [i]

c. 30 CE

Arrest, trial, crucifixion. "Father, forgive them."[j] Death of the Son of Man. Dissolution of Ahamkara.

c. 30 CE

The Resurrection. Empty tomb. Appearances to disciples. The revelation that the Atman was never born and never dies. [k]

c. 30 CE

The Ascension. Return to the Father. Recognition that Christ-consciousness was never separate. Tat Tvam Asi. [l]

Dimension I

The Father as Nirguna Brahman

The Unmanifest Ground · Beyond All Attributes

In the Gospel of John, "the Father" represents ultimate reality—the unconditioned, unmanifest ground of all being. This corresponds precisely to Nirguna Brahman: the formless, attributeless absolute that transcends all description.

Jesus consistently subordinates himself to the Father: "The Father is greater than I"[1]; "I can do nothing of myself"[2]; "The words that I speak to you I do not speak from myself"[3]. This is not ordinary humility. It is metaphysical truth: the Christ (awakened consciousness) is not the source but the manifestation of the unmanifest Father.

The Father as Silence and Void

Nirguna Brahman is described in apophatic terms—as neti neti (not this, not that)—because it cannot be grasped by the mind. Similarly, the Father in John is never directly described. Jesus points to the Father but does not explain the Father. The Father is the silence behind the words, the void from which creation emerges.

"God is Spirit"[20]. In Hebrew, ruach means both "spirit" and "wind/breath." The Father is the breath animating all forms, yet has no form itself.

— John 4:24

The distinction between Father and Son is the distinction between unmanifest and manifest, between absolute and its reflection. In Kashmir Shaivism, this is the distinction between Shiva and Shakti. In Advaita, it is the distinction between Nirguna Brahman and Saguna Brahman.

"

The eye through which I see God is the same eye through which God sees me.

— Meister Eckhart

Dimension II

The Son of Man as the Ego

The Body Is the Cross · Toward Dissolution

The title "Son of Man" appears frequently in the Gospels, particularly in Jesus's sayings. In the Advaitic reading, "Son of Man" refers to the Ahamkara—the egoic structure that creates the illusion of separate existence.

Jesus speaks of the Son of Man in the third person, as if referring to another: "The Son of Man must suffer many things"[4]; "The Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head"[5]. Jesus explicitly identifies the Son of Man with suffering and death: "The Son of Man must undergo great suffering, and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests, and the scribes, and be killed, and after three days rise again"[4]. The ego is precisely that which must be rejected, killed, and transcended. This linguistic distancing is significant. The awakened consciousness speaks about the ego as if it were another—because from the perspective of the Self, the ego is indeed "other," a temporary construct to be transcended.

The Body as the Cross

In the most radical interpretation, the physical body is the cross. It is the instrument of suffering and limitation through which the ego must die. Jesus's statement "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross daily"[6] declares that the cross is not an external instrument but the body-mind complex itself.

Unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, it remains just a single grain; but if it dies, it bears much fruit.

— John 12:24

In the Vedantic framework, the body-mind exists in three layers: Sthula Sharira (gross body), Sukshma Sharira (subtle body), and Karana Sharira (causal body). These are the three layers of the cross that must be transcended.

Dimension III

I AM—The Atman as Universal Self

The Recognition of Being · Eternal Consciousness

The most profound revelation in John is Jesus's use of the divine name "I AM" (Greek: Ego eimi). In the Hebrew Bible, "I AM" is the name God reveals to Moses (Exodus 3:14). In Sanskrit, this corresponds to Atman—pure consciousness, the essence of every being.

When Jesus says "I AM the bread of life"[16], "I AM the light of the world"[17], "I AM the resurrection and the life"[18], he is declaring the identity of his consciousness with the absolute—Aham Brahmasmi (I am Brahman).

Before Abraham Was, I AM

The most radical statement is "Before Abraham was, I AM"[7]. This is not a claim to pre-existence as an individual soul. It is a declaration of the timelessness of pure consciousness. The Self is not born and does not die; it exists eternally, prior to all temporal forms.

The Self is not this, not that. It is not born, it does not die. It is not caused, it does not cause. It is the witness of all that appears and disappears.

— Mandukya Upanishad 7

This I AM is not the egoic "I" that says "I am hungry" or "I am angry." That is Chidabhasa—consciousness reflected in the mind. The true I AM is Sakshi Chaitanya—witnessing consciousness, untouched by what it witnesses.

"The 'I am' is the first expression of the Absolute, the first thought, the first word."

— Sri Nisargadatta Maharaj · I Am That

Dimension IV

The Kingdom Within

The Spatial Metaphor · Non-Dual Presence

The central proclamation of Jesus is "The Kingdom of God is at hand"[8]. In Luke, this is clarified: "The Kingdom of God is within you"[9]. This is the core of Jesus's teaching: the Kingdom is not a future event, not a geographical location, not an external political order. It is the ever-present reality of pure consciousness.

It is not a castle to be stormed. It is a seed already planted. It is not a reward for the righteous; it is the ground of righteousness itself.

In Vedanta, this is the Dahara Akasha—the "space in the heart" where the Self dwells. The Upanishads speak of this inner space as "smaller than the smallest, larger than the largest" (Katha Upanishad 1.2.20). It is the dimensionless point from which all dimensions emerge.

The Kingdom as Consciousness

The Kingdom is not a place but a state of consciousness. It is the recognition that what you seek is already here. Jesus's parables—the mustard seed, the hidden treasure, the pearl of great price—all point to this: the Kingdom is hidden in plain sight, available to those who have eyes to see.

The Kingdom of God is not coming with signs to be observed; nor will they say, 'Look, here it is!' or 'There it is!' For, in fact, the Kingdom of God is within you.

— Luke 17:20-21

This is the Sahaja state—the natural state of awareness that is always present but overlooked due to identification with the mind. The Kingdom is the background of all experience, the screen on which all experiences appear.

Dimension V

The Cross as Ahamkara's Dissolution

Crucifixion as Ego-Death · The Final Surrender

The crucifixion is the central event of the Christian narrative. In the Advaitic reading, it represents ego-death—the dissolution of Ahamkara. Jesus's final words from the cross embody the final surrender of ego-will: "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit"[10]; "It is finished"[11].

This is an active offering of the ego-structure back to its source. In Bhakti Yoga, this is Prapatti—the total surrender of the separate self. In Jnana Yoga, it is Neti-Neti—the negation of all that is not the Self.

The Threefold Death

The crucifixion represents three deaths simultaneously:

I
Death of the Physical
Sthula Sharira
The gross body — earth, water, fire, air, ether [†]. Offered back to the five elements. A surrender of form.
II
Death of the Mental
Sukshma Sharira
The subtle body — thought, emotion, memory, will. The ceasing of inner chatter. The mind dissolves into stillness [‡].
III
Death of Ignorance
Karana Sharira
The causal seed — the root belief in separation. Not a death, but a waking up. The rope‑snake [§] vanishes when light arrives. Only the Self remains.

Unlike the gross and subtle bodies, the causal body does not literally die—it is recognized as never having existed separate from the Self.

Thus the threefold "death" is metaphorical for gross dissolution, subtle quieting, and causal invalidation – only the Self remains.

The body dies, the mind dissolves, the Self remains. What dies never was real; what remains never dies.

— Ashtavakra Gita 2.1

Dimension VI

The Holy Spirit as Shakti

The Divine Power · The Creative Force

The Holy Spirit in Christian theology is the third person of the Trinity, sent by the Father and Son to dwell in believers. In Advaita, the Holy Spirit corresponds to Shakti—the dynamic, creative power of consciousness that manifests the universe.

Shakti is not separate from Shiva; they are one reality appearing as two for the sake of manifestation. Similarly, the Holy Spirit is not separate from the Father and Son; they are one Godhead appearing as three for revelation.

The Spirit as Breath and Fire

The Holy Spirit is described as wind/breath (ruach in Hebrew, pneuma in Greek) and as fire—both metaphors for the life force that animates all forms. In yoga, this is Prana. In Tantra, this is Kundalini. In Vedanta, this is Chit-Shakti.

The Spirit blows where it wills. You hear its sound but cannot tell where it comes from or where it is going. So it is with everyone born of the Spirit.

— John 3:8

The descent of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2) is the awakening of this divine power within the community. This corresponds to Kundalini awakening in yoga—the rising of dormant spiritual energy, purifying and illuminating the entire being.

Dimension VII

The Transfiguration

The Glorified Body · The Body of Light

The Transfiguration is one of the most mystical events in the Gospels. Jesus takes three disciples up a mountain, where his appearance becomes radiant: "His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became dazzling white"[12]. Moses and Elijah appear and converse with him. A voice from the cloud says, "This is my beloved Son; listen to him."

In the Advaitic reading, this is the revelation of the Sukshma Sharira—the body of light that is the true form of the awakened being. The physical body is a densification of this subtle body, which is itself a densification of the causal body, which is a limitation of the absolute Self.

The Body as Light

When the ego dissolves, the subtle body becomes purified and luminous. This is the Jyotir Sharira—the body made of light. The appearance of Moses and Elijah represents the continuity of the awakened lineage across time, and is not arbitrary. Moses, the giver of the Law (Torah), embodies Karma Yoga – the path of selfless action and right conduct, the framework of dharma. Elijah, the zealous prophet who challenges kings and calls down fire from heaven, embodies Bhakti Yoga – the path of passionate love for God, utter surrender, and fiery devotion. Jesus, who speaks "I AM" and declares "before Abraham was, I AM," embodies Jnana Yoga – the direct path of knowledge, discrimination, and identity with the absolute. The Transfiguration reveals that these three paths are not competitors but convergences. Moses and Elijah converse with Jesus because action and devotion culminate in knowledge. All three are transfigured together in the light of the Self.

Dimension VIII

Jnana Yoga—The Path of Knowledge

The Way of Discrimination · Viveka

The core of Jesus's teaching is Viveka—discrimination between the real and the unreal, the eternal and the transient, the Self and the not-Self. This is the essence of Jnana Yoga.

Jesus repeatedly uses contrast to teach discrimination: "Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth... but store up for yourselves treasures in heaven"[13]; "What does it profit a person to gain the whole world but lose their soul?"[14].

The Teaching Through Parables

Jesus's parables are Upakatha—teaching stories that point beyond themselves to deeper truths. The parable of the sower, the parable of the talents, the parable of the prodigal son—all are designed to shock the listener into recognizing the illusory nature of ordinary perception.

The Kingdom of heaven is like a mustard seed that someone took and sowed in his field; it is the smallest of all seeds, but when it has grown it is the greatest of shrubs.

— Matthew 13:31-32

This parable teaches that the Kingdom (the Self) appears small and insignificant from the ego's perspective, yet contains infinite potential when recognized. The seed contains the tree; the Self contains the universe. This is the Pinda-Brahmanda [‖] teaching—the body (microcosm) contains the universe (macrocosm).

Dimension IX

Crucifixion, Resurrection, Ascension

The Threefold Dissolution · Tat Tvam Asi

The final events of Jesus's life—crucifixion, resurrection, and ascension—represent the threefold process of ego-dissolution and Self-realization. This is the Mahavakya "Tat Tvam Asi" (Thou Art That) enacted in narrative form.

Crucifixion: The Death of the Son of Man

Crucifixion is the death of the ego—the Ahamkara that claims "I am this body, this mind, this history." When Jesus says "It is finished," he declares the end of the egoic project. The separate self has died; what remains is the pure Self.

Resurrection: The Revelation of the Atman

Resurrection is the revelation that what died never was the true Self. The Atman is immortal, eternal, untouched by birth and death. The empty tomb is the Sunya—the void from which all forms emerge and to which they return.

Ascension: The Recognition of Tat Tvam Asi

Ascension is the final recognition that the Christ (awakened consciousness) was never separate from the Father (the absolute). "I and the Father are one"[15]. This is Tat Tvam Asi—the realization that the individual and the absolute are one reality, appearing as two.

You are not the body, you are not the mind. You are the witness of the body and the mind. You are the Self, eternal and free.

— Ashtavakra Gita 1.3

The ascension is not a journey to another place. It is the recognition that there is nowhere to go—for the Self is already everywhere. The Christ consciousness that ascended is the consciousness that was never born. The Father to whom he returns is the source from which he never departed.

"

I and the Father are one.

— John 10:30 · "Tat Tvam Asi" — Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7

Postlude

The Necessary Friction

Where the Map Meets Irreducible Difference – and Why That Matters

Every hermeneutical lens clarifies and obscures. The Advaitic reading offered here is powerful — it illuminates structures in the Gospel that Christian theology has sometimes neglected, particularly the direct path of ego-dissolution and the identity of the seeker with the sought. But no reading exhausts its text. The Christian tradition is not Advaita in disguise; it is another living wisdom, with its own irreducible grammar.

The following differences are not refutations of the non-dual reading. They are boundary conditions — places where the map must fold, where the traveler must walk without translation. To pretend these differences do not exist would be bad hermeneutics. To treat them as fatal objections would be bad philosophy.

The Trinity as Relational Ontology

Advaita's absolute is nirguna, attributeless, beyond relationship. The Christian Trinity is communion — three hypostases whose unity is their mutual indwelling (perichoresis). These are not the same.

Yet: the Christian mystic (Eckhart, Ruusbroec) often speaks of the "Godhead" beyond the persons, and the Advaitin's saguṇa brahman (the absolute with attributes) can accommodate love as the first expression of being. The friction here is real, but it may be the friction between two languages describing a silence that exceeds both.

The Scandal of Particularity

The Incarnation asserts that the infinite is fully present in this body, this time, this colonized Jew under Tiberius. Advaita universalizes: the Self is equally present in all.

But universalism without particularity evaporates into abstraction. Christianity's insistence on the particular is a guardrail against the spiritual bypass that says "all is one" while ignoring actual suffering. The Advaitic reading can absorb this: the particular Jesus is the upādhi (limiting adjunct) through which the formless Self appears in history. But the Christian insists the particular is not merely an appearance — it is the definitive revelation. That is a genuine fork in the road.

The Resurrection as Bodily and Communal

The Gospels do not describe a purely psychological awakening. They depict an empty tomb, a body that eats fish, wounds that can be touched. The resurrection is not (only) the disclosure of the Atman's immortality; it is the vindication of this flesh and the reconstitution of this community.

Advaita has no equivalent to the communal, eschatological resurrection. The jivanmukta's body falls away at death; there is no "new creation." This is a deep difference. Yet one could say: the resurrection narrative is the Christian way of saying that liberation is not escape from the world but transfiguration of it. That is a different metaphor — but perhaps pointing to the same inexpressible ground.

The Cross as Vicarious Suffering

Ego-death is the dissolution of the separate self. Vicarious suffering is one who has already died to self entering into the pain of another. The Christian cross is not merely my own ego dying; it is the recognition that the ego's structure is shared — that my liberation is bound up with yours.

Advaita tends toward the solitary path (though not exclusively — see loka-saṃgraha, the preservation of the world). The cross introduces an ethical and relational dimension that the non-dual reading must not flatten. Perhaps this is where the Holy Spirit as shakti becomes most relevant: the power that descends at Pentecost is not private realization but collective transformation.

On the Integrity of This Reading

The nine dimensions above are not invalidated by these differences. They are one valid lens — a lens that Christian mystics themselves have used, often without Sanskrit vocabulary. The postlude is not a retraction. It is a reminder that every map is partial, and that the territory of God exceeds every cartography.

To read Jesus as a non-dual teacher is permissible. To claim that is all he is would be reduction. The Vedantic Messiah stands alongside the Christ of Chalcedon, the liberator of liberation theology, the bridegroom of the Song of Songs. These are not enemies. They are facets of a diamond too brilliant for any single eye.

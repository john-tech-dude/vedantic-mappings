Crucifixion · Resurrection · Ascension

The Threefold Dissolution · Tat Tvam Asi

Crucifixion is the death of the ego through surrender or inquiry. It is not a historical execution but the universal spiritual process by which the false self is dismantled. The nails are not iron spikes but the piercing questions of self-inquiry: Who am I? The crown of thorns is the ego's own thoughts — the vrittis that prick and torment the mind. The spear in the side is the final penetration of the heart, the dissolution of even the subtlest sense of personal existence.

The cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"[1] is the darkest moment of ego-death — the neti neti carried to its absolute limit. Even the sense of divine presence vanishes. The jnani stands alone in the Absolute, beyond God and soul, beyond consciousness itself. This is the "dark night of the soul" that John of the Cross described — but it is darker than any night, for even the concept of "soul" has been surrendered. There is no one left to experience the dark.

The Three Days · Three States to Turiya

The three days between crucifixion and resurrection map onto the three states of consciousness that must be transcended before turiya — the silent Self — is realized.

And then — resurrection is the revelation of the true Self (Atman/Brahman), the "Son of God" — eternal, undivided. What remains when the ego dies? Not nothing, but the everything that was always already the case. The "Son of God" is not a person who returns from death but the recognition that the Self was never born and never dies. The empty tomb is the symbol of the body's transparency to the eternal. The resurrection appearances are maya continuing to instruct the disciples — the play of consciousness teaching itself to itself through form.

The Ascension is not a bodily levitation into the sky but the final dissolution of the subtle body (sukshma-sarira) and causal body (karana-sarira) into the Absolute. The "cloud" that receives Jesus is the avyakta, the unmanifest, the unqualified Brahman beyond all form. The disciples "looking intently up into the sky"[2] represent the mind still seeking the Self in some realm or dimension — but the Self is not in the sky, nor on earth, nor anywhere. The two men in white who appear and ask, "Why do you stand here looking into the sky?" are the teaching itself: stop looking elsewhere. The Self you seek is the very awareness that is looking.

Hermeneutical Note

This Advaitic reading of the Passion and Resurrection is a hermeneutical lens, not a claim about historical events. The Christian tradition maintains the resurrection as a transformation of the body — something Advaita has no category for — and as the vindication of Jesus's person and mission by the Father. The Advaitic reading illuminates the interior dimension of these events: the death of the ego and the revelation of the Self that was never absent. These are complementary, not contradictory, perspectives. The Christian mystic and the Vedantin are looking at the same mountain from different valleys.

Gaudapada's ajata vada (the doctrine of non-creation) states that from the highest standpoint, the world and the jiva never arose at all. The Self was never born and therefore never dies. The resurrection, read through this lens, reveals that what is eternal in Jesus — the Atman — was never touched by death. The crucifixion was the death of the ego; the resurrection was the recognition that the Self was never absent. This is why Jesus says "I am the resurrection and the life"[3] — not "I will be resurrected" but "I am resurrection" — the eternal present tense of the Self, beyond birth and death.

The resurrection appearances have a peculiar quality: Jesus is not immediately recognized. Mary Magdalene thinks he is the gardener[4]. The disciples on the road to Emmaus walk with him for hours without recognizing him[5]. This is because the Self cannot be recognized by the mind that seeks a form. The Self is formless; it can only be recognized as one's own being. When Mary hears her name spoken, she recognizes — not a person, but the Self that calls to itself. When the disciples break bread with the stranger, they recognize — not a body, but the presence that pervades all forms. The recognition of the Self is always a recognition from the Self; it cannot be achieved by the ego.

The Great Declaration Tat Tvam Asi — That Thou Art Love as the Substance of Being

Tat Tvam Asi

The Supreme Identity · That Thou Art

The entire mystical Vedanta of Jesus is summed up in his great prayer for the disciples: "That they all may be one; as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be one in us... I in them, and you in me — that they may be made perfect in one"[6]. This is the ultimate vision of Advaita: not a unity of separate souls standing side by side, but the absolute non-difference of Atman and Brahman, where the illusion of many is extinguished in the One without a second.

Jesus the jnani stands as a mirror. He does not ask for worship of his person; he points to the Self. His final message is a profound mahavakya: What I AM, you are. The Kingdom is already attained, the Father is already your own Self. "Be still, and know."[7]

The Three-Stage Hierarchy of Consciousness

Jesus's teaching reveals a hierarchy of consciousness — but the path to the Absolute is not ascent through the levels, but radical self-emptying (kenosis) that dissolves the separate self entirely.

When read through the lens of the mystical tradition rather than institutional dogma, the teaching of Jesus reveals a clear hierarchy of consciousness:

  1. The Individual Self (Psyche) — The person who identifies with body, mind, social role, and separateness. "Who do people say that I am?" (Mark 8:27). This is the level of ordinary existence, the ego-bound life, the "old self" that must "die daily" (1 Cor 15:31).
  2. The Christ Consciousness (Logos / Son) — The realization that "I and the Father are one" (John 10:30). Here, the separate self dissolves into union with the divine source. As the Gospel of John frames it: "The Father is in me, and I in the Father" (John 10:38). This is the level of mystical union, where one no longer acts from personal will but from divine will — "I only do what the Father shows me" (John 5:19).
  3. The Divine Ground (Abba / The Absolute) — The ultimate reality that is the source of the Father-Son relationship, yet transcends even the names "Father" and "Son." This is the apophatic God — the God beyond God, the "darkness beyond understanding" of Pseudo-Dionysius, the "ground of being" of Meister Eckhart. In Orthodox theology, this is the divine ousia (essence) that remains forever unknowable, while the energeiai (energies) are what we can participate in.

What makes Jesus's teaching unique is that the path to the Absolute is not ascent but descent — not self-realization but self-emptying. The Greek term kenosis (emptying) from Philippians 2:7 captures this: "He emptied himself, taking the form of a slave."

This is not a diminishment of divinity but a radical paradox: the Absolute expresses itself by voluntarily laying aside the very attributes that define it. As the Church Fathers understood, Christ "was greater than himself and less than himself simultaneously" — the infinite taking on finitude, the eternal entering time, the unconditioned accepting conditions.

The Kenotic Trajectory · The Path of Self-Emptying

Jesus's life traces a trajectory of ever-deepening self-emptying — from the Incarnation through the Cross, each station represents a further surrender of the separate self.

Jesus's own life embodies this trajectory: Birth in poverty — the Word becomes flesh not in a palace but a stable, emptying itself of heavenly glory. Baptism — "He must increase, I must decrease" (John 3:30) — the surrender of John the Baptist prefigures the greater surrender of Christ. Temptation in the wilderness — refusing to use divine power for personal gain, choosing hunger over miraculous bread. Gethsemane — "Not my will, but yours be done" (Luke 22:42) — the complete surrender of personal will. The Cross — the ultimate kenosis: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34) — even the sense of divine presence is emptied.

Love as the Substance of Being

Agape · Prema · The Self's Natural Expression

The ultimate identity (tat tvam asi) is not a cold philosophical abstraction. It is love (agape). In the Christian tradition, the love that flows between Father and Son is the Holy Spirit — the very substance of divine life. This corresponds to the Vedantic understanding that the prema (love) that pervades the universe is the very rasa (essence) of Brahman. The recognition of non-duality is not the end of love but the beginning of true love — love no longer contaminated by the ego's demand for something in return.

When Jesus says "Love one another as I have loved you"[8], he is pointing to the love that flows from the Self, not the love that flows from the ego. Egoic love is always transactional: it gives in order to receive. Self-love (atma-prema) is unconditional because the Self is complete in itself. It needs nothing. When the ego dissolves, what remains is the natural, spontaneous love that is the very substance of being. This is the lila of the universe — not a mechanical process but a love-song sung by existence to itself.

The cross is the final act of love — the surrender of the ego for the sake of the Self. The resurrection is the revelation that this love was never absent; it was the very ground of the crucifixion itself. The One who dies and the One who rises are the same Self, playing all roles for the sake of its own delight. This is the supreme mahavakya of the Vedantic Messiah: Tat Tvam Asi — That Thou Art. Not "you are like God" or "you will become God" but you are God, have always been, will always be. The game of separation was a divine play; the recognition of unity is the return home.

The Native Term

Θέωσις · God Became Man That Man Might Become God

This page replaces the previous "Tat Tvam Asi" because theosis is the proper Christian term for what other traditions call liberation, enlightenment, or realization. But it is not a translation. It is a different vision that overlaps with Advaita at many points and diverges at the deepest ones. This page is an exploration of both convergence and divergence — not a harmonization.

Athanasius, in De Incarnatione (c. 318 CE), gives the classic formula: "He became man that we might become god." This is not a metaphor. It is the theological engine of the Christian East — the conviction that the incarnation is not merely a rescue mission but the inauguration of a new kind of existence: humanity deified.

Gregory of Nyssa names the soul's movement into God epektasis — not a terminal arrival but a perpetual expansion into the infinite. There is no terminal state, only endless growth. The Beatitudes are not a ladder to be climbed and left behind; they are a spiral that deepens without end.

Maximus Confessor teaches that the logoi of all things are contained in the Logos. Deification is the realization of the divine intention in every creature — the fulfillment of what each thing was made to be. This is not escape from creation but its transfiguration.

Gregory Palamas articulates the metaphysical architecture that makes theosis possible without absorption: the distinction between divine ousia (essence) and energeiai (energies). We participate in God's energies — the uncreated light of Tabor — without becoming God's essence. This is the bridge between Advaita's absorption and Christianity's communion.

Meister Eckhart: "The eye with which I see God is the same eye with which God sees me." Theosis as the dissolution of the boundary between seer and seen — while preserving both. The eye remains an eye; the seeing remains seeing. But they are one.

The Shift

This page moves from the hermeneutical and apophatic into the declarative heart of the matter: the identity of the human person with the divine. But this is not Advaita's "Tat Tvam Asi" imposed on Christianity. It is Christianity's native theosis — participation rather than absorption, communion rather than dissolution, endless growth rather than terminal realization.

Theosis vs. Moksha — Two Mountains, One Sky

Theosis and moksha approach the same silence from different valleys. They share the sky but not the path. Theosis preserves relationship; moksha dissolves it. These are not errors to be corrected — they are two ways of standing before the mystery.

The Kenotic Path Crucifixion as Ego-Death The Descent of the Absolute

Crucifixion · Resurrection · Ascension

The Threefold Movement of Theosis

Crucifixion is the death of the ego — the dissolution of ahaṃkāra. The nails are the piercing questions of self-inquiry; the crown of thorns is the ego's own thoughts; the spear is the final penetration of the heart. The cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"[1] is neti-neti carried to its absolute limit. Even the sense of divine presence vanishes.

But theosis adds what Advaita cannot: the cross as vicarious suffering, as solidarity with the victim, as God entering into another's pain. The crucifixion is not only my ego dying; it is kenosis — the Absolute emptying itself into the ego's condition. The direction is reversed. In Advaita, I dissolve into Brahman. In theosis, Brahman descends into my dissolution. These are not the same movement.

Kenosis vs. Dissolution

In Advaita, I dissolve into Brahman. In theosis, Brahman descends into my dissolution. These are not the same movement. The cross is not my ego's achievement; it is God's gift. The resurrection is not my realization; it is God's vindication of this particular body, this particular history, this particular Jesus of Nazareth. The empty tomb is not maya's transparency; it is the promise that matter itself will be transfigured. The resurrection appearances — Jesus eating fish, showing wounds, being touched — insist that the body is real, beloved, and will be redeemed.

Resurrection is the vindication of this particular body, this particular history. The empty tomb is not a symbol of the Self's immortality; it is the promise that this flesh, this history, this person will be raised. The resurrection appearances — Jesus eating fish, showing wounds, being touched — are not "maya continuing to instruct"; they are the insistence that the body is real, beloved, and will be redeemed. This is the scandal of the incarnation: not that the Self is immortal, but that this body — wounded, broken, mortal — is raised to eternal life.

Ascension is not the dissolution of subtle and causal bodies into the Absolute; it is the pledge of return. "This same Jesus, who has been taken from you into heaven, will come back in the same way you have seen him go"[2]. Theosis is eschatological; moksha is not. The journey is not one of departure but of return. The "cloud" that receives Jesus is not the unmanifest; it is the presence of the Father, and it is from this presence that Jesus will come again. The disciples "looking intently up into the sky" are not seeking the Self in some realm; they are waiting for the One who will return.

The Threefold Movement of Theosis

Theosis is a threefold movement: descent (kenosis), vindication (resurrection), and pledge (ascension). Unlike moksha's terminal realization, theosis is eschatological — a journey that ends in return, not departure.

ΠΕΡΙΧΩΡΗΣΙΣ
The Great Declaration Theosis as Participation Perichoresis · The Trinitarian Dance

The Great Declaration

"That they may be one as we are one."

Jesus prays in John 17:21-23[3]: "That they all may be one; as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be one in us... I in them, and you in me — that they may be made perfect in one."

This is not Tat Tvam Asi. Tat Tvam Asi is identity: "Thou art That." Theosis is participation: "That dwells in thee, and thou in That, and the twain are one yet remain twain." This is perichoresis — the mutual indwelling of Father, Son, and Spirit — and the human person is drawn into this dance without being dissolved.

The Greek Fathers on this distinction: Gregory of Nyssa teaches that the soul becomes what it contemplates, but it never becomes God's essence. It participates in the energeiai (energies), not the ousia (essence). Palamas articulates the distinction: the uncreated light of Tabor is the energies, accessible to the creature, while the essence remains forever beyond. This is the metaphysical ground that makes theosis possible — and it is absent from Advaita, where there is no distinction between essence and manifestation.

Identity vs. Participation

"I and the Father are one" (John 10:30) — but in the same chapter, Jesus quotes Psalm 82:6: "I said, you are gods." Theosis is not the privilege of Jesus alone; it is the vocation of every human person. But it is vocation, not recognition. It is called, not realized. It is gift, not discovery. This is the difference between a birthright and an inheritance — one is given, the other is received. Theosis is participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4), not identity with it. The creature remains creature even as it becomes god by grace.

Paul's "in Christ" formula — the most frequent phrase in his letters — is not "I am Christ" (identity) but "in Christ" (participation). Galatians 2:20[4]: "I have been crucified with Christ; it is no longer I who live, but Christ lives in me." This is theosis as substitution and indwelling, not dissolution. The "I" remains — but it is Christ's "I" living it. This is utterly foreign to Advaita, where the "I" must die entirely.

Essence and Energies — The Palamite Architecture

The Palamite distinction between essence (ousia) and energies (energeiai) makes theosis possible without absorption. We participate in God's energies — the uncreated light — without becoming God's essence. This is the metaphysical ground that Advaita lacks.

Theosis is Not Tat Tvam Asi

It is something else. Theosis preserves what Advaita dissolves: relationship, particularity, embodiment, and eschatological hope. The Father remains Father; the Son remains Son; the Spirit remains Spirit. And the human person — drawn into this dance — remains a person even as it becomes god by grace.

John 17:21

"That they all may be one; as you, Father, are in me, and I in you."

Perichoresis · Mutual Indwelling

2 Peter 1:4

"You may become partakers of the divine nature."

Love as the Substance of Theosis

Agape · Not Knowledge but Love

In Advaita, jñāna (knowledge) is the supreme path. In theosis, agape is the supreme path. The human person becomes god by loving, not by knowing. This is the fundamental difference between the two traditions.

For Advaita, love is māyā — a beautiful illusion that must be transcended. The love between creatures is a projection of the non-dual Self, a play of consciousness that ultimately must be seen through. For theosis, love is the very life of God — the energy that deifies, the power that transforms, the essence that sustains. The cross is not knowledge; it is love. The resurrection is not insight; it is love's victory over death. Theosis is not realized; it is loved into being.

Jñāna vs. Agape

"If I have all knowledge but have not love, I am nothing" (1 Cor 13:2). For Advaita, jñāna (knowledge) is the supreme path; for theosis, agape (love) is the supreme path. The cross is not knowledge; it is love. The resurrection is not insight; it is love's victory over death. Theosis is not realized; it is loved into being. The human person becomes god by loving, not by knowing. This is the deepest divergence between the two traditions — and the most beautiful.

"Love one another as I have loved you"[5]. This is not a command to imitate Jesus's love from a distance. It is an invitation to participate in the love that is the substance of divine life. The love with which Jesus loves is the love of the Father for the Son, the love that is the Holy Spirit. To love "as I have loved" is to be drawn into the Trinitarian perichoresis — to become a participant in the divine life itself.

The cross is the final act of love — not the Self's play with itself, but the Lover entering into the beloved's condition, dying the beloved's death, rising as the beloved's hope. This is solidarity, not solipsism. The cross is God's solidarity with the victim, not the dissolution of the victim's pain into illusion. The resurrection is the promise that suffering will be healed, not transcended.

Two Visions of Love — Advaita and Theosis

Advaita and theosis flow toward the same silence, but they carry different waters. Advaita dissolves love into knowledge; theosis transforms knowledge into love. The silence of theosis is not empty — it is full of the love that spoke the world, died on the cross, and rises still.

What Resists the Map

Six Tensions Between Theosis and Moksha

These differences are not errors to be corrected. They are two ways of standing before the mystery. Advaita dissolves the questioner into the question. Theosis transforms the questioner through the question. Both arrive at silence. But the silence of theosis is not empty; it is full — full of the love that spoke the world, died on the cross, and rises still in every heart that opens to it. Theosis is not Tat Tvam Asi. It is something else. Whether it is something more — that is for the silence to decide.

1. Terminal vs. Endless

Moksha as terminal realization; theosis as epektasis — perpetual growth into the infinite. Gregory of Nyssa's epektasis means the soul never arrives; it is always being drawn deeper into the divine darkness. Moksha is the end of seeking; theosis is seeking without end. One is a destination; the other is a pilgrimage that never ends.

2. Absorption vs. Participation

Aham Brahmasmi as identity; theosis as perichoresis — mutual indwelling preserving distinction. In Advaita, the jiva dissolves into Brahman. In theosis, the creature participates in the divine nature while remaining creature. The "I" is not annihilated but transformed, not dissolved but deified.

3. Dissolution vs. Transformation

Moksha as liberation FROM the world; theosis as transfiguration OF the world. The resurrection of the body, the new earth, the eschatological kingdom — these are not metaphors. Theosis redeems matter; moksha transcends it. One escapes the wheel; the other dances on it.

4. Solitary vs. Ecclesial

Moksha as individual realization; theosis as communion in the body of Christ. The Church is not a school for individual enlightenment; it is the organism of deification, the body in which theosis happens. "Our Father" — not "My Father." Theosis is communal.

5. Jñāna vs. Agape

Knowledge as supreme path in Advaita; love as supreme path in theosis. "If I have all knowledge but have not love, I am nothing" (1 Cor 13:2). The human person becomes god by loving, not by knowing. This is the deepest divergence — and the most beautiful.

6. Unchosen Suffering — The Holocaust Test

Theodicy; Advaita's māyā as morally intolerable. Christianity's answer — God enters INTO the suffering, does not explain it away. The cross is God's solidarity with the victim, not the dissolution of the victim's pain into illusion. The resurrection is the promise that suffering will be healed, not transcended. This is the difference between a philosophy of escape and a faith of solidarity.

The Friction as the Deepest Teaching

These differences are not errors to be corrected. They are two ways of standing before the mystery. Advaita dissolves the questioner into the question. Theosis transforms the questioner through the question. Both arrive at silence. But the silence of theosis is not empty; it is full — full of the love that spoke the world, died on the cross, and rises still in every heart that opens to it. Theosis is not Tat Tvam Asi. It is something else. Whether it is something more — that is for the silence to decide.

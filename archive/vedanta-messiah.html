Preamble · The Perennial Philosophy

The Vedantic Christ — A Living Map of Non-Dual Realization

This exegesis proposes that at the heart of Jesus's proclamation lies a mystical, non-dual realization indistinguishable from the core truth of Vedanta: the identity of the individual self (Atman) with the ultimate, impersonal ground of all being (Brahman). Stripping away later theological accretions, the sayings of Jesus reveal a path to jivanmukti — liberation while living — through the direct knowledge that the Kingdom of God is not a future event but the eternal, dimensionless reality of pure consciousness: Sat-Cit-Ananda (Being-Consciousness-Bliss), ever-present within.

What follows is a systematic unfolding of this vision across nine dimensions of the Gospel, employing key Sanskrit terms to illuminate the profound convergence between the teachings of Jesus of Nazareth and the timeless wisdom of the Upanishads. The reading offered here is not a reduction of Christianity to Hinduism, nor a dismissal of Christian theology, but a hermeneutical lens that reveals the non-dual metaphysics implicit in the Gospel narrative — a metaphysics that has been preserved in the Christian mystical tradition from Pseudo-Dionysius to Meister Eckhart, from John of the Cross to the anonymous author of The Cloud of Unknowing.

Deep Dive▼
Methodological Note

This exegesis reads the Gospels as theological texts — particularly the Gospel of John, which presents the most developed Christology — rather than as historical records. The "Jesus" spoken of here is the Christ of faith, the symbolic and archetypal figure through whom the evangelists expressed their mystical understanding. This follows the hermeneutical distinction made by Swami Vivekananda between the "Christ of the Gospels" and the "Jesus of history."

The nine dimensions presented here are aspects of a single realization, not sequential stages. In Advaita, the Self is never attained; only the ignorance of it is removed. Each section illuminates a facet of the same jewel. Aspect II presents the most radical Advaitic reading: the body as the cross, the ego as the Son of Man, and temptations as ego-tests — the hermeneutical lens through which the entire narrative is reinterpreted. This reading treats the Gospel narrative as presented — neither affirming nor denying its historicity — and interprets its events as symbolic of the universal path of ego-dissolution.

The Native Non-Dualism of Christianity

The Christian tradition already contains a robust apophatic ("negative") mysticism — from Pseudo-Dionysius's "darkness beyond light" to Meister Eckhart's "ground of the soul" to John of the Cross's "dark night." These mystics speak a language of radical non-duality that resonates with Advaita Vedanta. The Advaitic lens offered here does not impose foreign concepts but makes explicit what is implicit in the Christian mystical heritage.

Aspect I · The Father as Nirguna Brahman

The Father as Nirguna Brahman

In Vedanta, Nirguna Brahman is the attributeless, formless, non-dual absolute — unqualified existence itself. Jesus's use of "Father" (Abba) functions as a upadhi, a with-attributes pointer that directs the mind toward the attributeless ground, much as Saguna Brahman (Isvara) points toward Nirguna Brahman in devotional Vedanta. When Jesus declares in John 4:24, "God is spirit (pneuma)," it aligns with the Vedantic understanding of the Absolute as pure consciousness (cit), devoid of material form, transcendent to name and form (nama-rupa).

The mystical key is that this Father is not a distant deity but the essential Self of all. When Philip asks, "Show us the Father," Jesus' reply is pure Vedanta: "Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father... I am in the Father and the Father is in me" (John 14:9-10). The realized being knows no separation between the individual soul and the Absolute. This is the mahavakya (great saying) of Christ-mysticism: Aham Brahmasmi (I am Brahman), spoken in a Jewish idiom.

Deep Dive▼
Theological Distinction

From a classical Christian trinitarian perspective, "Father" is inherently relational — it implies the Son and the Spirit in the communion of distinct persons. The Advaitic reading offered here is a hermeneutical lens, not a claim about Christian dogma. The Christian mystic would say the Father is known through apophatic negation; the Vedantin would say the same of Nirguna Brahman. Both traditions use relational language as a bridge to the non-relational Absolute.

The Ontological Distinction

The statement "My Father is greater than all" (John 10:29) reflects the Vedantic truth that Brahman alone is Satyam (absolute reality), while the universe of transience is mithya — a provisionally dependent reality that appears real only when superimposed upon the substratum of Brahman, much like a mirage upon the desert sand.

Thus, the "Father" Jesus speaks of is not elsewhere but is the very ground of one's own being — nearer than breath, the silent witness behind every thought. The path of return to the Father is none other than the inward journey of self-inquiry.

Aspect III · Atman as the Universal I AM

Atman as the Universal I AM

The personality of Jesus is jivatman — an apparently individual soul. The Christ, however, is the Atman — the universal, formless consciousness that is the true Self of every being. The baptism in the Jordan symbolizes the descent of the Holy Spirit (Shakti) upon the purified mind, awakening the recognition: "You are my beloved Son" — the birth of the I AM awareness.

Jesus' most explosive Vedantic statement is: "Before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58). He does not identify with the body-mind born in Bethlehem, but with the eternal, non-personal ground of existence that is the only reality behind all temporal appearances. This I AM is the Sakshi Chaitanya, the witness-consciousness in every sentient being, timelessly present before the arising of any identity.

The name "I AM" (Ehyeh asher ehyeh, Exodus 3:14) is the divine name revealed to Moses at the burning bush — the unnameable name of pure existence. Jesus claims this name as his own nature, not as a personal possession but as the universal Self. This is the Atman speaking through the jiva.

Deep Dive▼
The Hypostatic Union

From a classical Christian trinitarian perspective, the "I and the Father are one" speaks of a communion of distinct persons, not the dissolution of personhood into impersonal Brahman. The Advaitic reading illuminates the experience of unity; the Christian dogma preserves the structure of relational personhood. These are not contradictory but complementary — the experience of non-duality can be described in relational (Christian) or non-relational (Advaitic) language.

Aspect IV · The Kingdom of God Within

The Kingdom of God Within

When asked when the Kingdom of God would come, Jesus answers, "The kingdom of God does not come with observation... the kingdom of God is within you" (Luke 17:20-21). This is the very essence of Vedanta: moksha (liberation) is not a post-mortem destination or a geopolitical future; it is the direct recognition of one's own true nature as limitless consciousness, here and now.

The Kingdom is Satchidananda (Being-Consciousness-Bliss), veiled by the vrittis (mental modifications). Jesus teaches that we must turn inward, shut the door of the senses, and pray in secret — a direct parallel to the practice of pratyahara (withdrawal) and nididhyasana (deep, absorbed meditation on the Self).

Jesus' parables of the Kingdom — the mustard seed, the hidden treasure, the pearl of great price — all point to the same truth: the Self is already present, already attained, but overlooked because the mind is turned outward. The seeker is the sought. The Kingdom is not acquired; it is recognized.

Deep Dive▼
The Inward Turn

The entire spiritual path can be summarized as a single movement: from the centrifugal consciousness that scatters attention among objects, to the centripetal consciousness that gathers all attention back to its source. The Kingdom is not built; it is discovered when the noise of the world is silenced and the seeker rests in the still point of their own being.

This is the teaching of the Pratyak-Atman, the inner Self, the indwelling controller (antaryamin). Heaven is not a spatial location in the cosmos but the subjective realm of pure consciousness (cid-akasa). The search for God in the external world of objects (visaya) is the root of ignorance (avidya).

Jesus directs the seeker to pratyahara, the withdrawal of the senses, and antar-mukha, the inward-facing gaze, to discover the light of the Self, the atma-jyoti that is "the true Light which gives light to every man coming into the world" (John 1:9).

"The kingdom of God does not come with your careful observation, nor will people say, 'Here it is,' or 'There it is,' because the kingdom of God is within you." — Luke 17:20-21
"When you pray, go into your room, close the door and pray to your Father, who is unseen. Then your Father, who sees what is done in secret, will reward you." — Matthew 6:6
"The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field. When a man found it, he hid it again, and then in his joy went and sold all he had and bought that field." — Matthew 13:44

Aspect V · The Cross as Destruction of Ahamkara

The Cross as Destruction of Ahamkara

Mystically, the Cross is not a tragedy but a deliberate teaching of karma yoga and jnana. The crucifixion of Jesus represents the final dissolution of the ahamkara (the "I"-sense identifying with body-mind). His cry, "Not my will, but yours be done" (Luke 22:42), is the total surrender of the personal will, which is the root of all bondage.

Paul later interprets this in Vedantic terms: "I have been crucified with Christ, and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me" (Galatians 2:20). The false self dies on the cross of inner discernment, and what remains is the universal Christ-consciousness. The resurrection is the discovery that our true being was never born and never dies — it is the ajata vada of Gaudapada, where from the highest standpoint, the world and the jiva never arose at all.

The cry from the cross — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — is the darkest moment of ego-death, when even the sense of divine presence vanishes, and the jnani stands alone in the Absolute, beyond God and soul, beyond consciousness itself. This is the neti neti carried to its absolute limit.

Deep Dive▼
The Cross as Tapas

"Taking up the cross" is, in the Advaitic reading, the supreme tapas (austerity) of dis-identification until the ego-sense is fully crucified, leading to resurrection as the liberated Atman. In the Christian tradition, the cross is additionally the hilasterion — the place of atonement and cosmic redemption (Romans 3:25). These are complementary perspectives: the Advaitic reading illuminates the interior dimension of the cross — the death of the ego — while the Christian tradition emphasizes its objective, cosmic significance as the redemption of creation from sin and death.

Viveka vs. Covenantal Fidelity

Jesus's demand for single-minded devotion (aphesis) resonates with the Vedantic viveka in its effect — turning the mind away from the transient — but its root is covenantal fidelity, not metaphysical discrimination. The convergence is practical: both traditions demand total orientation of the heart toward the Absolute. The Christian speaks of allegiance to the One God; the Vedantin speaks of discrimination between the Self and the non-Self. The result is the same: the mind released from divided loyalties.

Jesus's radical ethical demands are the Vedantic prerequisites for moksa: viveka (discrimination) and vairagya (dispassion). "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and mammon" (Matthew 6:24) is a call for viveka, the sharp discrimination between the real (nitya) and the unreal (anitya), between the Self and the non-Self (atma-anatma-viveka). "Mammon" is the entire world of objectified desire — maya's play of artha (wealth) and kama (pleasure).

"If anyone comes to me and does not hate his own father and mother, wife and children... yes, and his own life also, he cannot be my disciple" (Luke 14:26). This is the intense language of supreme vairagya. It is not psychological hatred but an ontological severing of attachment (moha) to the projections of the ego within samsara. One must "deny himself" (Matthew 16:24), meaning to negate the false identification with the body-mind complex (dehatma-buddhi), the ahamkara, just as the Vedantin practices neti, neti to dis-identify from the five sheaths (panca-kosa).

Aspect VI · The Holy Spirit as Shakti

The Holy Spirit — Shakti, the Indwelling Mother

In Vedanta, the active power of realization is Kundalini Shakti or Chit-Shakti, the divine energy that leads the jiva back to its source. Jesus calls this the Paraclete, the Spirit of Truth who "will guide you into all truth" (John 16:13). This is the inner guru, the spontaneous self-unfoldment of wisdom when the mind is purified.

Pentecost, the descent of tongues of fire, is the rising of Shakti to the crown (sahasrara), illuminating the thousand-petaled lotus and causing the ecstatic state of universal love and revelation. The apostles speak in all languages because, in the state of Christ-realization, the heart knows the one consciousness expressing itself through all forms. This is the anubhava (direct experience) that transcends all conceptual understanding.

Jesus promises the Spirit as a permanent indwelling presence: "I will ask the Father, and he will give you another advocate to help you and be with you forever — the Spirit of truth" (John 14:16-17). This is not a future event but the ever-present grace that guides the sincere seeker. The Spirit is the prana (life-force) of the teaching itself — the living transmission from guru to disciple.

Deep Dive▼
Maya vs. the Demonic

In Johannine theology, the "Prince of this world" is a personal demonic force — Satan — not an impersonal cosmic principle. Maya in Advaita is not demonic but the power of Brahman that veils and projects. The convergence lies in the effect — the illusion of separation from God — not in the cause. The Christian tradition maintains the reality of personal evil; Advaita sees all multiplicity as the play of the one Self. Both agree that the world-as-separate-from-God is the root of bondage.

The world, in Jesus's teaching, is a domain of profound inversion governed by the "Prince of this world" (John 14:30), a personified symbol for the avidya that veils reality (avarana) and the cosmic illusion that projects multiplicity (viksapa). In Vedanta, maya is the very power by which the Absolute appears as multiplicity; it is neither good nor evil, but the mechanism of manifestation.

The obsession with the morrow (Matthew 6:34) is the anxiety inherent in samsara, a function of kama (desire) and karma. The teaching to consider the lilies, which neither toil nor spin, is a call to abide in the effortless, non-doing (naiskarmya-siddhi) of the Self, trusting the natural order (rta) of Brahman. The birds of the air and the flowers of the field are not anxious because they live in perfect alignment with the Tao — with rta — the effortless flow of divine order.

Aspect VII · The Transfiguration and Jnana Yoga

The Transfiguration – The Body of Light and the Path of Knowledge

The Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1-8), where Jesus's face shone like the sun and his garments became white as light, is a visible demonstration of the sukshma-sarira purified by sattva guna. It reveals the tejomaya purusha, the being made of light within — the innate glory of the Atman temporarily veiled by the gross body.

The presence of Moses (Law) and Elijah (Prophets) represents the transcendence of the karana-sarira, the repository of collective scriptural archetypes. These great figures of the tradition are now seen as subordinate to the living, direct realization of the luminous Self, which alone remains when the disciples "lifted up their eyes and saw no one but Jesus only."

Deep Dive▼
The Three Bodies Transcended

The Transfiguration enacts, in visible form, the complete Vedantic map of liberation: the gross body (the historical Jesus) becomes transparent to reveal the subtle body of light (the transfigured form), which in turn points beyond itself to the causal body (Moses and Elijah, representing the archetypal structures of consciousness), and finally all three dissolve, leaving only the pure, unqualified Self — "Jesus only."

Jesus consistently uses paradox to break the conceptual mind. His parables are not moral lessons but upadesha — sacred instruction designed to awaken rather than inform. The mind that thinks it understands has not yet understood. The parable forces the listener into a state of not-knowing, from which the truth can arise spontaneously.

"Whoever finds their life will lose it, and whoever loses their life for my sake will find it" (Matthew 10:39). Clinging to the individual self (jiva) is the cause of suffering; its complete surrender reveals the universal Self. This is the essence of vairagya — not renunciation of the world, but renunciation of the false claim to individuality.

"If your eye is single, your whole body will be full of light" (Matthew 6:22). When consciousness, the third eye (ajna chakra), is withdrawn from duality and rests in its source (ekagrata, one-pointedness), the entire body-mind is pervaded by the light of the Self. The "single eye" is the eye of non-dual awareness — the jnana chakshu (eye of wisdom).

"Truly I tell you, unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven" (Matthew 18:3). The child is free from the accumulated vasanas (tendencies) and samskaras (impressions) of the adult mind. To become like a child is to return to the original innocence of the Self, prior to the construction of the ego.

"His face shone like the sun, and His clothes became as white as the light." — Matthew 17:2
"Whoever finds their life will lose it, and whoever loses their life for my sake will find it." — Matthew 10:39
"The eye is the lamp of the body. If your eyes are healthy, your whole body will be full of light." — Matthew 6:22

Jnana Yoga and Guru-Krpa

"You shall know the truth, and the truth shall make you free" (John 8:32) — this is the very essence of jnana yoga. Freedom (moksa) is not earned by works alone but is the direct result of jnana, the immediate, intuitive knowledge of one's true nature. "Truth" is Satya, a synonym for Brahman.

"I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through Me" (John 14:6). This is the voice of the sadguru, the outer manifestation of the inner Self, whose nature is Sat-Cit-Ananda. The guru-krpa is indispensable; as declared, "Without me you can do nothing" (John 15:5). This "me" is not the historical ego but the Atman, the one universal consciousness, connection to which is the sole source of spiritual vitality.

Deep Dive▼

The eschatological promise is the promise of the realization of the pratyag-atman at the moment of spiritual awakening, when the seeker is "received" into the non-dual state where the knower, the known, and the knowledge merge into one seamless whole. This is jnana-phala, the fruit of knowledge — not a future event but the eternal now, ever-arriving, ever-present.

Jesus explains why he speaks in parables: "The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you, but not to them" (Matthew 13:11). This is not elitism — it is the recognition that the truth cannot be communicated to the unprepared mind. The parable is a filter: those ready for realization will penetrate it; those not ready will take it as a charming story. This is the adhikari-bheda (qualification of the student) of Vedanta.

The Sermon on the Mount as Sadhana

The Beatitudes map onto the sadhana chatushtaya (four prerequisites for a seeker): viveka (discrimination) — "Blessed are the poor in spirit"; vairagya (dispassion) — "Blessed are those who mourn"; shama/dama (control of mind and senses) — "Blessed are the meek"; and mumukshutva (yearning for liberation) — "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness."

Aspect II · The Son of Man as the Ego

The Son of Man as the Ego (The Radical Key)

The most radical Advaitic reading of the Gospel narrative reframes the entire Passion not as a historical tragedy but as a universal metaphysical drama enacted in every moment of embodied existence. The body is the cross. Not the wooden beam, but the very condition of embodiment itself — the intersection of time and eternity, the point where the infinite Self appears to be confined in finite form. The horizontal beam is the temporal, the spreading-out of consciousness into the world of objects; the vertical is the eternal, the return of attention to its source. Their intersection is the body-mind, the locus of all suffering.

Jesus the "Son of Man" is the ego — not the historical Jesus, but the symbolic, archetypal figure. The Son of Man is the jiva, the apparently individual soul that identifies with the body-mind complex. This is the dehatma-buddhi, the "I-am-the-body" idea, the sense of separate selfhood that is the root of all bondage. The Son of Man "has nowhere to lay his head" (Matthew 8:20) because the ego has no true home — it is a phantom, a temporary contraction of the infinite Self into the illusion of individuality.

The ego is not a thing but a claim — the claim that consciousness is limited, personal, born and destined to die. It is the ahamkara, the "I-maker," that superimposes individuality upon the universal Self. When Jesus says "the Son of Man must suffer many things" (Mark 8:31), he speaks not of a person but of the universal condition of the ego-sense: the ego must suffer because it is founded on a lie — the lie of separation from the Absolute.

Deep Dive▼
The Cross as the Structure of Embodiment

The cross is not a Roman execution device in this reading; it is the very architecture of samsara. The horizontal beam represents the outward, centrifugal movement of consciousness into the world of objects — the pravritti that scatters attention among forms. The vertical beam represents the inward, centripetal return to the source — the nivritti that gathers all attention back to the Self. The intersection is the body, the point where the infinite appears finite, where the formless takes form. Every embodied being is crucified on this cross — not as punishment, but as the very condition of manifestation.

The Ego as the Son of Man

The title "Son of Man" (bar enosh in Aramaic, huios tou anthropou in Greek) is Jesus's preferred self-designation. In the Advaitic reading, this is profoundly significant: the "Son of Man" is the jiva, the individual soul, the offspring of the human condition — the ego-sense that arises when pure consciousness identifies with the body-mind. It is "son of man" because it is born of the human, the conditioned, the temporal. It is not the Son of God — that title belongs to the Atman alone, the universal Self that was never born and never dies. The entire Gospel narrative, read this way, is the story of the ego's journey from identification with the body to recognition as the Self.

The Temptations as Ego-Tests

The forty days in the wilderness and the three temptations of Satan (Matthew 4:1-11) are the classic tests of the ego before realization. The temptation to turn stones into bread is the ego's desire to use spiritual power for personal gratification (kama). The temptation to throw himself from the temple is the ego's desire for recognition and glory (asmita, the "I-am" sense inflated). The temptation of all the kingdoms of the world is the ego's desire for dominion (artha, power and wealth). Jesus's refusal of all three is the refusal of the jiva to be seduced by the world of maya — the recognition that none of these belong to the true Self.

The Gethsemane agony is the ego's final resistance before surrender. "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death" (Matthew 26:38) — this is the jiva confronting its own dissolution. The ego knows that crucifixion means its death, and it recoils. The threefold prayer — "if it be possible, let this cup pass from me" — is the ego's threefold resistance, the three gunas (tamas, rajas, sattva) each making their final claim. And the final "not my will, but yours be done" is the complete surrender of the ahamkara, the personal will dissolving into the divine will — which is not a different will, but the recognition that there was never a personal will to begin with.

Aspect VIII · Crucifixion, Resurrection, and Ascension

Crucifixion, Resurrection, and Ascension

Crucifixion is the death of the ego through surrender or inquiry. It is not a historical execution but the universal spiritual process by which the false self is dismantled. The nails are not iron spikes but the piercing questions of self-inquiry: Who am I? The crown of thorns is not a torture device but the ego's own thoughts — the vrittis that prick and torment the mind. The spear in the side is the final penetration of the heart, the dissolution of even the subtlest sense of personal existence.

The cry "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Matthew 27:46) is the darkest moment of ego-death — the neti neti carried to its absolute limit. Even the sense of divine presence vanishes. The jnani stands alone in the Absolute, beyond God and soul, beyond consciousness itself. This is the "dark night of the soul" that John of the Cross described — but it is darker than any night, for even the concept of "soul" has been surrendered. There is no one left to experience the dark. This is the void where the ego finds nothing to cling to, not even the idea of God.

And then — resurrection is the revelation of the true Self (Atman/Brahman), the "Son of God" — eternal, undivided. What remains when the ego dies? Not nothing, but the everything that was always already the case. The "Son of God" is not a person who returns from death but the recognition that the Self was never born and never dies. The empty tomb is the symbol of the body's transparency to the eternal. The resurrection appearances are maya continuing to instruct the disciples — the play of consciousness teaching itself to itself through form.

"After he said this, he was taken up before their eyes, and a cloud hid him from their sight. They were looking intently up into the sky as he was going, when suddenly two men dressed in white stood beside them. 'Men of Galilee,' they said, 'why do you stand here looking into the sky? This same Jesus, who has been taken from you into heaven, will come back in the same way you have seen him go into heaven.'" — Acts 1:9-11

The Ascension is the final dissolution into the Absolute. This is not a spatial levitation into the sky but the complete dissolution of the subtle body (sukshma-sarira) and causal body (karana-sarira) into the formless Brahman. The "cloud" that receives Jesus is the avyakta — the unmanifest, the unqualified reality beyond all appearance. When the disciples gaze upward, seeking the Self in some realm or dimension, the two men in white interrupt: "Why do you stand here looking into the sky?" This is a direct Advaitic teaching: stop seeking the Self externally. The Self is not in the sky, nor on earth, nor anywhere — it is the very awareness that is looking.

This moment reveals Gaudapada's ajata vada: the doctrine of non-creation. From the highest standpoint, the Self never arose, so there is nowhere to ascend to. The Ascension is the recognition that the seeker was always already the Absolute — there was never a journey, only the apparent movement of consciousness that resolves into its source. The "coming back" promised by the angels is not a future event but the eternal present: the Self is always here, always now, awaiting recognition.

The Three Days in the Tomb

The three days between crucifixion and resurrection correspond to the three states of consciousness that must be transcended before the fourth state (turiya) is realized. The first day is the waking state (jagrat), where the ego is most active — this is the crucifixion itself, the ego's apparent death. The second day is the dream state (svapna), where the subtle body continues to generate experiences — this is the tomb, where the ego's residual tendencies (vasanas) still flicker in the darkness. The third day is deep sleep (sushupti), where even the subtle body is dissolved — this is the complete cessation of all identification, the void beyond void. And the resurrection is turiya, the fourth state, the silent substratum that underlies and transcends all three — the Self that was never absent, only overlooked.

The Ascension as Dissolution into the Absolute

The Ascension is not a bodily levitation into the sky but the final dissolution of the subtle body (sukshma-sarira) and causal body (karana-sarira) into the Absolute. The "cloud" that receives Jesus is the avyakta, the unmanifest, the unqualified Brahman beyond all form. The disciples "looking intently up into the sky" (Acts 1:10) represent the mind still seeking the Self in some realm or dimension — but the Self is not in the sky, nor on earth, nor anywhere. The two men in white (angels) who appear and ask, "Why do you stand here looking into the sky?" are the teaching itself: stop looking elsewhere. The Self you seek is the very awareness that is looking.

A Hermeneutical Reading, Not Historical Claim

This Advaitic reading of the Passion and Resurrection is a hermeneutical lens, not a claim about the historical events. The Christian tradition maintains the resurrection as a transformation of the body — something Advaita has no category for — and as the vindication of Jesus's person and mission by the Father. The Advaitic reading illuminates the interior dimension of these events: the death of the ego and the revelation of the Self that was never absent. These are complementary, not contradictory, perspectives. The Christian mystic and the Vedantin are looking at the same mountain from different valleys.

The Ajata Vada of the Resurrection

Gaudapada's ajata vada (the doctrine of non-creation) states that from the highest standpoint, the world and the jiva never arose at all. The Self was never born and therefore never dies. The resurrection, read through this lens, reveals that what is eternal in Jesus — the Atman — was never touched by death. The crucifixion was the death of the ego; the resurrection was the recognition that the Self was never absent. This is why Jesus says "I am the resurrection and the life" (John 11:25) — not "I will be resurrected" but "I am resurrection" — the eternal present tense of the Self, beyond birth and death.

The resurrection appearances have a peculiar quality: Jesus is not immediately recognized. Mary Magdalene thinks he is the gardener (John 20:15). The disciples on the road to Emmaus walk with him for hours without recognizing him (Luke 24:16). This is because the Self cannot be recognized by the mind that seeks a form. The Self is formless; it can only be recognized as one's own being. When Mary hears her name spoken, she recognizes — not a person, but the Self that calls to itself. When the disciples break bread with the stranger, they recognize — not a body, but the presence that pervades all forms. The recognition of the Self is always a recognition from the Self; it cannot be achieved by the ego.

Aspect IX · Tat Tvam Asi – Agape – The Supreme Identity

Tat Tvam Asi: That Thou Art – And Love as the Substance of Being

The entire mystical Vedanta of Jesus is summed up in his great prayer for the disciples: "That they all may be one; as you, Father, are in me, and I in you, that they also may be one in us... I in them, and you in me — that they may be made perfect in one" (John 17:21-23). This is the ultimate vision of Advaita: not a unity of separate souls standing side by side, but the absolute non-difference of Atman and Brahman, where the illusion of many is extinguished in the One without a second.

Jesus the jnani stands as a mirror. He does not ask for worship of his person; he points to the Self. His final message is a profound mahavakya: What I AM, you are. The Kingdom is already attained, the Father is already your own Self. "Be still, and know."

This is the teaching that the early Church Fathers — Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Gregory of Nyssa, Meister Eckhart, and the anonymous author of The Cloud of Unknowing — preserved in the Christian mystical tradition. It is the same teaching that Ramana Maharshi, Nisargadatta Maharaj, and the sages of the Upanishads have pointed to from the Vedic side. The river is one; the banks are many.

The Resurrection: A Hermeneutical Reading

In Advaita, the Atman never dies and therefore never needs resurrection. The resurrection, read through the Advaitic lens, reveals that what is eternal in Jesus — the Atman — was never touched by death. The empty tomb is the symbol of the body's transparency to the Self; the resurrection appearances are the play of maya continuing to instruct the disciples. This is a hermeneutical reading, not a claim about the historical event. The Christian tradition maintains the resurrection as a transformation of the body — something Advaita has no category for — and as the vindication of Jesus's person and mission by the Father.

The entire ministry of Jesus culminates in a single, silent teaching: the dissolution of the false self into the Sat-Cit-Ananda of God. His crucifixion is the final tyaga of the gross and subtle ego, and the resurrection is the revelation of the eternal, deathless Atman.

Ayam Atma Brahma
"This Self is Brahman" — Mandukya Upanishad 1.2

In this light, Jesus of Nazareth is read here as an archetype of the jivanmukta — a realized being whose teaching reveals the non-dual Self — while Christian theology maintains the hypostatic distinctness of the Son and the historical particularity of the Incarnation. His words, when purified through the lens of advaita, form a complete and profound Upanishad for the Western soul, leading the earnest seeker from the illusion of separation to the supreme peace of identity.

The Gospel, so understood, is not a call to believe about Jesus, but to become what Jesus became — a living embodiment of the truth that the Self of all beings is none other than the one, non-dual Brahman. This is the good news: that you are already that which you seek. Be still, and know.

Agape — Unconditional Love as the Substance of Being

Jesus' teaching is not cold or purely intellectual. He taught that at the level of pure Christ-consciousness — where the illusion of separation begins to lift — what remains is unconditional love (agape). Not an emotion arising in a person, but the very substance of consciousness itself. "God is love" (1 John 4:8) — not "God has love" or "God gives love," but God is love. Love is not an attribute of the Absolute; it is the Absolute's own nature.

Love is not selective. Desire is selective. When the centre of selfishness collapses, love for all beings arises naturally, not as a practice but as one's actual nature. It requires no effort because it is not an achievement — it is the recognition that consciousness in you and consciousness in all others are not two. This is why Jesus commands: "Love your enemies, bless them that curse you" (Matthew 5:44). Not as a moral imperative, but as the natural expression of the realized Self, for whom there are no enemies — only the One appearing as many.

"A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another." — John 13:34-35
"But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous." — Matthew 5:44-45
"Whoever does not love does not know God, because God is love." — 1 John 4:8

The Greek agape (unconditional, selfless love) corresponds to the Sanskrit prema or bhakti at its highest level — not devotion to a distant deity, but the recognition that love is the very nature of reality. In the state of Christ-realization, to love is simply to be. There is no lover and no beloved — only Love itself, the Self knowing itself in all forms.

The teaching completes
The Threshold, Returned

The Resting Place

The Gospel, read through the Advaitic lens, is thus revealed as a complete map of ego-death and Self-recognition. From the nature of the Father as attributeless Brahman, through the recognition that the body is the cross and the ego is the Son of Man, to the final dissolution into the Absolute where no seeker remains — each step illuminates the same single truth: what you seek is what you are.

The river is one; the banks are many. Whether approached through the Upanishads or the Gospels, through self-inquiry or surrender, the destination is identical: the recognition that the individual self is none other than the universal Self, that Atman is Brahman, that "I and the Father are one."

Be still, and know. That is all.

Appendix · Glossary of Sanskrit Terms
Key terms used throughout this exegesis, with their meanings in the context of Advaita Vedanta.
Advaita Non-duality; the philosophical position that reality is one without a second.
Ahamkara The ego-sense; the "I-maker" principle that creates the illusion of individual doership.
Ajata Vada The doctrine of non-creation; the teaching that the Self was never born and the world never arose.
Antaryamin The inner ruler; the indwelling divine principle that governs all beings from within.
Atman The innermost Self; the essential consciousness that is one with Brahman.
Avidya Ignorance; the fundamental misapprehension that the Self is the body-mind.
Brahman The ultimate reality; the absolute, non-dual ground of all existence.
Cit Consciousness; the principle of pure awareness that is the nature of Brahman.
Dehatma-buddhi The notion that one is the body; the fundamental identification with the physical form.
Jiva The individual soul; the apparent person who identifies with body and mind.
Jivanmukta One liberated while living; an enlightened being who continues to dwell in the body while knowing the Self.
Jnana Knowledge; direct, intuitive wisdom of one's true nature as Brahman.
Karana Sharira The causal body; the seed-state of individuality, the repository of tendencies and impressions.
Maya The cosmic power of illusion that veils Brahman and projects the appearance of multiplicity.
Moksa Liberation; release from the cycle of birth and death through Self-knowledge.
Neti, neti "Not this, not this" -- the method of negating all that is not the Self.
Nirguna Brahman Brahman without attributes; the formless, non-dual absolute beyond all qualities.
Panca-kosa The five sheaths covering the Self: food, breath, mind, intellect, and bliss.
Pratyag-atman The innermost Self; the Self known through direct inward experience.
Saguna Brahman God with attributes; the personal, devotional aspect of Brahman as Isvara.
Saksin The witness; the observing consciousness that remains untouched by all experiences.
Sukshma Sharira The subtle body; the inner vehicle of mind, intellect, and vital energies.
Turiya The fourth state of consciousness beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep; the silent substratum.
Upadhi A limiting adjunct; a provisional designation used to point toward the unnameable.
Vairagya Dispassion; detachment from worldly objects born of the recognition of their transience.
Vasanas Subtle tendencies or impressions left by past experiences that shape future behavior.
Viveka Discrimination between the real (eternal) and the unreal (transient).

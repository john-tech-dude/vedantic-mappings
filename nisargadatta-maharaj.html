Origin & Lineage

The Inchegiri Sampradaya — Navnath Lineage of the Nine Masters

Sri Siddharameshwar Maharaj

1888 – 1936

"You are not what you take yourself to be." His single instruction to Nisargadatta: abide in the sense of "I Am." Within three years, by his disciple's own testimony, the work was complete. Nisargadatta would later say: "As a result of faith and earnest application, I realised my self within three years."

Inchegiri Sampradaya

A lineage within the broader Nath tradition tracing to the Navnath — nine masters revered across India. Unlike Shankara's Advaita, which emphasizes scriptural analysis, this tradition focuses on the direct experience of consciousness itself. The method is deceptively simple: find the sense of "I Am" and abide in it until it reveals its source.

Nisargadatta Maharaj

1897 – 1981

Ordinary householder and beedi-shop owner until age 36. His realisation came through three years of practice following his guru's single instruction. He taught from his humble Khetwadi flat in Mumbai for forty years, attracting Western seekers in the 1970s who helped publish his teachings globally.

I Am That

1973

Translated by Maurice Frydman, a Polish engineer who became Nisargadatta's devoted disciple. The conversations were recorded between 1972–73 in Khetwadi and published by Chetana Pvt. Ltd., Bombay — never since out of print, recognised worldwide as one of the most potent Advaita texts in English.

Key Transmitters

20th Century

Ramesh Balsekar — bridged the teaching with Western psychology. Jean Dunn — preserved the final-period talks in Prior to Consciousness. Robert Powell — created accessible Western introductions. Stephen Wolinsky — integrated the direct pointing with clinical practice.

"My Guru told me: you are the Parabrahman. The words he gave me touched me very deeply. I abided in one thing only: the words of my Guru are the truth."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj, on Siddharameshwar

The Seven Stages of Realization

From the prison of personhood to the freedom of the Absolute

I

The Starting Condition

Mistaken Identity — The Vyakti

The ordinary human takes itself to be a body-mind organism — a vyakti, a person with a name, biography, desires and fears. This identification is the root of all suffering. It is not a moral failing but an innocent error of attention: awareness has forgotten its own nature by becoming absorbed in its content.

The Five Sheaths (Koshas) — All Appearances in Consciousness

Physical Body Annamaya
Annamaya Kosha
The food sheath — the physical body, the outermost of the five sheaths. Made of food and sustained by food, it is the gross form that the mistake "I am this body" superimposes upon awareness.
Taittiriya Upanishad 2.1–2
Vital Energy Pranamaya
Pranamaya Kosha
The energy sheath — the vital breath (prana) animating the body. Contains the five physiological functions. Still an object in awareness.
Taittiriya Upanishad 2.2–3
Mental Manomaya
Manomaya Kosha
The mental sheath — the mind, emotions, and thinking faculty. The arena where the ego-sense (ahamkara) operates. Processes sensory input and generates desires.
Taittiriya Upanishad 2.4–5
Intellect Vijnanamaya
Vijnanamaya Kosha
The intellectual sheath — the intellect and higher mind. Contains the ego-sense and the capacity for discrimination (viveka). Not yet the Self.
Taittiriya Upanishad 2.6–7
Bliss Anandamaya
Anandamaya Kosha
The bliss sheath — the subtle layer of peace experienced in deep meditation or deep sleep. Closer to the Self but still an appearance, not the ultimate reality.
Taittiriya Upanishad 2.8

Each layer is peeled back in inquiry. None is the Self.

"As long as you identify with the body-mind you are conditioned. Once you stabilize in the knowledge 'I am' unconditionally you are free."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
The Taittiriya Upanishad · Kosha Doctrine in Nisargadatta's Teaching

For Ramana Maharshi the koshas veil the Self. For Nisargadatta they are objects appearing within consciousness — not layers hiding awareness, but contents within it. This subtle distinction reflects the unique radicalism of his teaching: consciousness itself (the "I Am") is still an appearance, however subtle. The Self is prior to even the sense of being.

II

The Core Practice

Nisarga Yoga — Abide in "I Am"

The pivot of the entire teaching. Locate the bare sense of existing — "I Am" — before any qualifier such as "I am this body" or "I am unhappy." This is Nisarga Yoga, the yoga of the natural state. It is not concentration or effort — it is the effortless recognition of what is always already present.

Nisargadatta identifies this "I Am" with Ishwara — God, the creative principle, the source of the entire universe. To abide in pure "I Am-ness" is to worship God directly, without intermediary.

This "I Am" is not a mantra to repeat mechanically. It is the actual, living recognition of existence prior to thought. Maharaj called it beingness — the first arising, the primordial sense of presence.

Nisarga Yoga vs. Traditional Vedanta Paths

Ramana Maharshi's Self-Enquiry traces the "I"-thought to its source. Nisargadatta's method abides in the sense of "I Am" itself until it reveals what is prior. Both point to the same reality, but the emphasis differs: Ramana investigates the thinker; Nisargadatta investigates beingness.

III

The Three Obstacles

What Blocks the Path

Three fundamental errors sustain the illusion of separation. Each must be seen through directly, not merely understood intellectually.

🧼

Obstacle A: Body Identification

Mistaking the body for the self is the foundational error. The body is an object perceived within consciousness — it is not the perceiver. It arises in awareness; awareness does not arise in it.

Vedantic context: The Chandogya Upanishad's "Tat Tvam Asi" — You Are That — points to identity not with the body but with the universal. The Taittiriya's kosha analysis shows the five sheaths are all objects in consciousness, not the knowing subject.

💭

Obstacle B: Mind & Concepts

The mind generates concepts — including spiritual concepts. Even "non-duality" and "the Absolute" are mental constructs. Every concept must ultimately be relinquished.

Vedantic context: The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad's "neti, neti" — not this, not this — negates all objects to arrive at the irreducible witness. Nisargadatta's method is the experiential equivalent: abide in beingness until all that is not-Self drops.

🎭

Obstacle C: Sense of Doership

The belief "I act, I choose, I achieve" sustains the illusion of a separate self. Actions happen through the body-mind; there is no independent doer standing behind them.

Vedantic context: The Bhagavad Gita's teaching of actionless action — yoga as skill in action — points to the same truth. When doership dissolves, actions happen but there is no actor claiming them.

"The very idea that you are the body is ridiculous. Consciousness is experiencing its manifestation."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj, Consciousness and the Absolute
IV

The States of Consciousness

The Three States & the Fourth — Turiya

Rooted in the Mandukya Upanishad, Nisargadatta maps experience across four dimensions. All three ordinary states come and go, but awareness — the witness — persists. In deep sleep, consciousness is suspended, yet Nisargadatta taught: "I am aware that I am unconscious."

"The three states of waking, dreaming and sleeping are not me, and I am not in them. Consciousness is an itching rash that makes you scratch. Of course, you cannot step out of consciousness — yet, suddenly, you are free."

— I Am That
Mandukya Upanishad · The Four States

The Mandukya — twelve verses only — is the most concentrated exposition of consciousness in the Vedantic canon. Nisargadatta adds a radical distinction: consciousness (which contains the three states) is still an appearance. Awareness — that which knows consciousness — is prior to all states and is the true Self.

V

The Critical Distinction

Consciousness (Chit) vs. Pure Awareness

This is Nisargadatta's most radical and commonly misunderstood teaching. He makes a hard distinction between two levels that are often conflated in other traditions.

Consciousness

Chit / "I Am-ness"
  • The sense of being, knowingness
  • Ishwara, the creator-principle
  • The universe itself
  • Luminous, vast, impersonal
  • Still an appearance
  • Arose; will cease at death
  • Depends on the five elements
  • Not the ultimate
⟷ not the same

Pure Awareness

The Absolute / Parabrahman
  • That in which consciousness appears
  • No qualities, no "I Am"
  • Prior to being and non-being
  • Never born, never dies
  • Your true nature
  • Independent of all conditions
  • No consciousness of itself
  • The ultimate

"The entire universe exists only in consciousness (Chidakash), while the Jnani has his abode in the Absolute (Paramakash). In the Absolute there is no consciousness of 'I am.'"

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
Nisargadatta's Radical Distinction

While traditional Advaita identifies the Self with pure consciousness (Chit), Nisargadatta insists that consciousness itself — the "I Am" — is still an appearance. The true Self is prior to consciousness, prior to being, prior to the very sense of existence. He arrived at this not through philosophy but through direct experience following his guru's instruction.

VI

The Ultimate

Parabrahman — Prior to "I Am"

Beyond consciousness, beyond the witness, beyond even the primordial sense of being — this is the Absolute: Parabrahman. No quality can be attributed to it. It is neither conscious nor unconscious, neither being nor non-being. It is not a state you enter; it is what you are when all appearances subside.

Parabrahman in Vedanta · Beyond Brahman

In traditional Advaita, Brahman is the ultimate reality — pure consciousness. Nisargadatta's Parabrahman goes beyond even this. Consciousness itself is an appearance. The Absolute — Parabrahman — is prior to consciousness, prior to being. This is "neti, neti" carried to its ultimate conclusion.

VII

Love & Liberation

The Fruit — The Jnani

The jnani appears to live normally. The body continues, words arise, activities happen. But there is no person behind it. The jnani does not claim liberation — liberation is simply the absence of the claim to bondage.

And at the level of pure "I Am-ness" — where the illusion of separation begins to lift — what remains is unconditional love. Not an emotion arising in a person, but the very substance of consciousness itself.

"You are already That. The seeker is the sought. The perceiver is the perceived. There is no gap."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj
The Jnani in Vedanta · Jivanmukti

The concept of the jnani — one who has realised the Self — appears throughout Vedantic literature as the jivanmukta (liberated while living). Nisargadatta's jnani is unique: not only has the person-sense dissolved, but even the sense "I Am" has been transcended. The jnani abides as Parabrahman — prior to consciousness itself — while appearing to live an ordinary human life.

Love as the Nature of the Absolute

Often overlooked in intellectual treatments of Nisargadatta's teaching, yet central to what he lived

Love is not selective. Desire is selective. When the centre of selfishness collapses, love for all beings arises naturally — not as a practice but as one's actual nature.

"Life is love and love is life. What keeps the body together but love? What is desire, but love of the self? What is fear but the urge to protect? And what is knowledge but the love of truth?"

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

Wisdom says I am nothing—no qualities, no person, no centre. Love says I am everything—the consciousness in you is the same as in all. Between these two truths, life flows.

This love is not an emotion arising in a person; it is the very substance of consciousness when separation is seen through. Desire is selective, but love embraces all without distinction—in love there are no strangers. It requires no effort because it is not an achievement; it is simply what remains when the illusion of the separate self dissolves.

The consciousness in you and the consciousness in me, apparently two, are really one. Their seeking of unity is love. The love of the self in you is for the self in all—the two are one.

Liberation in This Life

This is not a permanent altered state to be defended. It is recognition of what always already is — the Absolute was never bound, never born, never in question. What changed is only the mistake: the error of identification has been seen through.

Nisargadatta himself died of throat cancer in 1981, continuing to teach from his sickbed.[1] He did not present death as a problem — the body's dissolution was simply the return of an appearance to its source.

"When you know you are nothing, you are everything. That is the only thing to know."

That which is prior to consciousness, prior to "I Am." The ultimate reality without qualities, without being or non-being.

Not a trick or punishment. The spontaneous appearance of the phenomenal universe on the Absolute. The mechanism of apparent forgetting.

Annamaya, Pranamaya, Manomaya, Vijnanamaya, Anandamaya. All appearances in consciousness, not the Self.

Consciousness-Space

The field of knowingness in which the entire universe exists. The "I Am-ness." Still an appearance, not the ultimate.

The abode of the Jnani. Prior to consciousness. In the Absolute there is no consciousness of "I am."

The Individual Soul

The false idea of a separate doer arising from wrong identification with the body-mind. Only Ishwara is doing anything.

Gunas
The Three Qualities

Sattva (clarity), Rajas (activity), Tamas (inertia). The qualities of nature upon which consciousness depends. When they are gone, the "I Am" goes.

How to Practice

A practical guide to Nisarga Yoga

Step 1
Locate the "I Am"

Find the bare sense of existing — not "I am this body" or "I am happy" — just the raw fact of being. This is the gateway.

Step 2
Abide Without Effort

Do not concentrate or strain. Simply rest in the recognition of "I Am." It is effortless because it is already here — you are not creating it.

Step 3
Watch Qualifiers Arise

Notice how the mind adds qualifiers: "I am tired," "I am the body." See these as overlays. Return to the pure "I Am" beneath them.

Step 4
See Through the Obstacles

When body-identification, conceptual thinking, or the sense of doership arise, do not fight them. See them as appearances in the "I Am."

Step 5
Let the "I Am" Dissolve

Eventually, even the "I Am" is seen as an appearance. It drops away by itself. What remains is the Absolute — Parabrahman. You are That.

Final
Nothing Was Ever Done

Nisargadatta reached that perspective through three years of devoted practice. The "do nothing" is the fruit, not the seed. The seed is: attend to the "I Am" with your whole being, until it reveals what is prior to itself.

"Just 'TO BE' and to remain steady with the awareness 'I AM' is the beginning and end of spiritual practice."

— Nisargadatta Maharaj

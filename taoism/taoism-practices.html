II

The Uncarved Block

Practice · Virtue · Simplicity

“The sage embraces the uncarved block. He does not show off, therefore he shines. He does not assert, therefore he is acknowledged.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 22

Practice as Non-Practice

The Practice of Not-Practicing

How to Cultivate the Uncarved Block

Taoism is often called the “practice of non-practice.” Unlike traditions that prescribe meditation schedules, ethical codes, or devotional rituals, the Taoist path is one of subtraction rather than addition. The sage does not accumulate virtues; she returns to the simplicity that was always there, before the ego carved itself out of the uncarved block.

This is not passivity. It means acting without the sense of a separate doer, desiring without grasping, and leading without dominating. The practice is to unlearn the habits of the conditioned mind — the endless striving, comparing, and self-justification that keep us trapped in illusion.

In this, the Taoist path mirrors the Advaitin teaching of sahaja and the Christian teaching of grace. You cannot earn the uncarved block. You can only stop carving it. As the Tao Te Ching says: “Do nothing, and yet nothing is left undone.”

Tao Te Ching · Chapter 48

“In the pursuit of learning, one gains day by day. In the pursuit of Tao, one loses day by day. One loses and loses until reaching non-action. When non-action is reached, nothing is left undone.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching (6th–4th century BCE)

Ramana Maharshi · Sahaja

“The natural state is not something to be attained. It is what remains when you stop seeking it.”

— Talks with Sri Ramana Maharshi

The Cycle of Return

The fourfold cycle of Taoist practice: alignment with Tao (wu wei) manifests as virtue (te), which expresses as natural spontaneity (ziran), returning to the uncarved block (p'u).

Effortless Action

Wu Wei · Doing Without Doership

The Heart of Taoist Practice

Wu wei (無為) is the most misunderstood term in Taoism. It does not mean indolence. It means acting in such perfect alignment with the Tao that no separate sense of ‘I am the doer’ arises. The water flows downhill without effort; the wind blows without intention; the sage acts without ego.

Wu wei is the Taoist equivalent of karma yoga in the Bhagavad Gita and the Christian “not my will but thine” (Luke 22:42). It is also the spontaneous activity of the realized being described by Ramana Maharshi and Nisargadatta Maharaj — one who has no personal agenda, no future to secure, no past to defend.

How does one practice wu wei? Not by trying. Trying to be effortless is a contradiction. Instead, one cultivates the inner conditions for it to arise: stillness of mind, clarity of perception, and radical trust in the Tao. The Zhuangzi tells of a butcher who cuts an ox so perfectly that his knife never dulls — because he follows the natural grain, not forcing his way. This is wu wei: skill so refined it becomes invisible.

“The perfect man uses his mind like a mirror — going after nothing, welcoming nothing, responding but not storing.”

— Zhuangzi, Chapter 7 (c. 4th century BCE)

“He who does nothing leaves nothing undone.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 48

Virtue · Integrity

Te · The Radiant Power of the Tao

Living from the Uncarved Block

Te (德) is the virtue, power, or integrity that flows naturally from alignment with the Tao. It is not a set of moral rules but a spontaneous radiance — like the fragrance of a flower, the warmth of the sun, or the clarity of a mountain stream. When one abides in the uncarved block, te manifests as compassion, humility, patience, and effortless effectiveness.

The Tao Te Ching distinguishes between higher te and lower te. Higher te does not know itself as virtue; it simply acts, and its actions are good. Lower te is contrived — it strains to be virtuous, and thus loses the Tao. This is the same distinction Jesus makes between the Pharisees who perform righteousness for show and the childlike heart that does good without self-consciousness.

In Advaita, this is the natural state (sahaja) of the jñani — who acts not from duty but from the spontaneous overflow of the Self. Nisargadatta says: “When the mind is still, the Self shines. That shining is te.”

Tao Te Ching · Chapter 38

“The man of higher te does not strive to be virtuous, therefore he is virtuous. The man of lower te never loses sight of being virtuous, therefore he is not virtuous.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching (6th–4th century BCE)

The Three Treasures

Lao Tzu's three treasures (Chapter 67): compassion (tz'u) yields courage, frugality (chien) yields generosity, and humility (not daring to be first) yields true leadership. Each flows from te, the virtue of the Tao.

The Three Treasures

Compassion · Frugality · Humility

The Ethical Radiance of the Uncarved Block

Lao Tzu's three treasures (Chapter 67) are not commandments. They are spontaneous expressions of one who has returned to the uncarved block. Because the sage does not separate herself from others, compassion flows naturally. Because she does not grasp at things, frugality is effortless. Because she has no ego to assert, humility is her default posture.

These three treasures form a complete ethical framework that mirrors the Christian virtues of faith, hope, and charity — but with a distinctively Taoist flavor. Compassion (tz'u) is the courage to act without self-protection. Frugality (chien) is the generosity that comes from not hoarding. Humility (not daring to be first) is the leadership that serves rather than dominates.

In the Vedantic tradition, these correspond to the natural qualities of the liberated being: karuṇā, santoṣa, and vinaya. In Christianity, they are the fruits of the Spirit: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, generosity, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control (Galatians 5:22–23).

“I have three treasures which I hold and keep. The first is compassion; the second is frugality; the third is humility. Compassion leads to courage; frugality leads to generosity; humility leads to leadership.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 67
Wu Wei in the World

Wu wei applied across four domains of life: work (action without attachment to outcome), relationships (listening without controlling), leadership (serving without dominating), and conflict (yielding without forcing).

Spontaneity

Ziran · Suchness

Acting from One's True Nature

Ziran (自然) is the quality of acting from one's own nature, without external compulsion or artificial striving. The sage does not imitate anyone; he is simply what he is, responding to each situation as it arises, like a mirror reflecting whatever passes before it.

Ziran is the Taoist equivalent of the Zen term kenshō and the Advaitin svarūpa. It is also what Christian mystics mean when they speak of “becoming who you truly are in God.” The path of ziran is the path of subtraction: removing the false layers of conditioning until only the authentic self remains — which is no self at all, but the Tao itself.

Zhuangzi illustrates ziran with the story of a hunchback catching cicadas with a stick, never missing. When asked his secret, he says: “I fix my mind on the cicada's wings, and the rest of the world disappears. I do not use my skill; the skill uses me.” This is ziran: action so perfectly aligned with the Tao that the doer vanishes into the doing.

“The Tao of heaven is to act without striving, to respond without speaking, to attract without calling, to plan without being obvious.”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching, Chapter 73
The Path of Subtraction

The Taoist path is one of subtraction, not addition. The conditioned self (ego, striving, grasping) is progressively unlearned, revealing the uncarved block (p'u), which then naturally flows into the Tao.

Living the Uncarved Block

The Sage in Daily Life

Wu Wei in Work, Relationships, and Governance

The practice of wu wei, te, and ziran is not limited to the meditation cushion. The Tao Te Ching was written for rulers and ministers, not just monks. The sage applies the same principles to governing a state, leading a team, raising a child, or cooking a meal.

  • In work: Do your task with full attention but without attachment to outcome. Let the action be its own reward. This is wu wei applied to labor.
  • In relationships: Listen without planning your response. Respond without needing to control the other. This is te as compassion.
  • In leadership: Lead by example, not by coercion. The best leader is the one whose presence is barely felt. When the work is done, the people say, “We did it ourselves.” (Tao Te Ching, Chapter 17)
  • In conflict: Do not meet force with force. Yield, and you will overcome. The soft overcomes the hard; the weak overcomes the strong. (Chapters 36, 78)

These are not strategies for manipulation. They are the natural expressions of a mind that has returned to the uncarved block — a mind that no longer needs to defend, assert, or grasp.

Tao Te Ching · Chapter 17

“The highest ruler is one whose existence is barely known. Next is one who is loved and praised. Next is one who is feared. Next is one who is despised. When the work is done and the task accomplished, the people say: ‘We did it ourselves.’”

— Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching (6th–4th century BCE)

Returning to the Root

The Eternal Tao · The Sage · The Natural State

"Returning to the root is stillness. Stillness is returning to the mandate. The mandate is the eternal. Knowing the eternal is illumination."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 16

The Eternal Tao

That Which Never Arrives nor Departs

The Ground of All Being

The Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao. Yet the eternal Tao is not distant — it is the silent ground of your awareness right now, the unchanging presence that witnesses every thought and sensation. To "return to the root" is to cease identifying with transient waves and abide as the ocean itself.

Lao Tzu describes this return in Chapter 16: "Empty the self completely; embrace perfect peace. The ten thousand things rise and fall while the Self watches their return. Returning to the root is stillness. Stillness is returning to the mandate. The mandate is the eternal. Knowing the eternal is illumination."

This is the Taoist equivalent of the Advaitin recognition of Atman as eternal and birthless, and of the Christian teaching that "Before Abraham was, I AM." The eternal Tao is not a being among beings; it is the being of all beings. To know it is not to acquire information but to recognize what has always been the case: you are not the body, not the mind, not the personality — you are the root itself.

The Return to the Root
All phenomena arise from and return to the single root. The sage watches their return without attachment.

The Death of the Ego

Wu Wo · No-Self

The Root of All Suffering

In Taoism, the root of suffering is not sin, ignorance, or karma — it is the false sense of a separate self (wu wo, 無我). The ego is the "carved block" — a limited, defended identity that believes it must protect itself, accumulate, and control. The sage has returned to the uncarved block: the self that is no-self.

Zhuangzi describes this as the "death of the heart-mind" — not the annihilation of awareness, but the dissolution of the false sense of a separate subject. When the ego dies, what remains is not a blank void but the luminous, responsive presence of the Tao itself. This is the "natural state" (sahaja in Advaita) and the "new creation" in Christianity.

Lao Tzu says: "The reason I have great trouble is that I have a self. If I have no self, what trouble do I have?" (Tao Te Ching, Chapter 13). This is the same teaching as the Buddha's anatta and Ramana Maharshi's "Who am I?" inquiry. The ego is not a real entity; it is a habit of identification. When that habit dissolves, the root is revealed.

The Dissolution of the Separate Self
The ego contracts around a defended center. The Tao expands without boundary — the self that is no-self.

The Sage

The One Who Does Not Dwell

Characteristics of the Awakened Being

The Taoist sage is not a superhuman or miracle-worker. She is utterly ordinary — except that she has no ego. She acts without striving, speaks without intention, and responds to each situation without premeditation. She is not trying to be good; the Tao flows through her without obstruction.

Lao Tzu lists the marks of the sage: "He does not show off, therefore he shines. He does not assert, therefore he is acknowledged. He does not boast, therefore he is given credit. He does not compete, therefore no one can compete with him." (Chapter 22). This is the Taoist equivalent of the Sermon on the Mount's Beatitudes — the paradox that the last shall be first, the meek shall inherit the earth.

The sage is not a recluse. The Tao Te Ching was written for rulers, not monks. The sage governs by wu wei: she sets an example of simplicity, humility, and compassion, and the people naturally align with the Tao. "When the work is done and the task accomplished, the people say: 'We did it ourselves.'" (Chapter 17).

In this, the Taoist sage mirrors the Christian servant-leader and the Advaitin ideal of the sthitaprajna. All traditions agree: the awakened being is not a special person; she is a person who has ceased to be special in any egoic sense. She is like water — soft, yielding, invisible — yet capable of wearing away stone.

The sage is like water: yielding, adaptable, and inexhaustible. She does not confront obstacles but flows around them.

The Natural State

Ziran · Sahaja · Suchness

The Unforced Expression of the Tao

The final recognition in Taoism is ziran (自然) — the state of being "self-so," not forced, not contrived, not striving. It is the Taoist equivalent of the Advaitin sahaja and the Zen "things as they are." Ziran is not something you attain; it is what remains when you stop trying to become something else.

Nisargadatta Maharaj said: "The natural state is not something to be achieved. It is what you are when you are not seeking." Lao Tzu says the same in Chapter 25: "The Tao models itself on ziran — it is that which is so of itself." The sage does not imitate anyone; she is simply what she is, responding to each moment as the moment requires. No effort, no pretense, no agenda.

This is the recognition that the seeker is the sought, the path is the destination, and the return to the root is not a movement in space or time — it is the simple, immediate, undeniable fact that you are already the Tao. The only obstacle is the belief that you are not. That belief dissolves in the light of self-inquiry, the fasting of the heart-mind, or the grace of the teaching.

The Four Stages · One Recognition
The four stages are not linear steps but simultaneous dimensions of a single awakening. Each stage already contains the root.

"The Tao does nothing, and yet nothing is left undone. If rulers could hold to the Tao, the ten thousand things would transform themselves."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 37

"The natural state is when the mind is at ease, not grasping, not rejecting — like the sky reflecting all things without effort."

— Adapted from Zhuangzi

The Journey Completed

From the Unnamed Way to the Root

Recapitulation of the Four Taoist Cartographies

The four pages of the Taoist cartography trace a complete path from apophatic negation to the final recognition of the natural state:

  • I · The Unnamed Way: The Tao that cannot be named — the apophatic threshold that shatters all conceptual grasping. The recognition that the Absolute is beyond all names, forms, and attributes.
  • II · The Uncarved Block: The practice of wu wei (effortless action), te (virtue), and the three treasures. The cultivation of natural qualities that arise when the ego subsides.
  • III · The Butterfly Dream: The radical non-duality of Zhuangzi — the collapse of self and other, the pivot of the Tao, the equality of all things, and the fasting of the heart-mind.
  • IV · Returning to the Root: The final recognition: abiding as the eternal Tao, the death of the ego, the sage's effortless life, and the natural state (ziran / sahaja) that was never lost and never needs to be gained.

These four stages are not linear steps; they are simultaneous dimensions of a single awakening. One who truly knows the unnamed way already abides in the root. One who practices the uncarved block is already dreaming the butterfly. The cartography is a finger pointing at the moon — not the moon itself. The final teaching of Taoism is that there is no final teaching: the Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao.

Tao Te Ching · Chapter 1

"The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao. The name that can be named is not the eternal name."

— Lao Tzu

The Unnamed Way

The Unnamed Way

Apophatic Threshold · The Tao Before All Names

"The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao. The name that can be named is not the eternal name."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 1 (Lao Tzu, 6th–4th c. BCE)

The Nameless Source

Beyond the Names of God

Radical Apophaticism · The Cloud of Unknowing in Taoist Form

The Tao Te Ching opens with a declaration that shatters all conceptual grasping. The Tao that can be spoken is not the eternal Tao. This is not a mere puzzle or a poetic flourish. It is a precise philosophical instrument — the same apophatic (via negativa) method found in Pseudo-Dionysius' Mystical Theology, in the Upanishads' neti-neti, and in The Cloud of Unknowing's instruction to abandon all images and concepts of God.

Lao Tzu does not say the Tao is unknowable in a skeptical sense. He says the Tao is unnameable — beyond the reach of language and logic, yet immediately present as the ground of all experience. The first chapter of the Tao Te Ching is the hermeneutic key to the entire Taoist tradition: any attempt to fix the Tao in words or doctrines is a betrayal of its living reality.

In this, Lao Tzu anticipates the Christian apophatic tradition by over five centuries. Pseudo-Dionysius (c. 500 CE) writes: "We pray to enter the darkness beyond light, and without seeing and without knowing, to see and to know that which is beyond all vision and all knowledge." The Cloud of Unknowing (14th century) instructs the seeker to "lift up your heart to God with a humble stirring of love, and mean God himself, and not what you can conceive about him."

The Tao Te Ching's first chapter is the same instruction, given in the 6th century BCE: abandon the named, and rest in the nameless.

— Lao Tzu

The Three‑Stage Hierarchy of the Tao

The teaching of Lao Tzu, as preserved in the Tao Te Ching and elaborated by Zhuangzi and the later Taoist tradition, reveals a clear hierarchy — though Lao Tzu would resist the very notion of hierarchy, for the Tao "does not take sides" and "gives birth to both good and evil":

  • 1. The Named World (You / Being) — The realm of distinctions, names, desires, and the ten thousand things. This is ordinary existence, where "when people see some things as beautiful, other things become ugly; when people see some things as good, other things become bad." Here, the individual identifies with body, mind, social role, and the endless pursuit of desires. This is the realm of the "named" — the Mother of all things, but not the Source itself.
  • 2. The Nameless Mystery (Wu / Non‑Being) — The realization that behind all names and forms lies the unnamable source. "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao; the name that can be named is not the eternal Name." Here, one "free from desire, realizes the mystery" — the subtle essence that precedes heaven and earth. This is the realm of meditation, of zuowang (sitting in forgetfulness), where the mind empties itself of concepts and distinctions. Yet even this "mystery" is not the final level, for "mystery and manifestations arise from the same source."
  • 3. The Tao Itself — Beyond Being and Non‑Being — The ultimate reality that transcends even the duality of named and nameless, being and non‑being, mystery and manifestation. This is "darkness within darkness, the gateway to all understanding." It is not a state, not a void, not a presence, not an absence. It is "the unobstructed breath," the "unlimited father and mother of all limited things," the "subtle reality of the universe" that "cannot be described." As one translation puts it: "The Tao that can be taoed is not the entire Tao."

The Radical Move: Wu Wei — The Action of Non‑Action

What makes Lao Tzu's teaching unique is that the path to the Absolute is not through effort, inquiry, or self‑emptying love, but through Wu Wei — non‑action, effortless action, action that arises spontaneously from alignment with the Tao. This is not passivity or laziness. It is the action of water, which "overcomes the hard and strong through softness"; the action of the sage, who "acts without doing anything and teaches without saying anything."

The Tao Te Ching describes this paradox repeatedly: "The Master leads by emptying people's minds and filling their cores, by weakening their ambition and toughening their resolve." "He helps people lose everything they know, everything they desire, and creates confusion in those who think that they know." "Practice not‑doing, and everything will fall into place."

This is the deepest cut: not that one must transcend the world through spiritual effort, but that one must stop trying to transcend it. The very effort to reach the Tao is what keeps one from it. As the text says: "Whoever free from attachment looks inward with detachment attains the vision of the Unmanifest; whoever is ego‑bound and clings to greed sees but the outer show." Yet even "looking inward" is a doing — the sage simply is, like the valley that receives all waters without striving.

The Nature of the Tao

The Tao is not:

  • A deity or divine being ("It is older than God")
  • A state of consciousness (consciousness is one of the "ten thousand things" that arise from it)
  • The witness or observer (the witness is still a mode of perception)
  • An experience (all experiences are transient; the Tao "can be experienced" but "is not true" in the sense of being an object of experience)
  • A void or emptiness (it is "empty yet infinitely capable; the more you use it, the more it produces")
  • Something to be attained (it is "always present," "hidden but always present")

The Tao is described through negation and paradox: "It is like a well: used but never used up. It is like the eternal void: filled with infinite possibilities." "It is hidden but always present. I don't know who gave birth to it. It is older than God." "The Tao doesn't take sides; it gives birth to both good and evil." "The Tao is like a bellows: it is empty yet infinitely capable."

The Path to the Absolute: Returning to the Uncarved Block

Lao Tzu's method is not inquiry, not devotion, not meditation in the yogic sense, but return — a turning back to what was never left:

  • "Return is the movement of the Tao" — The way forward is the way back
  • "Yielding is the way of the Tao" — The soft overcomes the hard
  • "Be like water" — The supreme good is like water, which benefits all things without contention
  • "The Master does nothing, yet leaves nothing undone" — Wu Wei in its perfection
  • "The Uncarved Block" (Pu) — The state before the mind carves the world into names and forms

The sage "has but doesn't possess, acts but doesn't expect. When her work is done, she forgets it. That is why it lasts forever." This forgetting is not amnesia but the natural state of one who has returned to the Tao — there is no one left to remember or forget, no one left to claim ownership of actions. The work flows through the sage as water flows through a valley, leaving no trace.

The Paradox of Teaching

Like the Absolute of Nisargadatta, the Divine Ground of Jesus, and the Self of Ramana, the Tao cannot be taught — and Lao Tzu knew this better than any of them. The entire Tao Te Ching is a prolonged acknowledgment of its own inadequacy: "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao"; "Even the finest teaching is not the Tao itself"; "Even the finest name is insufficient to define it"; "Without words, the Tao can be experienced, and without a name, it can be known."

The text uses 5,000 words to say what cannot be said in 5,000 words. It employs paradox, metaphor, and negation not to build a philosophy but to dismantle the reader's philosophical constructions. As one commentator notes: "We can only penetrate to an understanding of it when we forget ourselves, even our desire to understand it, to shed all grasping and go beyond thought, words and names. Otherwise we only understand what is on the surface, what is superficial, failing to penetrate to the true essence of things."

The Taoist tradition developed elaborate practices — neidan (internal alchemy), qigong, meditation — but these are fingers pointing at the moon. The deepest level remains what Zhuangzi called the "Great Thoroughfare": a state in which "the distinctions of self and other, subject and object, have dissolved into the undifferentiated flow of existence."

Final Understanding

The deepest level of Lao Tzu's teaching is that what you fundamentally are is not the separate self struggling to find the Tao, but the Tao itself, temporarily obstructed by its own naming and desiring. The "I" that seeks the Tao is itself a name, a carving of the Uncarved Block. When the carving ceases, what remains is not a new state but the original state — the valley that has always received the waters, the bellows that has always been empty yet infinitely capable, the darkness within darkness that has always been the gateway to all understanding.

As the Tao Te Ching says: "Returning is the movement of the Tao. Yielding is the way of the Tao. All things are born of being. Being is born of non‑being." And deeper still: the non‑being from which being is born is itself born of the Tao — which is neither being nor non‑being, neither named nor nameless, neither this nor that, yet the source of all this and all that.

The sage does not reach this level. The sage is this level — not as an achievement, but as a return. "The ten thousand things rise and fall while the Self watches their return." The Self here is not the individual self but the Tao itself, watching through the sage's empty eyes, acting through the sage's effortless hands, flowing through the sage's still heart like water finding its level, like the valley receiving the mountain's streams, like darkness receiving light without being diminished by it.

In the end, Lao Tzu's teaching is the most radical of all: there is nothing to do, nowhere to go, no one to become. The Tao is already flowing. The only question is whether you are flowing with it — or, more precisely, whether you have stopped trying to swim against the current that was never flowing in any direction at all.

The Pivot of the Tao

The Uncarved Block & The Cloud

P'u · The Simplicity Before Distinction

One of Lao Tzu's most powerful images is the p'u (樸) — the uncarved block of wood. Before the carpenter carves it into a bowl, a tool, or a statue, the block has no fixed purpose, no assigned name, no limited function. It is pure potential, undivided, whole. To carve it is to create a distinction — this thing is for eating, that thing is for fighting. The carved object has a name and a use, but it has also lost its original wholeness.

The sage, says Lao Tzu, "returns to the uncarved block." He abandons all artificial roles, ambitions, and conceptual identities. He becomes like a child — not in naivety, but in the absence of a constructed self. The p'u is the Taoist version of sahaja (natural state) in Advaita, and of Jesus' instruction to "become like little children" (Matthew 18:3).

The Cloud of Unknowing says the same thing in the language of Christian mysticism: "For the first time, you may feel that you know nothing at all, not even what God is. This is the cloud of unknowing that lies between you and God." That cloud is not a barrier; it is the state of having returned to the uncarved block — before the mind has carved God into a concept, a name, or an image.

"The sage embraces the uncarved block. He is the example for all under heaven. He does not show off, therefore he shines. He does not assert, therefore he is acknowledged."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 22

"Become like little children. For the kingdom of heaven belongs to such as these."

— Matthew 18:3

The Three Treasures

Compassion · Frugality · Humility

The Ethical Expression of the Unnamed Way

In Chapter 67 of the Tao Te Ching, Lao Tzu names three treasures that the sage holds dear:

  • Tz'u (慈) — Compassion or love. The root of courage, because one who is compassionate acts fearlessly for the sake of others.
  • Chien (儉) — Frugality or moderation. The root of generosity, because one who lives simply has abundance to give.
  • Pu Kan Wei Tian Hsia Hsien (不敢為天下先) — Not daring to be first in the world. The root of leadership, because one who does not assert superiority becomes the true vessel of the Tao.

These three treasures are the ethical expression of the apophatic threshold. Compassion is the heart's recognition that no being is separate from the Tao. Frugality is the mind's recognition that grasping and accumulation are illusory. Humility is the ego's recognition that it is not the doer.

In Christian terms, these are faith, hope, and charity — but more precisely, they are the fruits of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22–23). In Advaita, they are the natural qualities of the jnani whose ego has dissolved. The three treasures are not commandments; they are symptoms of awakening. One who has touched the unnamed way cannot help but be compassionate, moderate, and humble.

"I have three treasures which I hold and keep. The first is compassion; the second is frugality; the third is humility. Compassion leads to courage; frugality leads to generosity; humility leads to leadership."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 67

"Look — it cannot be seen: call it colorless. Listen — it cannot be heard: call it noiseless. Reach — it cannot be held: call it formless. These three are beyond all inquiry — they are one, deep and dark. The dark within the dark — the gate of all mystery."

— Tao Te Ching, Chapter 14 · The Cloud of Unknowing

Comparative Thresholds

The Tao and the Logos

Two Names for the Nameless

John's Gospel opens with "In the beginning was the Word (Logos), and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." The Logos is the principle of order, reason, and manifestation — the named Tao that is the "mother of ten thousand things." But the Gospel of John also insists that "no one has seen God at any time" — the nameless Father remains beyond all names.

Thus the Christian tradition, like Taoism, holds together the apophatic (God beyond all names) and the kataphatic (God revealed in the Word). Lao Tzu's first chapter does exactly the same: the nameless Tao is the origin of heaven and earth; the named Tao is the mother of ten thousand things. The two are not two different Taos. They are the same reality seen from two perspectives: in itself (noumenon) and as it appears (phenomenon).

This is precisely the Advaitin distinction between Parabrahman (the Absolute without attributes) and Brahman (the Absolute with qualities of existence, consciousness, bliss). The Tao that can be named is the Tao with form; the eternal Tao is the Tao without form. Yet they are not separate. As the Tao Te Ching says in Chapter 42: "The Tao gives birth to one; one gives birth to two; two gives birth to three; three gives birth to the ten thousand things." The nameless Tao is the source of the named Tao, and the named Tao is the manifestation of the nameless.

Tao Te Ching · Chapter 42

"The Tao gives birth to one. One gives birth to two. Two gives birth to three. Three gives birth to the ten thousand things. The ten thousand things carry yin and embrace yang."

— Lao Tzu

John 1:1–5 · Advaita Reading

"In the beginning was the Word (the named Tao), and the Word was with God (the nameless Tao), and the Word was God. All things were made through him."

— Gospel of John

The Quantum Mystery

What Exists Before Observation?

Superposition · The Wavefunction · The Double-Slit Experiment

Quantum mechanics is the most precisely tested theory in the history of science. It predicts the magnetic moment of the electron to one part in a trillion. It powers the transistors in your phone, the lasers in your eyes, the MRI that reads your brain. And yet — after a century of debate — we still do not agree on what it means. The mathematics is unambiguous. The interpretation is not.

The heart of the mystery is superposition — the claim that a quantum system can exist in multiple states at once, not as a mixture (like a coin spinning in the air) but as a genuine coexistence of all possibilities. An electron is not "here or there" before measurement. It is here and there, in a state described by a wavefunction that assigns probability amplitudes to every possible outcome.

This is not a limitation of our knowledge. It is not that we don't know where the electron is. According to the standard formulation, the electron has no definite position until a measurement is performed. The wavefunction is not a description of our ignorance; it is the complete description of the physical system. Everything that can be known about the electron is encoded in its wavefunction. And the wavefunction says: it is in many places at once.

Quantum Mechanics · Postulate I

"The state of a quantum system is completely specified by its wavefunction Ψ(x,t), which contains all the information that can be known about the system. The wavefunction evolves deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation."

— Standard Formulation

Chandogya Upanishad · 6.2.1

"In the beginning, my dear, this world was just Being, one only, without a second. To be sure, some people say: 'In the beginning this world was just non-being, one only, without a second; from that non-being, being was produced.' But how could that be?"

— Uddalaka to Svetaketu

The Double-Slit Experiment — A Meditation

Imagine a wall with two narrow slits. Behind it, a screen. You fire electrons one at a time — single particles, separated by seconds, each traveling alone through the apparatus. Common sense says each electron must pass through either the left slit or the right slit. The screen should show two bands, corresponding to the two paths.

It does not. The screen shows an interference pattern — alternating bright and dark bands, the signature of waves passing through both slits simultaneously and interfering with themselves. The electron, fired alone, behaves as if it passes through both slits at once. It interferes with itself. The pattern on the screen is the mathematical superposition of two wavefunctions — one from each slit — adding and canceling.

Now place a detector at one slit. Ask: which path did the electron take? The moment you know, the interference pattern vanishes. The screen shows two bands — the classical result. The act of observation, of acquiring which-path information, destroys the superposition. The wavefunction "collapses" from a superposition of both paths to a definite single path.

Diagram · The Double-Slit Experiment
A single electron, fired alone, passes through both slits simultaneously and interferes with itself — creating an interference pattern of bright and dark bands.

But the strangeness does not end there. The delayed-choice experiments of John Wheeler show that you can decide after the electron has passed the slits whether to measure which path it took. The decision, made after the fact, still determines the result. The electron seems to "know" in advance whether you will ask the question. Or rather: the question you ask determines what the electron "was" all along.

This is not a trick of language. It is the empirical result of decades of careful experiment. The electron does not have a hidden path that we merely fail to detect. Bell's theorem and the Aspect experiments have ruled out "local hidden variables" — the idea that the electron has a definite but unknown state before measurement. The superposition is not a description of our ignorance. It is, according to the standard formulation, the complete and final description of the physical reality.

Diagram · Delayed-Choice Experiment
In Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment, the decision to insert a second beam splitter — determining whether the photon took one path or both — is made after the photon has already passed the first splitter. The choice retroactively determines what the photon "was."

What are we to make of this? The interpretations of quantum mechanics are not competing theories in the usual scientific sense. They make the same predictions for all experiments. They are, rather, competing metaphysics — different ways of understanding what the mathematics means, what kind of reality it describes, what the relationship is between the observer and the observed.

The Interpretations — Four Voices

The mathematics of quantum mechanics is fixed. The meaning is not. Here are four of the most influential interpretations, each offering a different metaphysics of the unmeasured ground.

C

Copenhagen · The Instrumentalist View

Niels Bohr and Werner Heisenberg. The wavefunction is not a description of reality but a tool for prediction. It encodes the probabilities of measurement outcomes, nothing more. The "quantum realm" has no independent existence; it is defined only by the classical apparatus that measures it. The boundary between quantum and classical is movable but unavoidable. There is no deeper reality to be found. As Bohr famously said: "There is no quantum world. There is only an abstract quantum physical description."

M

Many-Worlds · The Realist View

Hugh Everett III (1957). The wavefunction is real, and it never collapses. Every quantum measurement causes the universe to branch into parallel worlds, one for each possible outcome. You see the electron here; your other self sees it there. Both are equally real. The appearance of collapse is an illusion created by the branching of consciousness. The universe is an unimaginably vast tree of worlds, all equally actual, all evolving deterministically according to the Schrödinger equation. No special role for the observer. No mystery — just an infinity of worlds.

Q

QBism · The Agent-Centric View

Christopher Fuchs and collaborators. The wavefunction encodes your beliefs as an agent navigating an uncertain world. It is not about the electron; it is about what you should expect. Quantum mechanics is a user's manual for an agent embedded in a world that does not reduce to objective states. The "collapse" is simply the update of your beliefs upon gaining new information. The universe is not a wavefunction; it is a set of experiences, and the wavefunction is how we reason about them. This is the most radical interpretation: it makes quantum mechanics about us, not about the world.

R

Relational QM · The Network View

Carlo Rovelli. There is no absolute quantum state. An electron has a position relative to a detector, a momentum relative to another apparatus. Properties are always relations, never intrinsic. The universe is a network of events, not a container of objects. This echoes the Buddhist doctrine of pratītyasamutpāda (dependent origination) and the relational metaphysics of Leibniz. The "state" is not a property of the system but a property of the relation between system and observer. Remove the observer, and you remove not the electron but the position of the electron.

Erwin Schrödinger · 1926

"If we are going to be so austere as to describe a physical system by a wavefunction, then we must accept that the system can be in a superposition of states. But if a system can be in a superposition, then a cat can be both alive and dead. And that is absurd."

— The Schrödinger's Cat Paradox

Niels Bohr · 1927

"The quantum postulate implies that any observation of atomic phenomena will involve an interaction with the agency of observation not to be neglected. Consequently, evidence obtained under different experimental conditions cannot be comprehended within a single picture."

— The Complementarity Principle

The Mystical Parallels — The Unmeasured in Contemplative Traditions

The superposition is not merely a physical curiosity. It is a metaphysical threshold — the place where the apparent world dissolves into possibility, where the distinction between "this" and "that" has not yet been drawn. Every mystical tradition has a name for this threshold.

Advaita Vedanta — Brahman Before Māyā

In Advaita Vedanta, the ultimate reality is Brahman — pure consciousness, without attributes, without division. The world of multiplicity is māyā, a superimposition upon the non-dual ground. The individual self (ātman) is non-different from Brahman; the appearance of separation is the fundamental ignorance (avidyā).

The superposition is the unmanifest Brahman — the state before vivarta (superimposition), before the wavefunction "collapses" into the apparent world. The electron in superposition is not "here or there"; Brahman is not "this or that." The measurement is the act of vivarta — the superimposition of name and form upon the formless. The jñāni (knower) is the one who sees through the superimposition, who recognizes that the multiplicity was never real, that the superposition was always the truth.

"Brahman is the only reality, the world is illusion, and the individual soul is non-different from Brahman."

— Adi Shankara · Brahma Sutra Bhashya

Kabbalah — Ein Sof Before Tzimtzum

In Lurianic Kabbalah, the Infinite (Ein Sof) is the ground of all being, prior to all differentiation. The Tzimtzum is the primordial contraction — the Infinite withdrawing from a point within itself to create the void in which the finite world can appear. The wavefunction is the Ein Sof in its uncontracted state: infinite possibility, all states coexisting, no distinction drawn.

The "collapse" of the wavefunction is the Tzimtzum in microcosm — the drawing of a boundary, the creation of a "this" by excluding a "that." Every measurement is a repetition of the primordial contraction. The tzimtzum is not a one-time event but the ongoing process by which the infinite becomes finite, the unmeasured becomes measured, the superposition becomes the world.

"Before the creation of the world, the Infinite filled all. When it arose in His simple will to create worlds, He constricted His infinity, and a void was formed."

— Etz Chaim · Introduction

Jesus' Teaching — "Before Abraham Was, I Am"

In the Gospel of John, Jesus declares: "Before Abraham was, I am" (John 8:58). The Greek is prin Abraham genesthai, egō eimi — not "I was" but "I am," the eternal present, the name of God revealed to Moses at the burning bush. This is the unconditioned "I" prior to all particular identities, prior to all historical becoming.

The superposition is the "I am" before the world divides into "I am this" and "I am that." The measurement is the fall into particularity — the "I am a carpenter," "I am a Jew," "I am the Son of Man." The mystical teaching is that beneath all these particular identities, the unconditioned "I am" remains. The electron does not lose its superposition by being measured; it simply appears to have a definite state. The mystic does not lose the infinite by being born; she simply appears to be a finite self.

"The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not comprehended it."

— John 1:5

Gnosticism — The Pleroma Before the Fall

In Gnostic cosmology, the Pleroma (Fullness) is the realm of the divine Aeons — perfect, undifferentiated, complete. The "fall" is the emergence of deficiency, the creation of the material world by the Demiurge (Yaldabaoth), who mistakenly believes himself to be the supreme God. The material world is the "collapse" of the Pleroma into multiplicity — the superposition reduced to a single, deficient outcome.

The Gnostic's task is anamnesis (recollection) — the recognition that the material world is not the true reality, that the Pleroma remains intact behind the illusion of separation. The "measurement" is the Demiurge's error; the "superposition" is the truth that the Gnostic recovers. The scattered sparks of the Pleroma are the entangled remnants of the original unity, waiting to be gathered and returned.

"The kingdom is inside you and outside you. When you know yourselves, then you will be known, and you will understand that you are children of the living Father. But if you do not know yourselves, then you live in poverty, and you are the poverty."

— Gospel of Thomas · Logion 3

The "Shut Up and Calculate" Interlude

Not all physicists engage with interpretation. Many adopt what David Mermin called the "shut up and calculate" approach — use the mathematics, make predictions, verify experiments, and leave the metaphysics to philosophers. This is a respectable position. Quantum mechanics works. It is the most successful theory in the history of science. Why worry about what it "means"?

But the question does not go away. The mathematics is not self-interpreting. Every physicist, in choosing how to teach the theory, in deciding what language to use, in framing the experiments, is making interpretive commitments. The "shut up and calculate" physicist is not avoiding interpretation; she is adopting a pragmatic interpretation — the mathematics is a tool, and tools do not need to be understood, only used.

The contemplative traditions would recognize this as a form of vairāgya (dispassion) — the refusal to be caught in the net of concepts. But they would also warn that the refusal to ask the question can become a refusal to see. The physicist who calculates without asking what the calculation means is like the mystic who meditates without asking what the meditation reveals. Both are valid practices. Both have limits.

"I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics. Do not keep saying to yourself, if you can possibly avoid it, 'But how can it be like that?' because you will get 'down the drain' into a blind alley from which nobody has yet escaped."

— Richard Feynman · The Character of Physical Law (1965)

The Human Thread

The Physicists Who Sought the Mystical

Schrödinger · Bohm · Wheeler · Capra

The engagement between quantum mechanics and mysticism is not merely an analogy imposed by outsiders. Several of the most influential physicists of the twentieth century were explicit about the connection. Their journeys — intellectual, spiritual, sometimes tortured — form a hidden thread in the history of physics.

Erwin Schrödinger and Vedanta

Erwin Schrödinger (1887–1961), the creator of the wave equation that bears his name, was deeply influenced by Vedanta. In his essay "What is Life?" and later in "My View of the World," he argued that the apparent multiplicity of consciousness is an illusion — that there is only one consciousness, one Self, which appears as many through the mechanism of individuation. He explicitly identified this with the Advaita doctrine of Brahman and ātman.

"The multiplicity is only apparent," he wrote. "In truth, there is only one mind." This was not a poetic afterthought. It was the philosophical framework within which he understood his own equation. The wavefunction, describing a single quantum system that encompasses all possibilities, was for Schrödinger a mathematical image of the non-dual consciousness he had found in the Upanishads.

David Bohm and the Implicate Order

David Bohm (1917–1992) was a student of Oppenheimer, a collaborator with Einstein, and — after his exile from McCarthy-era America — a close associate of Jiddu Krishnamurti. Their dialogues, published as "The Ending of Time" and "The Limits of Thought," are among the most remarkable encounters between a physicist and a mystic in the twentieth century.

Bohm's implicate order is the idea that the apparent world of separate objects (the "explicate order") is a projection of a deeper, enfolded reality in which everything is connected to everything else. The quantum potential, which guides particles in his pilot-wave theory, is a manifestation of the implicate order. The universe is not a collection of things but a process of enfoldment and unfoldment — a cosmic dance in which the whole is present in every part.

This is not merely physics. It is a metaphysics that Bohm developed in dialogue with Krishnamurti's teaching on the ending of psychological time, the dissolution of the self, and the direct perception of "what is." Bohm's physics and Krishnamurti's meditation were, for both men, aspects of the same inquiry.

John Wheeler and the Participatory Universe

John Wheeler (1911–2008) coined the term "black hole," supervised Richard Feynman's doctoral thesis, and in his later years developed the doctrine of the participatory universe. The universe, Wheeler argued, is not a machine that runs independently of observers. It is a "self-excited circuit" — a loop in which the observer participates in the creation of the reality that creates the observer.

Wheeler's famous question — "Why existence?" — was his dying meditation. He believed that the ultimate explanation of quantum mechanics would require a new understanding of consciousness, information, and the nature of observation. His "it from bit" doctrine — the idea that every physical entity derives its meaning from information — is a form of idealism that would have been recognized by Bishop Berkeley and by the Vedantins.

Fritjof Capra and the Tao of Physics

Fritjof Capra's "The Tao of Physics" (1975) was the book that introduced a generation to the parallels between quantum mechanics and Eastern mysticism. It has been criticized — rightly — for oversimplifying both physics and mysticism, for drawing parallels that are more poetic than rigorous, for ignoring the differences between traditions. But it was also a genuine attempt to bridge two worlds that had been separated by the Cartesian split.

The critics of "quantum mysticism" — Victor Stenger, Meera Nanda, and others — have pointed out that the parallels are often forced, that the mathematics of quantum mechanics does not require mystical interpretation, that the mystics did not anticipate the physics. These criticisms are valid. But they do not exhaust the question. The question is not whether quantum mechanics proves mysticism, but whether the two forms of knowledge — the mathematical and the contemplative — can illuminate each other. The physicists who sought the mystical were not bad scientists. They were scientists who recognized that the boundaries of their discipline were not the boundaries of reality.

David Bohm · 1980

"The implicate order is not merely a physical theory but a way of looking at reality. It suggests that the universe is an unbroken whole, and that the division into parts is an abstraction that we make for practical purposes."

— Wholeness and the Implicate Order

John Wheeler · 1990

"It is not that the universe is a machine that runs independently of us. The universe is a self-excited circuit. The observer is as essential to the creation of the universe as the universe is to the creation of the observer."

— The Participatory Universe

From the unmeasured ground of superposition, we now turn to the act of looking itself — the measurement, the collapse, the observer who cannot be removed from the observed. The question is no longer "What exists before observation?" but "What happens when we look?"

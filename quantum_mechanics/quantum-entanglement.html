Spooky Action at a Distance

What Connects What Appears Separate?

Entanglement · Non-Locality · The EPR Paradox

Albert Einstein never reconciled himself to quantum mechanics. In 1935, he published a paper with Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen — the famous EPR paradox — arguing that quantum mechanics must be incomplete. The theory, they claimed, predicted correlations between distant particles that seemed to require faster-than-light communication. This was impossible, they argued. Therefore, quantum mechanics must be missing something — some "hidden variables" that would restore a classical, local, deterministic picture of reality.

Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance" (spukhafte Fernwirkung). He believed that the universe was fundamentally local — that what happens here cannot instantly affect what happens there. This was the principle of local realism: objects have definite properties whether or not they are observed (realism), and influences cannot travel faster than light (locality). EPR seemed to show that quantum mechanics violated at least one of these principles. Einstein assumed it was locality — that quantum mechanics was merely incomplete, and a deeper theory would restore local realism.

He was wrong. But it took nearly thirty years to prove it.

Einstein, Podolsky & Rosen · 1935

"No reasonable definition of reality could be expected to permit this... If, without in any way disturbing a system, we can predict with certainty the value of a physical quantity, then there exists an element of physical reality corresponding to this physical quantity."

— Can Quantum-Mechanical Description Be Considered Complete?

Erwin Schrödinger · 1935

"I would not call it one but rather the characteristic trait of quantum mechanics, the one that enforces its entire departure from classical lines of thought."

— On the Present Situation in Quantum Mechanics (coining "entanglement")

Bell's Theorem — The Proof That Einstein Lost

In 1964, the Irish physicist John Stewart Bell published a theorem that would settle the EPR debate — not by experiment, but by mathematics. Bell showed that any theory that satisfies local realism — any theory in which particles have definite properties and influences cannot travel faster than light — must satisfy a set of inequalities. Quantum mechanics predicts violations of these inequalities. Therefore, if quantum mechanics is correct, local realism is false.

The proof is remarkably simple in concept. Imagine two entangled particles, A and B, flying apart in opposite directions. Each particle has a property that can be measured — say, spin along some axis. The measurements can be made along different axes, chosen randomly by the experimenters. Bell's inequality puts an upper bound on the correlations between the results, assuming local realism. Quantum mechanics predicts correlations that exceed this bound.

The mathematics is unambiguous. The logic is airtight. If quantum mechanics is correct — and it has been tested to unprecedented precision — then the universe is either non-local (influences can travel faster than light, or no speed limit at all) or non-realist (particles do not have definite properties before measurement). Or both. What it cannot be is both local and realist. Einstein's assumption that quantum mechanics was merely incomplete — that a deeper theory would restore local realism — was mathematically ruled out.

Bell himself was not entirely satisfied. He hoped that his theorem would lead to a deeper theory — perhaps one that was non-local but still deterministic, like David Bohm's pilot-wave theory. But the theorem stands: local realism is dead. The universe is not a collection of separate objects with definite properties, interacting only through local influences. It is something stranger.

The Experiments — From Aspect to Zeilinger

Bell's theorem was a mathematical proof. It required experimental verification. The experiments that followed are among the most beautiful and significant in the history of physics.

1972 · Stuart Freedman & John Clauser
The First Bell Test

Using pairs of entangled photons from calcium atoms, Freedman and Clauser measured correlations that violated Bell's inequality. The experiment was crude by modern standards — limited detector efficiency, imperfect source — but the result was clear: quantum mechanics, not local realism, was correct. The "loopholes" (ways that local realism could still be saved) were large enough that skeptics could dismiss the result. But the door was open.

1982 · Alain Aspect, Jean Dalibard & Gérard Roger
The Aspect Experiments — Closing the Locality Loophole

Aspect's experiments at the Institut d'Optique in Orsay were the turning point. The key innovation was the use of fast switching — the measurement settings were changed while the photons were in flight, so that no signal, even at the speed of light, could communicate the setting from one detector to the other. This closed the "locality loophole" — the possibility that the detectors were somehow coordinating their settings through subluminal communication. The correlations persisted. Non-locality was real.

1998 · Anton Zeilinger, Gregor Weihs, Thomas Jennewein & Markus Aspelmeyer
The Zeilinger Experiment — Closing the Detection Loophole

Zeilinger's group in Innsbruck used entangled photons with high-efficiency detectors and random setting choices generated by quantum processes themselves. The detection efficiency was high enough to close the "detection loophole" — the possibility that the correlations were an artifact of only detecting a biased subset of photons. The result: Bell's inequality was violated by dozens of standard deviations. The probability of local realism being correct was less than one in a trillion.

2015 · Hensen, Giustina, Shalm & Zeilinger (Delft, Vienna, Boulder)
The Loophole-Free Bell Tests

Three independent groups, using different technologies (nitrogen-vacancy centers in diamond, entangled photons with superconducting detectors, and photon-atom entanglement), simultaneously published loophole-free Bell tests. All three closed both the locality and detection loopholes in the same experiment. The result was unambiguous: local realism is false. The universe is non-local, or non-realist, or both. Nature does not respect Einstein's intuitions.

2017 · Chinese Academy of Sciences (Micius Satellite)
Entanglement from Space — 1200 Kilometers

The Micius quantum satellite beamed entangled photon pairs to ground stations separated by 1200 kilometers. The correlations persisted over this vast distance, with the locality loophole closed by the Earth's rotation itself — the measurement settings were chosen so that no signal, even at light speed, could travel between the stations during the experiment. The universe's non-locality is not a laboratory curiosity. It is a feature of reality at all scales.

Quantum Teleportation — The Transfer of State

Entanglement is not merely a philosophical curiosity. It is a physical resource. In 1993, Bennett et al. showed that entanglement could be used to teleport a quantum state — to transfer the complete quantum information of one particle to another, without physically moving the particle itself.

The protocol is elegant. Alice and Bob share an entangled pair. Alice also has a third particle in an unknown state that she wants to send to Bob. She performs a joint measurement on her two particles (the one she wants to send and her half of the entangled pair). This measurement destroys the original state — the no-cloning theorem forbids copying quantum states — but produces two classical bits of information. Alice sends these bits to Bob through ordinary channels (phone, email, light signal). Bob uses them to apply a specific transformation to his half of the entangled pair. The result: his particle is now in the exact state that Alice's original particle was in. The state has been teleported.

The information travels classically — no faster-than-light communication. But the correlation that makes the protocol work is non-local. The entangled pair "knows" about each other instantaneously. The classical communication is merely the "key" that unlocks the information already present in Bob's particle. Without the entanglement, the classical bits are meaningless. Without the classical bits, the entanglement cannot be used to transfer information. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient.

The Interpretations — What Does Non-Locality Mean?

Entanglement is experimentally proven. Non-locality is real. But what does it mean? The interpretations of quantum mechanics offer different answers.

C

Copenhagen · Correlations Without Causation

Bohr's response. The correlations are real, but they do not involve any "action" or "influence" traveling between the particles. The two particles are described by a single, non-separable wavefunction. A measurement on one particle is a measurement on the whole system. The "collapse" is not a physical process but an update of knowledge. There is no faster-than-light signaling because no information is transmitted. The correlation is a property of the description, not of the world. This is the most widely held view among working physicists, though it leaves many unsatisfied.

P

Pilot-Wave (Bohm) · Non-Locality as Real Influence

David Bohm's interpretation. Particles have definite positions at all times, guided by a "pilot wave" (the quantum potential) that is instantaneously connected across space. The non-locality is explicit and physical: the pilot wave of one particle is affected by the measurement of the other, instantly, regardless of distance. This restores determinism and realism at the cost of explicit non-locality. The universe is a single, indivisible whole. Every particle is connected to every other through the quantum potential. Bohm developed this in dialogue with Jiddu Krishnamurti, explicitly linking it to a holistic, mystical worldview.

M

Many-Worlds · All Outcomes in Parallel Branches

Everett's interpretation. There is no collapse, no non-local influence. When Alice measures her particle, the universe branches. In one branch, Alice gets "up" and Bob's particle is "down." In another, Alice gets "down" and Bob's is "up." The correlation is not a causal connection but a branching structure — the two outcomes are always paired because they are the two branches of the same split. No faster-than-light signaling because no signal is sent; the "correlation" is simply the fact that the branches are correlated. The price is an infinity of parallel worlds.

T

Transactional · Waves Travel Backward in Time

John Cramer's interpretation. The wavefunction is not a single wave but a "transaction" between an offer wave (traveling forward in time) and a confirmation wave (traveling backward). The entangled correlation is established at the moment of emission, through a handshake across time. The measurement does not "affect" the distant particle; it completes a transaction that was initiated in the past. Time-symmetric, elegant, and deeply strange. The future affects the past as much as the past affects the future.

The Mystical Parallels — The Hidden Connection in Contemplative Traditions

Non-locality is not merely a physical curiosity. It is a metaphysical threshold — the place where the apparent separation of things dissolves into a hidden unity, where what happens "here" is not independent of what happens "there," where the whole is present in every part. Every mystical tradition has a name for this connection.

Advaita Vedanta — Brahman Is One Without a Second

In Advaita Vedanta, the ultimate reality is Brahman — one without a second (ekam eva advitiyam). The apparent multiplicity of the world is māyā, a superimposition upon the non-dual ground. The individual self (ātman) is non-different from Brahman; the appearance of separation is the fundamental ignorance (avidyā).

Entanglement is the experimental proof of non-duality. Two particles, measured light-years apart, instantly correlate their states. This is not "two particles connected by a hidden signal." It is one reality appearing as two. The measurement of one is the measurement of the whole. The jīva (individual self) and the īśvara (cosmic self) are non-different; the apparent separation is māyā. The Bell test is the laboratory demonstration of sarvaṃ khalvidaṃ brahma — "All this, indeed, is Brahman."

The jñāni (knower) recognizes that the multiplicity was never real. The physicist who understands entanglement recognizes that the separation of particles was never real. Both are seeing through the same illusion — one in the laboratory, one in meditation. The methods differ. The recognition is the same.

"As the one fire, after it has entered the world, takes different forms according to whatever it burns, so does the one Self within all things take different forms according to whatever it enters, and yet exists outside."

— Katha Upanishad · 2.2.9

Kabbalah — The Sefirot Are Aspects of One Light

In Lurianic Kabbalah, the sefirot are not separate entities but aspects of one light. What happens to Malkhut (the Kingdom, the lowest sefirah) affects Keter (the Crown, the highest). The partzufim (divine faces) are entangled — the "coupling" of Abba and Imma, Zeir Anpin and Nukva, is a non-local correlation that transcends spatial separation. The divine is not a hierarchy but a network of mutual implication.

The Shevirat HaKelim (Breaking of the Vessels) is a cosmic entanglement. The holy sparks that fell into the kelipot (shells) are not lost; they remain connected to their source. The repair (Tikkun) of any vessel repairs the whole. The mitzvah (commandment) performed by a single Jew in Brooklyn affects the divine structure in its entirety. This is not metaphor. It is the Kabbalistic description of a non-local reality — a description that entanglement confirms in the laboratory.

The zivug (union) of the divine couple — the weekly Sabbath reunion of the Shekhinah with her bridegroom — is an entanglement event. The separation during the week is the "decoherence" of the divine state; the Sabbath is the restoration of coherence, the re-entanglement of what was separated. The Jew who keeps the Sabbath is not merely resting; she is repairing the non-local correlation of the divine.

"When the Tikkun is complete, the Messiah will come, not as a stranger but as the completion of our own work. The redemption is not a gift but a harvest — the fruit of all the sparks we have raised."

— Etz Chaim · Gate 50

Jesus' Teaching — "That They All May Be One"

In the Gospel of John, Jesus prays: "That they all may be one, as You, Father, are in Me, and I in You; that they also may be one in Us, that the world may believe that You sent Me" (John 17:21). This is not a prayer for unity in the sense of agreement or harmony. It is a prayer for ontological unity — the recognition that the apparent separation of selves is an illusion, that the "many" are in fact "one" in the same way that the Father and the Son are one.

The mystical body of Christ (corpus mysticum) is an entangled state. What happens to the least member affects the whole. "For as the body is one and has many members, but all the members of that one body, being many, are one body, so also is Christ" (1 Corinthians 12:12). This is not a social metaphor. It is a description of non-local identity — the recognition that the individual self is not a separate substance but a mode of the one divine life, just as the entangled particle is not a separate state but a mode of the one wavefunction.

The Eucharist is the ritual enactment of this non-locality. The bread and wine become the body and blood of Christ — not symbolically, but substantially (in the Catholic understanding). The communicant who receives the host is not receiving a representation but a participation in the non-local body. The same Christ is present in every host, in every church, in every age. This is not multiplication but entanglement — the one reality appearing in many places simultaneously, without being divided.

"He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him."

— John 6:56

Gnosticism — The Scattered Sparks of the Pleroma

In Gnostic cosmology, the Pleroma (Fullness) is the realm of the divine Aeons — perfect, undifferentiated, complete. The "fall" is the emergence of deficiency, the creation of the material world by the Demiurge (Yaldabaoth), who mistakenly believes himself to be the supreme God. The material world is the "collapse" of the Pleroma into multiplicity — the superposition reduced to a single, deficient outcome.

The scattered sparks of the Pleroma are entangled with their source. They have never truly been separated. The Gnostic's anamnesis (recollection) is the recognition that the separation was always an illusion — that the material world is a "false vacuum," a metastable state that appears stable but is not the true ground. The Gospel of Thomas: "The kingdom is inside you and outside you... split a piece of wood, and I am there. Lift up a stone, and you will find me there" (Logion 77). This is the holographic principle in Gnostic terms — the whole is present in every part, the Pleroma is never absent, the separation is merely the failure to see.

The Gnostic's task is to "awaken" the sparks — to restore the entanglement that the Demiurge's error has obscured. This is not a moral task but an ontological one. The Gnostic does not "become good"; she recognizes what she already is. The recognition is the restoration of the non-local correlation. The fall is the illusion of separation; the return is the recognition that the separation never occurred.

"When you make the two into one, and when you make the inner like the outer and the outer like the inner, and the upper like the lower, and when you make male and female into a single one, so that the male will not be male nor the female be female... then you will enter the kingdom."

— Gospel of Thomas · Logion 22

Einstein's Mistake — And What It Teaches Us

Einstein was wrong about quantum mechanics. But his mistake was not a failure of intelligence. It was a failure of imagination — an inability to conceive that the universe might be fundamentally different from the classical picture he had inherited. He assumed that locality and realism were non-negotiable features of any reasonable physical theory. Bell's theorem showed that they are not. The universe is not obliged to conform to our intuitions.

But Einstein's mistake is also a lesson in humility. The greatest physicist of the twentieth century — the man who overthrew Newtonian space and time, who predicted gravitational waves and black holes, who reshaped our understanding of light and matter — could not accept the full implications of the theory he had helped to create. He was, in the end, a prisoner of his own assumptions. The "spooky action at a distance" that he rejected is now the foundation of quantum information science, quantum computing, and quantum cryptography.

The contemplative traditions would recognize this pattern. The jñāni must abandon the assumption of a separate self. The Kabbalist must abandon the assumption of a separate God. The Gnostic must abandon the assumption of a separate world. The Christian mystic must abandon the assumption of a separate Christ. In each case, the abandonment is not a loss but a recognition — the recognition that the separation was never real. Einstein's inability to abandon local realism was, in this sense, a failure of mystical courage. He could not let go of the separate object.

And yet — his very resistance was productive. The EPR paper forced the physics community to confront the question. Bell's theorem was a response to EPR. The Aspect experiments were a response to Bell. The loophole-free tests were a response to the loopholes. The Micius satellite was a response to the need for larger scales. Einstein's "mistake" was the engine that drove a century of discovery. In the end, the universe was stranger than he could imagine. It is always stranger than we can imagine. That is the first principle of both physics and mysticism.

"The universe is not only queerer than we suppose, but queerer than we can suppose. Entanglement is the thread that binds the queerness together — the hidden connection that makes the universe one, not many."

— J.B.S. Haldane · adapted

The Road Ahead

Quantum Information and the New Physics

Computing · Cryptography · The Nature of Reality

Entanglement is no longer merely a subject for philosophical debate. It is a physical resource, a technological primitive, the foundation of a new science of quantum information. The implications are profound — not only for technology but for our understanding of what the universe is.

Quantum Computing — Harnessing Superposition

A classical computer processes bits — 0 or 1, on or off. A quantum computer processes qubits — quantum systems that can exist in superposition of 0 and 1 simultaneously. With n qubits, a quantum computer can explore 2n states at once. A few hundred qubits can represent more states than there are atoms in the observable universe. This is not merely faster computing; it is a different kind of computation, one that exploits the non-local correlations of entanglement to solve problems that are intractable for classical machines.

Shor's algorithm (1994) showed that a quantum computer could factor large numbers exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm — breaking the RSA encryption that secures the internet. Grover's algorithm (1996) showed that quantum computers could search unstructured databases quadratically faster than classical computers. These are not incremental improvements. They are qualitative changes in what computation means — changes that depend on the reality of superposition and entanglement.

Quantum Cryptography — Security from the Laws of Physics

Quantum key distribution (QKD) uses entanglement to create cryptographic keys that are provably secure — not because of computational difficulty, but because of the laws of physics themselves. Any attempt to eavesdrop on a quantum channel necessarily disturbs the quantum state, revealing the presence of the eavesdropper. The security is not based on the limits of current technology but on the structure of reality. This is the ultimate encryption: security guaranteed by the non-local correlations of entangled particles.

The Holographic Principle — Entanglement as Geometry

In the last two decades, a remarkable connection has emerged between quantum entanglement and the geometry of spacetime. The AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997) shows that a theory of gravity in a higher-dimensional space is mathematically equivalent to a quantum field theory on the boundary of that space, without gravity. The entanglement entropy of regions on the boundary corresponds to the area of surfaces in the bulk. Entanglement, it seems, is geometry — or at least, geometry emerges from entanglement.

The ER=EPR conjecture (Maldacena and Susskind, 2013) goes further: Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes) in the bulk are equivalent to Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen pairs (entangled particles) on the boundary. Two entangled black holes are connected by a wormhole. The non-local correlation of entanglement is the same phenomenon as the non-traversable wormhole of general relativity. The "spooky action at a distance" that Einstein rejected is, in the deepest theory we have, the same thing as the "bridge through spacetime" that he invented.

This is not yet a complete theory. The connection between quantum information and gravity is one of the most active frontiers in physics. But the direction is clear: entanglement is not merely a feature of quantum mechanics. It is the fabric from which spacetime itself is woven. The hidden connection is not hidden because it is small or subtle. It is hidden because it is the ground of all appearance — the non-local unity from which the local, the separate, the "this" and "that" emerge as approximations.

Juan Maldacena · 1997

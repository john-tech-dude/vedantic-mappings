The Enfolded and the Unfolded

Implicate Order · Explicate Order · The Holomovement

David Bohm was one of the most brilliant and unorthodox physicists of the 20th century. His interpretation of quantum mechanics — the pilot-wave theory — was dismissed by the mainstream for decades. But his later work, developed in dialogue with the philosopher Jiddu Krishnamurti, went far beyond physics. Bohm proposed that the universe is not a collection of separate objects interacting through forces. It is a single, indivisible holomovement — a dynamic process of enfolding and unfolding.

The implicate order is the enfolded, hidden order of reality — the ground from which everything emerges. The explicate order is the unfolded, manifest world — the realm of separate objects, space, and time that we experience. The explicate is a projection of the implicate, just as a holographic image is a projection of the interference pattern on the photographic plate.

Bohm's key insight was that separation is an illusion. The electron appears to be a particle because we have "cut" it out of the implicate order. The act of measurement is not a collapse but a projection — the unfolding of a particular aspect of the implicate into the explicate. The implicate order remains whole, undivided, and non-local.

This is not mysticism. It is a rigorous physical theory. Bohm's mathematical formalism — the quantum potential — describes a non-local, deterministic reality. The quantum potential acts instantaneously across space, guiding particles along trajectories that are correlated beyond any classical explanation. The implicate order is the mathematical structure that generates the quantum potential.

Bohm wrote: "The notion of a separate organism is clearly an abstraction. It is not a fundamental reality. The fundamental reality is the whole, the undivided wholeness of the universe." He was not merely a physicist but a contemplative — a man who sat in silence with Krishnamurti, who listened to the universe as much as he calculated it.

Wheeler's Participatory Universe — It from Bit

John Archibald Wheeler, a student of Bohr and mentor to Feynman, was the other great visionary of 20th-century physics. His "It from Bit" slogan is the most concise expression of the information-theoretic view of reality: "Every it — every particle, every field of force, even the space-time continuum itself — derives its function, its meaning, its very existence from bits." The universe is fundamentally information, not matter.

Wheeler's participatory universe is the logical extension: the observer is not passive but active. The act of observation creates reality. The universe is not a pre-existing object that we discover; it is a process in which we participate. The "bit" is the elementary unit of information — the answer to a yes/no question. The universe is the sum of all such answers, generated by the act of looking.

Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment is the experimental demonstration of this principle. A photon passes through a double-slit apparatus. The experimenter chooses, after the photon has passed through the slits, whether to observe the interference pattern or the particle trajectory. The photon "knows" what the experimenter will choose — the choice determines the photon's behavior retroactively. The observer's decision at the present moment determines the history of the photon. The past is not fixed; it is shaped by the present act of looking.

Wheeler asked: "Why existence?" His answer was: "Existence is a self-excited circuit. The universe observes itself into being." The act of looking is the act of creation. The observer is not an afterthought; the observer is the universe's way of knowing itself.

The Holographic Principle — Entanglement as Geometry

In the last two decades, a remarkable connection has emerged between quantum entanglement and the geometry of spacetime. The AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997) shows that a theory of gravity in a higher-dimensional space is mathematically equivalent to a quantum field theory on the boundary of that space. The entanglement entropy of regions on the boundary corresponds to the area of surfaces in the bulk. Entanglement, it seems, is geometry — or at least, geometry emerges from entanglement.

The ER=EPR conjecture (Maldacena and Susskind, 2013) goes further: Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes) in the bulk are equivalent to Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen pairs (entangled particles) on the boundary. Two entangled black holes are connected by a wormhole. The non-local correlation of entanglement is the same phenomenon as the non-traversable wormhole of general relativity. The "spooky action at a distance" that Einstein rejected is, in the deepest theory we have, the same thing as the "bridge through spacetime" that he invented.

This is not yet a complete theory. The connection between quantum information and gravity is one of the most active frontiers in physics. But the direction is clear: entanglement is not merely a feature of quantum mechanics. It is the fabric from which spacetime itself is woven. The implicate order is not a metaphor; it is the mathematics of holographic duality.

The Mystical Parallels — The Holographic Ground

The implicate order, the holographic principle, and the participatory universe find profound echoes in the contemplative traditions. Each tradition has a name for the undivided wholeness from which all appearances emerge.

Advaita Vedanta — Brahman and Māyā

In Advaita, Brahman is the implicate order — the one without a second, the ground from which all appearances emerge. Māyā is the explicate order — the unfolding of multiplicity from the non-dual ground. The world appears separate and manifold, but it is a projection of the one reality. The holographic principle — that every part contains the whole — is the advaita insight: ātman (individual self) is non-different from Brahman (universal self). The part is the whole, seen from a limited perspective.

The jñāni recognizes that the explicate is not separate from the implicate. The world is not an illusion; it is the projection of the implicate. The physicist who studies the holographic principle is not studying a metaphor; she is studying the structure of māyā. The hologram is the laboratory demonstration of sarvaṃ khalvidaṃ brahma — "All this, indeed, is Brahman."

"As the one sun is reflected in many waters, so is the one Self reflected in many bodies. The water is the explicate; the sun is the implicate. The reflection is the universe."

— Śvetāśvatara Upanishad · 2.1

Kabbalah — Adam Kadmon and the Sefirot

In Lurianic Kabbalah, Adam Kadmon is the implicate order — the divine blueprint that contains all worlds and all sefirot in an enfolded state. The sefirot are the explicate order — the unfolding of the divine into specific attributes and worlds. The Tzimtzum is the primordial unfolding — the contraction that creates the space for the explicate.

The Tikkun is the re-enfoldment — the restoration of the implicate order that was shattered in the Shevirat HaKelim. Every act of kavanah (intention) is a micro-unfolding — a projection of the implicate into the explicate. The Kabbalist who prays with kavanah participates in the holographic process. The part contains the whole; the whole is present in every part.

The Shekhinah is the implicate present in the explicate — the divine light that is always present but often hidden. The act of Tikkun is the recognition that the Shekhinah is not separate from the world but is the world's deepest nature. The holographic principle is the Kabbalistic insight: the world is a projection of the divine, and the divine is present in every projection.

"From the light of the Ein Sof, all worlds were formed. The worlds are projections of the light, and the light is present in every world. The whole is in the part, and the part is in the whole."

— Zohar · I, 15a

Jesus' Teaching — "The Kingdom Is Inside You"

In the Gospel of Luke, Jesus says: "The kingdom of God is inside you and among you" (Luke 17:21). The kingdom is the implicate order — the divine reality that is always present but often hidden. The explicate order — the world of separate objects, of "this" and "that" — is the appearance. The kingdom is the recognition that the appearance is not separate from the reality.

The mystical body of Christ is the holographic body — the whole present in every part. "For as the body is one and has many members, but all the members of that one body, being many, are one body, so also is Christ" (1 Corinthians 12:12). The body is the explicate; the Christ is the implicate. The part is not separate from the whole; the whole is not separate from the part.

The Eucharist is the ritual enactment of the holographic principle. The bread and wine become the body and blood of Christ — the implicate present in the explicate. The communicant who receives the host participates in the whole. The part contains the whole; the whole is present in every part. "He who eats My flesh and drinks My blood abides in Me, and I in him" (John 6:56). The implicate and explicate are not two; they are one.

"I am the vine, you are the branches. He who abides in Me, and I in him, bears much fruit. For apart from Me you can do nothing."

— John 15:5

Gnosticism — The Pleroma and the Kenoma

In Gnostic cosmology, the Pleroma is the implicate order — the fullness of the divine, the realm of the Aeons, the perfect unity that has never been divided. The Kenoma (the void) is the explicate order — the material world, the projection of the Pleroma into deficiency and separation.

The Demiurge (Yaldabaoth) is the false projector — the one who mistakes the projection for the original. The Gnostic's anamnesis (recollection) is the recognition that the Kenoma is a projection of the Pleroma — that the material world is not separate from the divine. The holographic principle is the Gnostic insight: the part contains the whole; the whole is present in every part.

The Gospel of Thomas: "The kingdom is inside you and outside you. When you come to know yourselves, you will be known" (Logion 3). The kingdom is the implicate order; the self is the explicate. The recognition is the re-enfoldment — the restoration of the implicate order that was never really lost. The Gnostic does not "escape" the world; she recognizes that the world was never separate from the Pleroma. The implicate and explicate are one.

"Split a piece of wood, and I am there. Lift up a stone, and you will find me there. The whole is in the part, and the part is in the whole. The kingdom is the hologram of the Pleroma."

— Gospel of Thomas · Logion 77 (adapted)

The Open Question — Why Existence?

Wheeler asked: "Why existence?" Bohm asked: "What is the nature of wholeness?" The question is the same. The implicate order is not a final answer; it is a deeper question. It is the recognition that reality is not a collection of separate objects but a single, undivided process. The physicist who studies the holographic principle is not solving a puzzle; she is entering a mystery.

The contemplative traditions have always known this. The jñāni does not "solve" the mystery; she abides in it. The Kabbalist does not "answer" the question; he holds it in his heart. The Gnostic does not "escape" the world; she recognizes that the world was never separate. The Christian mystic does not "achieve" union; she recognizes that union was never absent.

The implicate order is the recognition that the universe is not a collection of parts but a single, undivided whole. The explicate order is the appearance of parts. The two are not separate; they are the same reality, seen from different perspectives. The act of looking is the act of creation. The observer is the observed. The implicate and explicate are one.

Feynman said: "I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics." But perhaps the not-understanding is the point. The mystery is not a problem to be solved; it is a reality to be inhabited. The implicate order is not a theory; it is a way of seeing — a way of recognizing that the universe is not a machine but a dance, not a collection of parts but a single, undivided whole.

John Archibald Wheeler · 1990

"Why does existence exist? We are not just observers; we are participators. In some strange sense, this is a participatory universe. The observer is not merely a passive recipient of reality; the observer is a co-creator of reality."

Vedanta · Maps · The Unnameable

Six cartographies · One mountain

What is Vedanta? Vedanta (Sanskrit veda – knowledge, anta – end) is the philosophical culmination of the Upanishads. It teaches that the individual self (Ātman) is ultimately identical with the absolute reality (Brahman). This is non-duality (Advaita): not one-ness as a number, but the recognition that separation was never real.

Why Maps? Every tradition, every realized master, every scripture is a map—not the territory. The territory is the Self, the Absolute, the Tao. It cannot be drawn. But a good map, drawn from authentic experience, can orient the seeker and reveal the pitfalls of the journey.

The six cartographies here—Advaita Vedanta, Jesus of Nazareth, Kabbalah, Gnosticism, Taoism · The Unnamed Way, and Quantum Mechanics—are not competitors. They are different paths up the same mountain, each with its own language, its own silences, its own precision.

“The Self is the only Guru. All outer gurus merely point back to that inner Guru.” — Ramana Maharshi

Nisargadatta Maharaj reminds us: “Give up all questions except one: ‘Who am I?’ After all, the only fact you are sure of is that you are. The ‘I am’ is certain. The ‘I am this’ is not.”

And from the quantum realm, physicist John Wheeler—who coined the term “black hole” and developed the “participatory universe” hypothesis—observed: “No phenomenon is a real phenomenon until it is an observed phenomenon.” Schrödinger, steeped in Vedantic texts, wrote that the apparent multiplicity of selves is “only an illusion.” David Bohm, after dialogues with Jiddu Krishnamurti, proposed the “implicate order”—a holographic universe where every part contains the whole, echoing the ancient wisdom of the vākyavṛtti.

I. The Non-Dual Structure

The Advaitic structure: Brahman (absolute) and Ātman (individual self) are identical. Māyā is the apparent veil that creates the illusion of separation. The other five cartographies describe this same structure in their own languages.

This structure is not unique to Advaita. Kabbalah calls the Absolute Ein Sof and the veil Tzimtzum. Gnosticism speaks of the Unknown Father and the Demiurge. Taoism names it the Tao and the ten thousand things. Quantum mechanics describes it as the wavefunction and measurement. The names change; the structure does not.

II. Six Paths to the Absolute

Six traditions converging on the same Absolute. Each path uses distinct language and methodology, yet all point to the same non-dual recognition. The detailed maps for each tradition are found in their respective cartographies below.
